Welcome to Blockchain Hunter

The complete field manual for cryptocurrency crime investigation. Grounded in the FACT Attribution Framework with Canadian legal context and advanced technical tradecraft.

🔑 Core Framework: FACT Attribution
This guide is built around the FACT Attribution Framework (Shavers, 2025) — a jurisprudence-centered methodology that explicitly separates identification ("what acted?") from attribution ("who acted?"). Every investigation in this guide applies FACT's four stages: Forensic Authority → Analyze Evidence → Correlate & Sequence → Testify & Transfer.
🎯 What is a Blockchain Investigator?

A blockchain investigator traces the movement of illicit funds through cryptocurrency networks, combining on-chain forensics with OSINT to unmask criminals who exploit crypto's pseudonymous nature. The field sits at the intersection of financial crime investigation, cybersecurity, digital forensics, and legal process.

The best investigators — like the anonymous ZachXBT — have helped recover over $350M for victims and been first to identify state-sponsored hackers. But real-world attribution requires more than tracing a wallet. It requires applying structured frameworks like FACT to ensure your conclusions are legally defensible.

🔑 Core Investigator Principles
  • Device ≠ Person: The FACT Framework's central principle. A wallet address is like a device — identifying it is not the same as attributing it to a person. Multi-stream convergence is required.
  • Follow the money: The blockchain never lies. Every transaction is permanently recorded. Your job is to read the ledger.
  • On-chain + off-chain: Combine blockchain data with OSINT, social media, breach data, and traditional intelligence.
  • Speed matters: Funds move fast. The faster you trace, the more likely exchanges can freeze assets before cash-out.
  • Documentation is evidence: Archive everything with timestamps. Your notes could become court exhibits.
  • Falsify, don't just confirm: Actively test whether your attribution could be wrong. Enumerate alternative actors.

Blockchain Foundations

Understanding how blockchains work and why they're both investigable and misunderstood by criminals.

⛓️ What is a Blockchain?

A blockchain is a publicly available, immutable ledger of every transaction ever made on that network. When Person A sends Bitcoin to Person B, that transaction is cryptographically verified by thousands of computers and permanently written into a block — chained to every previous block.

This transparency is what makes investigation possible. Unlike traditional financial crime where investigators need warrants to access bank records, on-chain data is publicly visible to everyone — including investigators.

Tokens, Smart Contracts & DeFi — Explained

Tokens (ERC-20 / TRC-20 / SPL)

A token is a virtual currency asset that runs on another blockchain's infrastructure. USDT (Tether) is an ERC-20 token on Ethereum and a TRC-20 token on Tron. Tokens do not have their own blockchain — they piggyback on the host chain. Investigating USDT flows requires reading Ethereum or Tron transactions, not a separate ledger.

Key token types: Stablecoins (USDT, USDC — pegged to USD), Governance tokens (voting rights in DeFi protocols), Memecoins (high-risk, often pump-and-dump vehicles).

Smart Contracts

A smart contract is self-executing code deployed on a blockchain that automatically enforces the terms of an agreement when conditions are met — no intermediary needed. DeFi protocols (Uniswap, Aave, Compound) are built entirely on smart contracts.

For investigators: smart contracts are deterministic and publicly readable. Their code can be analyzed for vulnerabilities. The deployer wallet is often investigable through its funding source.

DeFi (Decentralized Finance)

DeFi refers to financial services (lending, trading, borrowing) built on blockchain smart contracts with no central operator. Since there's no central authority to subpoena, DeFi is increasingly used for money laundering — but all activity is on-chain and traceable.

Key DeFi concepts for investigators: Liquidity Pools (AMM), DEXs (Uniswap, SushiSwap), Yield Farming, Flash Loans, and Bridges.

Ransomware Payment Flows

Ransomware is malware that encrypts a victim's data and demands cryptocurrency payment for the decryption key. Ransomware payments create an on-chain record that investigators can trace.

Common ransomware payment chains: Bitcoin (most common) → Mixer/Tumbler → Instant exchange → Cash-out venue. Some groups now demand Monero. Payment addresses are unique per victim, enabling investigators to build a victim map.

Key Terminology

Master the language of blockchain investigation. Sourced from TRM Labs, FACT Framework, FATF guidelines, and Canadian legal context.

🔍
Attribution

The process of assigning blockchain addresses to specific real-world entities. Under the FACT Framework, attribution requires multi-stream evidence convergence — not just address identification. Example: proving that wallet bc1q… is controlled by John Smith, not just that it deposited to Binance.

Blockchain Tracing

Tracking the source and destination of cryptocurrency transactions. The goal is to identify the actual controller of an otherwise pseudonymous address by following money to or from entities that hold real-world identity data (VASPs).

Smart Contract

Self-executing code deployed on a blockchain (primarily Ethereum) that automatically enforces agreement terms. Powers all DeFi protocols. Smart contracts are publicly readable — their logic, deployer wallet, and upgrade history are fully investigable on Etherscan.

DeFi (Decentralized Finance)

Financial services built on blockchain smart contracts with no central operator. Includes DEXs, lending protocols (Aave, Compound), yield farming, and liquidity pools. No central operator to subpoena — but all activity is on-chain and traceable. DeFi is increasingly used for money laundering and fund layering.

Token (ERC-20 / TRC-20)

A virtual currency that runs on another blockchain's infrastructure. Examples: USDT (Tether), USDC (Circle), LINK (Chainlink). Tokens don't have their own chain — investigating them requires reading the host chain (Ethereum, Tron, etc.).

Ransomware

Malware that encrypts victim data and demands cryptocurrency (usually BTC, sometimes XMR) for the decryption key. Each victim receives a unique payment address — creating an investigable payment graph. Investigators trace from payment address through laundering chain to cash-out venue.

UTXO (Unspent Transaction Output)

Bitcoin's accounting model. Rather than account balances, Bitcoin tracks unspent outputs. Every BTC transaction consumes previous UTXOs as inputs and creates new UTXOs as outputs. This model is critical for address clustering — the Common Input Ownership heuristic (CIUA) is based on UTXO mechanics.

Account Model (Ethereum)

Ethereum uses account-based tracking (like a bank account) rather than UTXOs. Each address has a balance that is directly debited/credited. Simpler for tracing — but investigators must also track "internal transactions" (smart contract calls) that don't appear as standard transfers on basic block explorers.

Chain-Hopping

Moving assets between different blockchains (e.g., BTC → ETH → TRON) through bridges to obscure asset control. Forces investigators to track across multiple chains and tool sets. Lazarus Group's primary laundering technique post-Bybit hack.

VASP (Virtual Asset Service Provider)

Any platform for buying, selling, or trading crypto. VASPs maintain KYC records that can identify real-world controllers. Examples: Binance, Coinbase, Kraken. In Canada, VASPs are regulated as Money Services Businesses (MSBs) under FINTRAC.

KYC (Know Your Customer)

Real-world identification collected by exchanges. Typically includes: name, DOB, address, government ID, selfie, email. When obtained via legal process, KYC data links a wallet address to a real person — often the breakthrough that cracks a case.

Mixers & Tumblers

Services that pool and redistribute crypto to break the audit trail. Examples: Tornado Cash (ETH — OFAC sanctioned 2022), Wasabi Wallet (BTC). Using mixers does not make tracing impossible — investigators can analyze anonymity sets and look for timing correlations. The on/off ramps remain the key chokepoints.

Flash Loan

An uncollateralized instant loan in DeFi that must be borrowed and repaid within a single transaction. Attackers use flash loans to temporarily manipulate prices and drain protocols. The seed funds for flash loan attacks often trace back to a central exchange deposit weeks prior — a key investigative lead.

AMM & Liquidity Pools

Automated Market Makers (AMMs) like Uniswap use liquidity pools rather than order books. Funds deposited into liquidity pools are commingled — the linear trail is broken by AMM logic, which is a challenge for chain reconciliation. Investigators must track LP token issuance and redemption events.

Internal Transactions (Ethereum)

Smart contract-to-smart contract calls that move ETH or tokens but do NOT appear as standard transfers on basic block explorers like Etherscan's default view. A significant portion of DeFi movement happens via internal transactions. Investigators must use "Trace" logs or tools like Tenderly or Phalcon to see them.

Taint Analysis / Risk Scoring

Calculating how many degrees of separation a wallet has from a sanctioned entity or illicit source. Direct exposure = 1 hop. Indirect = 2+ hops. Commercial tools (TRM Labs, Chainalysis) express taint as a percentage. A 15%+ exposure to ransomware-linked wallets is a significant red flag (Khinkis case).

Peeling Chain

An obfuscation technique where funds move through many sequential addresses, each time peeling off a small amount, to confuse investigators. In a 1,000+ hop peeling chain, investigators must distinguish between the "payment output" (going to destination) and the "change output" (going back to the sender) at each step.

First Funder Analysis

A key tracing technique: identify the "genesis" of a wallet by looking at the very first transaction that funded an address. This parent wallet often traces back to a CEX deposit (with KYC) or another identifiable entity — linking the suspect to a real-world identity at the earliest point.

The FACT Attribution Framework

The jurisprudence-centered methodology at the heart of this guide. FACT separates identification from attribution — a critical distinction in crypto investigations.

⚖️ The Attribution Gap — Why FACT Matters

Digital forensics has become highly effective at answering what happened, on which device, how and when. What has been far less developed is a structured, legally grounded way to answer: "Which specific person is most likely responsible for those actions, and how confident are we?"

In blockchain investigations, this gap is acute. An investigator can identify that Wallet X deposited to Binance — but that is technical identification, not person-level attribution. The named KYC account holder could be a straw identity, a stolen identity, or a professional money launderer. FACT provides the structure to go from wallet to person defensibly.

The Critical Distinction: Identification vs Attribution

Technical Identification

Answers "What acted?" — which artifact, address, device, account, or system is involved. Example: "Wallet bc1q… deposited 154 BTC to Binance account #12345."

This is within the core expertise of blockchain forensic analysts. It does NOT establish who, as a person, acted.

Person-Level Attribution

Answers "Who acted?" — assigning responsibility to a specific real-world person through converging evidence. Example: "John Smith (DOB 1988-03-14, Abbotsford BC) controlled wallet bc1q… based on KYC records, IP logs, and social media OSINT."

Requires multi-stream evidence convergence. Under FACT, this requires explicit documentation of alternative actors and how they were ruled out.

The FACT Lifecycle: F → A → C → T

F
F — Forensic Authority & Compliance
Establish legal ground before analysis
  • Document legal or organizational authority (warrant, subpoena, PIPEDA consent, employer policy)
  • Define scope, jurisdiction, proportionality, and retention
  • Establish chain-of-custody requirements
  • Prevent premature identity assumptions
A
A — Analyze Evidence
Structured technical analysis
  • Identify specific digital actions (transaction, transfer, deposit)
  • Acquire and examine on-chain and off-chain evidence
  • Evaluate artifact reliability (timestamp accuracy, potential manipulation)
  • Generate initial candidate actors and alternative explanations
C
C — Correlate & Sequence
Multi-stream convergence across identity layers
  • Correlate across identity layers: artifact → device → account → session → person
  • Integrate digital, physical, contextual, and testimonial evidence
  • Test each alternative hypothesis (not just the primary)
  • Determine whether convergence threshold for person-level attribution is met
T
T — Testify & Transfer Findings
Defensible reporting and handoff
  • Finalize conclusions with explicit uncertainty (weak/moderate/strong/very strong support)
  • Prepare reports explaining the reasoning pathway and how alternatives were handled
  • Support adjudicators in understanding both what is known and what must remain unresolved

FACT Lite — Triage Profile

⚡ FACT Lite: For Early Uncertainty

FACT Lite is designed for time-boxed triage where attribution might matter later. It includes F + A only (authority/scope + initial analysis) — excluding C + T unless escalation is warranted.

FACT Lite may NOT be used to justify person-level attribution or adverse action. Escalate to full FACT before naming, disciplining, accusing, or reporting externally.

Output of FACT Lite: candidate hypotheses, evidence preservation plan, and escalation decision — not attribution.

Blockchain Tracing in Practice

Step-by-step methodology for tracing illicit funds — from acquiring an address to generating defensible attribution under the FACT Framework.

🗺️ The Three-Step Framework

Per TRM Labs' methodology: (1) Identify illicit conduct — acquire the address/transaction from SAR, complaint, or OSINT. (2) Trace proceeds — follow the fund flow, map the transaction network, identify leverage points. (3) Seek disruption — serve legal process on VASPs (FACT stage T), seek seizure or forfeiture.

Detailed Tracing Steps

A
Input Transaction into Blockchain Tool
Copy the TXID or address into TRM Labs, Arkham, or a block explorer. Best practice: acquire addresses electronically to avoid transcription errors. Many tools auto-populate from partial addresses.
B
Map the Network & Identify Leverage Points
Ask: How many transactions? When was last activity? What's the current balance? Is it a pass-through address? Is it part of a cluster? Does it have exposure to a VASP with KYC records?
C
Perform First Funder Analysis
Look at the very first transaction that funded the suspect wallet. This "genesis" source often traces back to a CEX deposit (with KYC) or another identified entity — linking the suspect to real-world identity at the earliest point before they began covering their tracks.
D
Identify VASPs for Legal Process (FACT Stage F/T)
After plotting an address and its counterparties, identify which VASPs received funds. Screenshot the graph as evidence for prosecutors. Use TRM's "Know Your VASP" tool to find jurisdiction, legal contact, and KYC availability.
E
Correlate with OSINT (FACT Stage C)
Apply FACT's Correlate stage: integrate the on-chain findings with off-chain OSINT streams. Do IP addresses from exchange subpoenas match locations visible in OSINT? Does transaction timing match social media activity? Two independent streams required for attribution.

Red Flags: Pass-Through Addresses

🚩 Key Indicators
  • Only two transactions: one in, one out (entire sum immediately sent)
  • Large sums moved in rapid succession (e.g., 39 minutes apart)
  • $0 current balance after significant value passed through
  • Part of a large cluster of addresses
  • No economic rationale — the controller paid fees for an unnecessary step

MODULE 03b // INTERMEDIATE
UTXO vs Account-Based Tracing

The two dominant blockchain accounting models require fundamentally different tracing approaches. Mastering both is essential.

Bitcoin: UTXO Model

Bitcoin doesn't track balances — it tracks Unspent Transaction Outputs (UTXOs). Each transaction consumes previous UTXOs as inputs and creates new UTXOs as outputs. Think of UTXOs like individual bills: you receive a $20 bill and a $50 bill, not a "$70 balance."

Investigator implications:

  • Change output detection: Bitcoin transactions often produce two outputs — one to the recipient, one "change" back to the sender. Identifying change addresses reveals more of the suspect's wallet cluster.
  • Peeling chain mechanics: In a 1,000-hop peeling chain, each transaction has a "payment output" (moving toward destination) and a "change output" (back to sender). Investigators track which output continues the payment chain using amount analysis and timing.
  • Common Input Ownership (CIUA): If two addresses both sign inputs in the same transaction, the same entity holds both private keys — clustering them together.
Ethereum: Account Model

Ethereum tracks balances directly, like a bank account. Each address has a balance that is debited and credited. Simpler in concept — but Ethereum adds critical complexity for investigators: internal transactions.

Internal Transactions: When a smart contract calls another contract and moves funds, this creates an "internal transaction" — which does NOT appear as a standard transfer in basic Etherscan views. A significant portion of DeFi activity is invisible at the surface level.

How to trace internal transactions:

  • Etherscan → "Internal Txns" tab (shows ETH internal transfers)
  • Tenderly — full transaction simulation and trace visualization
  • Phalcon (Blocksec) — DeFi attack trace analysis
  • Alchemy/Infura API → trace_transaction endpoint
🔬 UTXO Peeling Chain — Step-by-Step

Here's how to mathematically track through a peeling chain on Bitcoin:

Transaction 1:
  Input:  1.0 BTC (from victim wallet)
  Output A: 0.95 BTC → bc1qSUSPECT1 (payment hop)
  Output B: 0.05 BTC → bc1qCHANGE1 (back to sender)

Transaction 2:
  Input:  0.95 BTC (from bc1qSUSPECT1)
  Output A: 0.90 BTC → bc1qSUSPECT2 (continues chain)
  Output B: 0.05 BTC → bc1qCHANGE2 (back to sender)

After 20 hops:
  Final deposit: ~0.50 BTC → Binance deposit address
  
INVESTIGATIVE INSIGHT: bc1qCHANGE1, bc1qCHANGE2, etc. 
all belong to the SAME entity. Each change address 
expands the address cluster you can subpoena.

Tools like TRM Labs and Chainalysis Reactor automate this analysis — they calculate which output is "payment" vs "change" using amount heuristics, reuse patterns, and round-number detection.

Bridge & Cross-Chain Reconciliation

How to follow funds when criminals hop between blockchains — the most technically demanding aspect of modern crypto investigation.

🌉 The Chain-Hopping Challenge

When a criminal sends BTC to a bridge in exchange for ETH on another chain, the linear transaction trail is broken. Investigators must manually reconcile the two sides: the Lock/Burn event on the source chain, and the corresponding Mint/Unlock event on the destination chain.

Without proper bridge reconciliation, a sophisticated criminal can effectively lose an investigator at each bridge crossing. Lazarus Group exploited this after the Bybit hack, fragmenting $1.5B across dozens of bridges.

Event Matching: Bridge Reconciliation

🔗 Lock/Burn ↔ Mint/Unlock Correlation

Every bridge protocol emits contract events that investigators can match:

Bridge EventSource ChainDestination ChainHow to Correlate
Lock/BurnTokens locked or burned in bridge contract—Note timestamp + unique sequence ID emitted in the contract event log
Mint/Unlock—Tokens minted or released to recipient addressMatch sequence ID + timestamp (±2 block finality times) to find the corresponding destination transaction

Practical example (Lazarus post-Bybit):

1. Source: Ethereum
   Lock Event: ETH bridge contract receives 2,000 ETH
   Emits: DepositId=0x4af1b2, timestamp=1740172801
   
2. Destination: Avalanche
   Search Avalanche for: DepositId=0x4af1b2
   Find: WETH minted to 0xLAZARUS_NEW_WALLET
   Timestamp: 1740172843 (42s later — within expected range)
   
3. New investigation target: 0xLAZARUS_NEW_WALLET on Avalanche

Tools like Pulsy and Socketscan automate this correlation. For bridges not indexed by these tools, investigators must manually query the bridge contract's event logs using Etherscan's "Logs" tab or the Alchemy API.

Liquidity Pool Attribution Challenge

🌊 When the AMM Breaks the Trail

When funds pass through a DEX liquidity pool (Uniswap, Curve, etc.), the trail can be broken by the AMM's mechanics. Funds commingled in a pool lose direct linearity — you can no longer say "these exact tokens came from Wallet X."

Investigative workarounds:

  • LP token tracking: When adding liquidity, a user receives LP tokens representing their share. Track the LP token issuance to the depositor and then the LP token redemption to find the recipient.
  • Proportional analysis: If a suspect deposits 100 ETH into a pool of 1,000 ETH (10% share), then a 200 ETH withdrawal shortly after that 10% share was active is highly probative.
  • Temporal proximity: Deposits and withdrawals within a short time window are likely the same actor — especially if the amounts and token pairs match.
  • Cross-pool clustering: The same wallet address depositing into and withdrawing from multiple pools creates a cluster even without direct chain tracing.

Smart Contract Forensics

Investigating DeFi exploits, flash loan attacks, and distinguishing hackers from MEV bots.

⚡ Flash Loan Attribution

Flash loans allow attackers to borrow enormous amounts of capital without collateral for a single transaction. The attack is instantaneous and complex — but the attacker's identity is often visible long before the attack.

Tracing flash loan attackers backward:

  • Seed funds analysis: Flash loans require no collateral but DO require a small amount of ETH for gas fees. Trace the gas-funding wallet backward — this seed funding often leads to a CEX deposit made weeks prior.
  • Deployer wallet: The smart contract used to execute the flash loan attack was deployed by a wallet. That deployer wallet's funding source is investigable.
  • Profit destination: Where did the flash loan profits go? Trace forward from the attacker's profit address through laundering attempts.

MEV Bot vs Hacker — Critical Distinction

🤖 Don't Investigate the Wrong Entity

A common pitfall in DeFi exploit investigation: confusing a Maximal Extractable Value (MEV) bot with the actual hacker. MEV bots monitor the mempool for profitable transactions and may "front-run" an attacker — executing a similar trade milliseconds before the hack, profiting from the same vulnerability.

Malware Analysis for Crypto Investigators

Extracting wallet addresses, C2 infrastructure, and encryption routines from malicious payloads — the technical bridge between ransomware and blockchain tracing.

⚠️ Safety First
Always perform malware analysis in an isolated, air-gapped virtual machine. Never execute unknown binaries on a connected workstation. Use dedicated malware analysis environments (REMnux, FlareVM).
🔬 Extracting Hardcoded Wallet Addresses

Ransomware and crypto-stealing malware often contain hardcoded wallet addresses for receiving payments or C2 fees. These addresses are your primary investigative lead linking the malware to on-chain activity.

Extraction techniques:

  • Static string extraction: Use strings (Linux) or FLOSS (FireEye Labs Obfuscated String Solver) to extract all strings from a binary — including hardcoded addresses. Bitcoin addresses match regex ^[13][a-km-zA-HJ-NP-Z1-9]{25,34}$ or ^bc1[a-z0-9]{39,59}$.
  • YARA rules: Write YARA rules targeting known wallet address patterns or cryptocurrency-related strings to scan malware samples.
  • Dynamic analysis: Run the malware in a controlled environment (Cuckoo Sandbox) and capture all network traffic and file writes — payment addresses often appear in ransom notes or config files written to disk.
  • Unpacking: Packed/obfuscated malware must be unpacked first. Tools: UPX, PE-bear, x64dbg for manual unpacking.
# Extract potential crypto addresses from binary
strings ransomware.exe | grep -E "^[13bc][a-zA-Z0-9]{25,62}$"

# YARA rule for Bitcoin address detection
rule bitcoin_address {
  strings:
    $btc = /\b[13][a-km-zA-HJ-NP-Z1-9]{25,34}\b/
    $bech32 = /\bbc1[a-z0-9]{39,59}\b/
  condition:
    any of them
}
🕸️ Extracting C2 Infrastructure

Command-and-control (C2) infrastructure connects the malware to the attacker's servers. Identifying C2 domains and IPs creates additional investigative pivots beyond the blockchain trail.

C2 extraction methods:

  • Network capture (Wireshark/Zeek): Run malware in sandbox and capture all DNS queries, HTTP/HTTPS connections. Beacon intervals and User-Agent strings are often distinctive.
  • Config decryption: Many RATs/ransomware variants store C2 addresses in encrypted config sections. Identifying the decryption routine (often XOR or AES) allows config extraction. Tools: malware-config extractor scripts on GitHub by kevoreilly.
  • WHOIS/passive DNS: Once C2 domains are identified, query PassiveDNS (Farsight DNSDB, VirusTotal) to find historical IP associations and related domains registered by the same actor.
  • VirusTotal Intelligence: Upload sample to VT to find related malware samples sharing the same C2 infrastructure — expanding the attribution network.
🔑 Encryption Routine Analysis

Understanding the encryption routine in ransomware can help victims (and investigators) recover files, verify payment claims, and attribute the malware to a specific criminal group.

Key techniques:

  • Identify crypto libraries: Look for imports of CryptoAPI (Windows), OpenSSL, or common crypto library signatures in the binary.
  • Key generation analysis: Weak key generation (using system time as entropy) can be exploitable. Document the PRNG algorithm.
  • Key storage: Does the key get sent to C2 before encryption? Is it stored locally? Understanding this helps assess recovery options.
  • Attribution via implementation quirks: Unique coding patterns in encryption implementations can match a sample to a known threat actor. Lazarus Group, for example, has documented code reuse patterns across its malware families.

Canadian Legal Framework for Crypto Investigations

FINTRAC reporting, PIPEDA considerations, PCMLTFA obligations, and RCMP asset seizure — the Canadian legal landscape every investigator operating in Canada must understand.

📌 Disclaimer
This section provides educational context for Canadian legal frameworks applicable to crypto investigations. It is not legal advice. Consult qualified legal counsel for specific cases.
🏦 FINTRAC — Canada's Financial Intelligence Unit

The Financial Transactions and Reports Analysis Centre of Canada (FINTRAC) is Canada's financial intelligence unit. Since 2014, cryptocurrency exchanges operating in Canada have been required to register as Money Services Businesses (MSBs) and comply with FINTRAC's reporting requirements.

Key FINTRAC obligations for Canadian VASPs:

  • Large Cash Transaction Reports (LCTR): Report any cash transaction of $10,000 CAD or more within 15 days.
  • Suspicious Transaction Reports (STR): File when there are reasonable grounds to suspect a transaction is related to money laundering or terrorist financing. No dollar threshold. STRs are a primary source of investigative leads for law enforcement.
  • Electronic Funds Transfer Reports (EFTR): Report international crypto transfers of $10,000 CAD or more.
  • Terrorist Property Reports: Immediately report if a VASP knows or suspects it has possession of terrorist property.
  • KYC verification: All Canadian VASPs must verify identity of clients before processing transactions above thresholds.

For investigators: FINTRAC can share financial intelligence with Canadian law enforcement (RCMP, CBSA, CSIS) in designated circumstances. Access is not direct — investigators must follow proper channels through their agency's Financial Intelligence team.

📋 PCMLTFA — The Primary Canadian AML Legislation

The Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act (PCMLTFA) is the foundational Canadian law governing AML/CFT obligations, including for cryptocurrency businesses.

Key provisions for investigators:

  • Establishes the legal framework for MSB registration and compliance obligations
  • Requires VASPs to maintain records for 5 years — transaction records, KYC documents, business correspondence
  • Provides the legal basis for FINTRAC to receive and analyze financial intelligence
  • The "Travel Rule" (FINTRAC's version of FATF Recommendation 16): VASPs must collect and transmit originator and beneficiary information for crypto transfers ≥ $1,000 CAD
  • Penalties for non-compliance: up to $2M per violation and/or imprisonment

Practical investigative use: When serving Canadian VASPs with legal process, reference the PCMLTFA obligations to establish that the VASP has maintained the records you're requesting. Their obligation to retain records under PCMLTFA is your assurance the data exists.

🔏 PIPEDA — Privacy Considerations for OSINT in Canada

The Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act (PIPEDA) governs how private-sector organizations collect, use, and disclose personal information in Canada. For blockchain investigators, PIPEDA creates important boundaries on OSINT activities.

PIPEDA considerations for blockchain investigators:

  • Publicly available information: PIPEDA contains an exemption for information that is "publicly available" as specified by regulation (including information appearing in a public registry, a court record, or deliberately made public by the individual). On-chain blockchain data is generally considered publicly available.
  • Third-party OSINT tools: Be cautious with tools that access breached/leaked databases (Snusbase, IntelX) — accessing and using personal information obtained through breaches may raise legal issues under PIPEDA even if the tool is technically accessible.
  • Law enforcement exception: PIPEDA does not apply to federal or provincial law enforcement agencies acting in their official capacity. Private investigators and civilian researchers do not have this exception.
  • Cross-border data: When investigating Canadian suspects using US-based tools, PIPEDA compliance obligations may still apply to how you store and use the information collected.

Best practice: Consult your agency's legal counsel or privacy officer before using breach-data tools or conducting surveillance-level OSINT on Canadian suspects.

Mutual Legal Assistance Treaty. International agreement enabling legal cooperation, used to obtain evidence from foreign-jurisdiction VASPs.
Monero (XMR)
Privacy coin using ring signatures, stealth addresses, and RingCT to hide sender, recipient, and amounts. Significantly harder to trace. When a trail goes dark at XMR, investigators pivot to exchange chokepoints (on/off ramps).
OSINT
Open Source Intelligence. Information from publicly available sources — social media, domain registrations, breach databases, court filings. Combined with on-chain data for full attribution under FACT.
Pass-Through Address
Address receiving and immediately sending all funds, leaving $0 balance. A major red flag for layering and illicit activity.
PCMLTFA
Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act — Canada's primary AML legislation. Governs VASP/MSB obligations including KYC, record retention (5 years), STR filing, and the Travel Rule ($1,000 CAD threshold).
Peeling Chain
Obfuscation: funds move through many sequential addresses, each time peeling a small amount. Bitcoin UTXO mechanics allow investigators to track payment vs change output at each step.
PIPEDA
Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act — Canada's private-sector privacy law. Governs collection, use, and disclosure of personal information. Law enforcement agencies have exceptions; private investigators do not.
Ransomware
Malware encrypting victim data and demanding crypto payment (BTC/XMR). Each victim receives a unique payment address. Investigators trace payments through laundering chain to OTC cash-out venues.
SAR / STR
Suspicious Activity Report (US) / Suspicious Transaction Report (Canada). Filed by financial institutions when suspicious transactions are detected. Primary source of investigative leads.
Smart Contract
Self-executing code on a blockchain enforcing agreement terms automatically. Powers all DeFi. Publicly readable on Etherscan — deployer wallet and code are investigable.
Supply Chain Attack
Compromising a target indirectly via a trusted third-party supplier. The Bybit hack exploited Safe{Wallet} — a developer machine compromise that injected malicious JS into Bybit's signing UI.
Taint Analysis
Calculating exposure (degrees of separation) between a wallet and a sanctioned/illicit source. Expressed as percentage. 15%+ exposure to ransomware-linked wallets is a significant red flag.
Token (ERC-20 / TRC-20 / SPL)
Virtual currency running on another blockchain's infrastructure. USDT is ERC-20 on Ethereum and TRC-20 on Tron. Investigation requires reading the host chain, not a separate ledger.
Tornado Cash
Ethereum-based mixing protocol. OFAC sanctioned 2022. Lazarus Group's preferred ETH mixing tool. Developer Roman Storm convicted 2024. Using TC does not make tracing impossible — timing and amount analysis can correlate deposits and withdrawals.
UTXO (Unspent Transaction Output)
Bitcoin's accounting model. Transactions consume UTXOs as inputs and create new UTXOs as outputs. Powers CIUA clustering heuristic. Central to peeling chain analysis.
VASP (Virtual Asset Service Provider)
Any platform for buying, selling, or trading crypto. In Canada, regulated as MSBs under FINTRAC/PCMLTFA. KYC data obtainable via legal process.
YARA
Pattern-matching tool used in malware analysis. YARA rules can be written to detect cryptocurrency wallet address patterns in binary files, enabling automated scanning of malware samples for embedded wallet addresses.
