Pillar 1: Cybersecurity Tradecraft

🔬 DFIR & Malware Analysis Extracting C2 & Wallet Artifacts ›

Digital Forensics and Incident Response (DFIR) is the first line of evidence collection. For e-crime investigators, the key is extracting crypto-relevant artifacts from malware payloads — not just understanding what the malware did, but identifying the financial infrastructure behind it.

  • Ransomware payloads contain hardcoded Bitcoin addresses and wallet references in their ransom notes or configuration files. These are extracted via static analysis (Ghidra, IDA Pro) or memory forensics (Volatility).
  • Infostealer logs (recovered via Cavalier, Hudson Rock) capture saved browser credentials, crypto wallet seeds, and even keylogged exchange login sessions.
  • C2 infrastructure often reuses the same IP ranges or domains across campaigns, creating pivot points that connect a wallet address to a known threat actor group.
  • The Bybit breakthrough: Hudson Rock identified a Safe{Wallet} developer machine infected with infostealer malware weeks before the $1.5B hack.
💡 Analyst Tip
When analyzing ransomware, always extract the full ransom note as a text artifact. Threat actors frequently reuse wallet addresses across victims — a single address can link dozens of separate victim payments into a single financial trail.
🎯 Threat Hunting & IoC Development Proactive On-Chain & Cloud Detection ›

Threat hunting moves the investigator from reactive to proactive. For crypto investigations, this means developing on-chain Indicators of Compromise (IoCs) — behavioral signatures that flag illicit activity before a victim reports it.

  • On-chain IoCs: Wallet addresses linked to known threat actors (FBI PSA lists), bridge contracts associated with prior hacks, mixer contract addresses, and instant-exchange deposit addresses.
  • Cloud IoCs: Unusual API calls to exchange endpoints, credential stuffing patterns against exchange login pages, and abnormal withdrawal request volumes.
  • Cross-domain correlation: Matching network logs (firewall, DNS, VPN) against blockchain timestamps to identify the precise moment a compromise led to a crypto transfer.
📡 Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI) Adversary Profiling & TTP Mapping ›

CTI is the structured discipline of understanding, documenting, and anticipating adversary behavior. For e-crime investigators, CTI converts raw attribution into actionable intelligence that predicts the next strike.

  • MITRE ATT&CK: Map every observed behavior to a TTP. Lazarus Group's "Contagious Interview" campaign maps to T1566 (Phishing) + T1204 (User Execution) + T1041 (Exfiltration).
  • Diamond Model: Structure adversary intelligence around four vertices — Adversary, Infrastructure, Capability, and Victim.
  • Behavioral fingerprinting: Lazarus Group's documented signature includes accepting value losses to expedite laundering — a state-sponsored actor behavioral fingerprint unique to groups with infinite patience.

Pillar 2: Financial Crime Investigation

💰 FININT & OSINT Convergence From Wallet to Real-World Identity ›

Financial Intelligence (FININT) converges blockchain data with open-source intelligence to build a complete picture of the adversary. No single source is sufficient — the power comes from the intersection of multiple independent evidence streams.

  • On-chain → VASP: Tracing funds to an exchange creates a legal chokepoint where KYC data can be obtained.
  • On-chain → social media: Blockchain timestamps matched against bragging posts and social activity.
  • On-chain → domain registrations: The Bybit hack's attribution trail included a domain registered with a Gmail address previously used in Lazarus Group's "Contagious Interview" campaign.
⚖️ AML, PCMLTFA & Regulatory Frameworks Canadian & International Compliance ›

Findings must be legally defensible to drive consequences. Investigators must navigate both Canadian and international regulatory frameworks.

  • PCMLTFA (Canada): Requires Canadian VASPs to collect and report suspicious transactions to FINTRAC.
  • FATF Travel Rule: Requires VASPs to share originator/beneficiary data for transfers above thresholds.
  • OFAC sanctions: Any interaction with OFAC-sanctioned wallets (Lazarus Group, Tornado Cash) creates legal exposure.
  • Chain of custody: Every on-chain screenshot, export, and archived web page must be timestamped and documented to be admissible.
🔐 Fraud Investigation & Asset Recovery From Evidence to Seizure ›

The ultimate goal of financial crime investigation is disruption — freezing assets before they vanish, securing KYC records that identify perpetrators, and building the case for criminal prosecution or civil forfeiture.

  • Peeling chain tracking: Following funds through sequential wallets requires documenting each hop with its TXID, amount, timestamp, and any attribution label.
  • Bridge analysis: Cross-chain moves (BTC→AVAX, ETH→TRON) are traced using specialist tools (Pulsy, Socketscan).
  • Emergency freeze: Coordinating with exchange compliance teams and law enforcement to freeze funds before the criminal's withdrawal window closes.

You cannot investigate what you don't understand. This module builds foundational knowledge of how blockchains work and why they're investigable.

⛓️ What is a Blockchain?

A blockchain is a publicly available, immutable ledger of every transaction ever made on that network. When Person A sends Bitcoin to Person B, that transaction is cryptographically verified by thousands of computers and permanently written into a "block" — which is chained to every previous block. This transparency is what makes blockchain investigation possible.

⚠️ Privacy Coins
Monero (XMR) and Zcash (ZEC) hide sender, recipient, and amount cryptographically. Significantly harder to trace — but not impossible. Exchanges that handle conversions to/from privacy coins create investigable chokepoints.

Glossary & Key Resources

Glossary

🔍
Address Clustering
Grouping multiple blockchain addresses likely controlled by the same entity, using heuristics like Common Input Ownership (CIUA).
APT (Advanced Persistent Threat)
Sophisticated, state-sponsored threat actor. Lazarus Group (DPRK) is the most active crypto-focused APT.
Attribution
Assigning a blockchain address — and ultimately, a specific action — to a specific real-world person. Requires multi-stream evidence convergence under the FACT Framework.
Block Explorer
Tool for querying blockchain data. Examples: Etherscan (ETH), Blockchair (BTC), Solscan (SOL).
Blockchain
A publicly available, immutable ledger of completed transactions. The permanent audit trail that makes crypto investigation possible.
Chain-Hopping
Moving assets between different blockchains via bridges to obscure the trail. E.g., BTC→ETH→TRON.
Change Address
In Bitcoin, the address receiving the "change" back to the sender. Identifying these reveals more wallets controlled by the same entity.
Common Input Ownership (CIUA)
Bitcoin address clustering heuristic: if two addresses both sign inputs in the same transaction, they share the same private key holder.
Counterparty
The party on the opposite side of a transaction. Identifying VASP counterparties creates legal chokepoints.
DEX (Decentralized Exchange)
A cryptocurrency exchange without a central authority or KYC requirements. Funds swapped without identity verification — complicates investigations but all trades are on-chain.
Direct Exposure
When an address directly transacted with an illicit source (one hop away).
EXIF Data
Metadata embedded in photos, often including GPS coordinates, device info, and timestamps. Can reveal suspect location via photo OSINT.
Exposure
How directly proceeds have traveled from one address to another. Commercial tools quantify as a percentage. 10%+ is typically flagged as significant.
FACT Framework
Forensic Authority & Compliance → Analyze Evidence → Correlate & Sequence → Testify & Transfer. A jurisprudence-centered attribution methodology by Brett Shavers (v1.1, 2025).
FACT Lite
Triage profile (F + A only). Preserves authority and hypothesis discipline without permitting person-level attribution. Must escalate to full FACT before naming anyone.
FATF
Financial Action Task Force. International body setting AML/CFT standards. Coined the term "VASP" and established the Travel Rule for crypto transfers.
FININT
Financial Intelligence. Combining blockchain analytics with OSINT, AML data, and traditional financial intelligence to identify real-world actors behind crypto transactions.
Flash Loan Attack
DeFi exploit using uncollateralized instant loans to manipulate prices and drain protocols in a single transaction. Entirely on-chain and traceable.
FOR500
SANS/GIAC course: Windows Forensic Analysis. Covers artifact recovery, registry, memory forensics — critical for extracting crypto-related artifacts from compromised endpoints.
FOR508
SANS/GIAC course: Advanced Incident Response, Threat Hunting, and Digital Forensics. Core DFIR course for E-Crime Disruptors; maps to FACT Stages A and C.
FOR509
SANS/GIAC course: Enterprise Cloud Forensics and Incident Response. Essential for investigating exchange infrastructure compromises and API-layer attacks.
FOR589
SANS/GIAC course: Cybercrime Investigations. The most directly applicable SANS course — covers cryptocurrency tracing, dark web, ransomware payments, and OSINT techniques.
GIAC
Global Information Assurance Certification. The certification arm of SANS Institute. Issues certifications aligned to SANS courses (e.g., GCFE for FOR500, GCFE for FOR508).
Indirect Exposure
When an address received funds that passed through an intermediate address from an illicit source (two+ hops away).
Instant Exchange
No-KYC swap services (ChangeNow, FixedFloat). Frequently used for money laundering layering. On-chain record still exists — no subpoena target but traceable.
KYC (Know Your Customer)
Real-world identification collected by exchanges — government ID, selfie, address. The key data investigators request via legal process to link a wallet to a person.
Lazarus Group
North Korea's elite state-sponsored hacking unit. Stole $2B+ in 2025 including the $1.5B Bybit hack. Controlled by the Reconnaissance General Bureau (RGB).
Mempool
Queue of unconfirmed transactions waiting for block inclusion. Investigators can monitor the mempool for real-time alerts on tracked addresses.
Mixer / Tumbler
Services that pool and redistribute crypto to break the chain of association. Examples: Tornado Cash (ETH), Wasabi Wallet (BTC). OFAC has sanctioned several.
MLAT
Mutual Legal Assistance Treaty. International agreement enabling legal cooperation between countries for obtaining evidence from foreign-jurisdiction VASPs.
Multisig Wallet
Wallet requiring multiple private key signatures. Used for exchange security — exploited in Bybit and WazirX hacks via supply chain attacks on signing infrastructure.
OFAC
U.S. Office of Foreign Assets Control. Sanctions cryptocurrency addresses linked to Lazarus Group, Tornado Cash, and other sanctioned entities.
OSINT
Open Source Intelligence. Information from publicly available sources — social media, domain registrations, court filings. Combined with on-chain data for full attribution.
OTC Broker
Over-the-counter broker facilitating large crypto trades outside of exchanges. Sometimes used for money laundering cash-out.
Pass-Through Address
An address that receives and immediately sends all funds, leaving $0 balance. Major red flag for illicit layering infrastructure.
PCMLTFA
Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act. Canada's primary AML legislation. Requires VASPs to report suspicious transactions to FINTRAC.
Peeling Chain
Funds moved through many sequential addresses, each time peeling off a slightly smaller amount, to confuse investigators tracing the trail.
Private Key
The secret cryptographic key controlling a wallet. Whoever has the private key controls the funds — and is the person FACT's C stage must attribute.
Pseudonymous
Not fully anonymous. Blockchain addresses don't contain names, but behavioral patterns and links to real-world identity make them investigable.
Rug Pull
Scam where developers drain a token's liquidity pool and abandon the project after attracting investor funds.
SANS Institute
The world's largest cybersecurity training organization. Offers DFIR, cybercrime, and incident response courses (FOR500, FOR508, FOR509, FOR589, SEC504) directly relevant to crypto investigation.
SAR (Suspicious Activity Report)
Report filed by financial institutions for suspicious transactions. Common investigative lead source for crypto cases (FINTRAC in Canada, FinCEN in USA).
SEC504
SANS/GIAC course: Hacker Tools, Techniques, and Incident Handling. Covers adversary TTPs, network forensics, and incident scoping — foundational for understanding exchange hacks and ransomware delivery.
Smart Contract
Self-executing code on a blockchain. DeFi protocols run on smart contracts; exploits target their logic. All execution is publicly visible on-chain.
Straw Identity
A person used to open an account on behalf of the real criminal. Common in money laundering. FACT's A stage requires this as a candidate actor.
Supply Chain Attack
Compromising a target by attacking a trusted third-party supplier. E.g., the Safe{Wallet} developer machine in the Bybit hack.
Timing Analysis
Cross-referencing blockchain timestamps against external events (social media, login logs) to correlate identities or behaviors.
Tornado Cash
Ethereum-based mixing protocol. Sanctioned by OFAC in 2022. Lazarus Group's preferred ETH mixing tool. Developer Roman Storm convicted in 2024.
Transaction Hash (TXID)
Unique identifier for a specific blockchain transaction. The receipt number — used to look up exact transactions on block explorers.
VASP
Virtual Asset Service Provider. Any platform for buying, selling, or trading crypto. Most hold KYC data investigators can obtain via legal process.
