Pillar 1: Cybersecurity Tradecraft

🔬 DFIR & Malware Analysis Extracting C2 & Wallet Artifacts ›

Digital Forensics and Incident Response (DFIR) is the first line of evidence collection. For e-crime investigators, the key is extracting crypto-relevant artifacts from malware payloads — not just understanding what the malware did, but identifying the financial infrastructure behind it.

  • Ransomware payloads contain hardcoded Bitcoin addresses and wallet references in their ransom notes or configuration files. These are extracted via static analysis (Ghidra, IDA Pro) or memory forensics (Volatility).
  • Infostealer logs (recovered via Cavalier, Hudson Rock) capture saved browser credentials, crypto wallet seeds, and even keylogged exchange login sessions.
  • C2 infrastructure often reuses the same IP ranges or domains across campaigns, creating pivot points that connect a wallet address to a known threat actor group.
  • The Bybit breakthrough: Hudson Rock identified a Safe{Wallet} developer machine infected with infostealer malware weeks before the $1.5B hack.
💡 Analyst Tip
When analyzing ransomware, always extract the full ransom note as a text artifact. Threat actors frequently reuse wallet addresses across victims — a single address can link dozens of separate victim payments into a single financial trail.
🎯 Threat Hunting & IoC Development Proactive On-Chain & Cloud Detection ›

Threat hunting moves the investigator from reactive to proactive. For crypto investigations, this means developing on-chain Indicators of Compromise (IoCs) — behavioral signatures that flag illicit activity before a victim reports it.

  • On-chain IoCs: Wallet addresses linked to known threat actors (FBI PSA lists), bridge contracts associated with prior hacks, mixer contract addresses, and instant-exchange deposit addresses.
  • Cloud IoCs: Unusual API calls to exchange endpoints, credential stuffing patterns against exchange login pages, and abnormal withdrawal request volumes.
  • Cross-domain correlation: Matching network logs (firewall, DNS, VPN) against blockchain timestamps to identify the precise moment a compromise led to a crypto transfer.
📡 Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI) Adversary Profiling & TTP Mapping ›

CTI is the structured discipline of understanding, documenting, and anticipating adversary behavior. For e-crime investigators, CTI converts raw attribution into actionable intelligence that predicts the next strike.

  • MITRE ATT&CK: Map every observed behavior to a TTP. Lazarus Group's "Contagious Interview" campaign maps to T1566 (Phishing) + T1204 (User Execution) + T1041 (Exfiltration).
  • Diamond Model: Structure adversary intelligence around four vertices — Adversary, Infrastructure, Capability, and Victim.
  • Behavioral fingerprinting: Lazarus Group's documented signature includes accepting value losses to expedite laundering — a state-sponsored actor behavioral fingerprint unique to groups with infinite patience.

Pillar 2: Financial Crime Investigation

💰 FININT & OSINT Convergence From Wallet to Real-World Identity ›

Financial Intelligence (FININT) converges blockchain data with open-source intelligence to build a complete picture of the adversary. No single source is sufficient — the power comes from the intersection of multiple independent evidence streams.

  • On-chain → VASP: Tracing funds to an exchange creates a legal chokepoint where KYC data can be obtained.
  • On-chain → social media: Blockchain timestamps matched against bragging posts and social activity.
  • On-chain → domain registrations: The Bybit hack's attribution trail included a domain registered with a Gmail address previously used in Lazarus Group's "Contagious Interview" campaign.
⚖️ AML, PCMLTFA & Regulatory Frameworks Canadian & International Compliance ›

Findings must be legally defensible to drive consequences. Investigators must navigate both Canadian and international regulatory frameworks.

  • PCMLTFA (Canada): Requires Canadian VASPs to collect and report suspicious transactions to FINTRAC.
  • FATF Travel Rule: Requires VASPs to share originator/beneficiary data for transfers above thresholds.
  • OFAC sanctions: Any interaction with OFAC-sanctioned wallets (Lazarus Group, Tornado Cash) creates legal exposure.
  • Chain of custody: Every on-chain screenshot, export, and archived web page must be timestamped and documented to be admissible.
🔐 Fraud Investigation & Asset Recovery From Evidence to Seizure ›

The ultimate goal of financial crime investigation is disruption — freezing assets before they vanish, securing KYC records that identify perpetrators, and building the case for criminal prosecution or civil forfeiture.

  • Peeling chain tracking: Following funds through sequential wallets requires documenting each hop with its TXID, amount, timestamp, and any attribution label.
  • Bridge analysis: Cross-chain moves (BTC→AVAX, ETH→TRON) are traced using specialist tools (Pulsy, Socketscan).
  • Emergency freeze: Coordinating with exchange compliance teams and law enforcement to freeze funds before the criminal's withdrawal window closes.

Blockchain Foundations

You cannot investigate what you don't understand. This module builds foundational knowledge of how blockchains work and why they're investigable.

⛓️ What is a Blockchain?

A blockchain is a publicly available, immutable ledger of every transaction ever made on that network. Think of it like a global spreadsheet anyone can read, but no one can delete rows from.

When Person A sends Bitcoin to Person B, that transaction is cryptographically verified by thousands of computers and permanently written into a "block" — which is chained to every previous block. This transparency is what makes blockchain investigation possible. Unlike traditional financial crime where investigators need warrants to access bank records, on-chain data is publicly visible to everyone.

How Transactions Work

CLICK EACH STEP TO EXPAND

🔑 Step 1 — Wallet Creation Public Key / Private Key ›

A user creates a crypto wallet, which generates a private key (a secret cryptographic password) and a public address (like a bank account number). The address is pseudonymous — it doesn't contain the user's name, but it is permanently traceable on the blockchain.

Whoever controls the private key controls the funds. This is why investigators focus on identifying the controller of an address, not just the address itself — the private key may be held by a different person than the one named in KYC records.

📡 Step 2 — Transaction Broadcast Signed & Submitted to Network ›

When sending crypto, the sender broadcasts a signed transaction to the network: "I'm sending 0.5 BTC from address 1ABC… to address 3XYZ…." This is signed with the private key, cryptographically proving the sender controls the funds.

This signature is the on-chain equivalent of a handwritten signature on a bank transfer — and it cannot be forged. For investigators, this means every transaction is authenticated evidence of the controller's intent.

✅ Steps 3–5 — Verification, Confirmation & Public Record Immutable & Permanent ›

Network nodes verify the transaction is valid (the sender has the funds, the signature is correct) and add it to the mempool (pending queue). Miners or validators bundle pending transactions into a block and add it to the chain.

On Bitcoin, this takes ~10 minutes. On Ethereum, ~12 seconds. Once confirmed, the transaction is permanent and irreversible — a core principle investigators rely on. The transaction is now visible to anyone using a block explorer: sender address, recipient address, amount, timestamp, and transaction hash (ID).

MODULE 01B // BEGINNER — FOUNDATIONAL MODEL
UTXO vs Account-Based Tracing

Bitcoin and Ethereum use fundamentally different transaction models. Understanding the distinction is essential — address clustering, change detection, and tracing methodology differ completely between the two.

₿ UTXO Model — Bitcoin

Unspent Transaction Output. Bitcoin does not track balances. Instead, it tracks discrete "coins" — chunks of BTC that were received and not yet spent. When you spend Bitcoin, you consume entire UTXOs and create new ones as change.

Think of UTXOs like physical banknotes. If you have a $50 bill and buy something for $30, you hand over the $50 and receive $20 change. Bitcoin works identically — if you receive 1 BTC and send 0.4 BTC, the full 1 BTC UTXO is consumed and a new 0.4 BTC output goes to the recipient, plus a change output of ~0.6 BTC (minus fees) back to you.

Investigator implication: Every Bitcoin transaction exposes all inputs simultaneously — enabling Common Input Ownership clustering and change address detection. The transaction graph is exceptionally rich for tracing.

Ξ Account-Based Model — Ethereum

Balance tracking. Ethereum works like a bank account. Each address has a running balance that increases or decreases with each transaction. There is no concept of "consuming" a coin — you simply deduct from your balance and add to the recipient's.

Think of it like a spreadsheet: "Address A has 5 ETH. Address A sends 2 ETH to Address B. Address A now has 3 ETH. Address B now has 2 ETH." No change addresses. No input consolidation. Each transaction is a direct balance adjustment.

Investigator implication: No change address detection. CIUA clustering doesn't apply. Smart contract interactions add complexity — a single transaction can trigger dozens of internal transfers only visible on block explorers that decode contract calls.

🔗 UTXO Clustering Deep Dive — CIUA & Change Detection Bitcoin's Built-In Investigative Gift ›

Common Input Ownership (CIUA): In a Bitcoin transaction, if Address A and Address B both appear as inputs (senders), they must both have signed the transaction — meaning the same person controls both private keys. This single heuristic has allowed investigators to cluster thousands of addresses into single entities.

Change address detection: When a Bitcoin transaction sends funds, it typically creates two outputs: (1) the payment to the recipient and (2) change returned to the sender. Investigators identify change outputs by analyzing amounts (round numbers often go to recipients; odd amounts are often change), timing (change address first used at same time as original wallet), and script type matching.

Peeling chain identification: A common UTXO laundering pattern where each transaction sends the majority of funds forward and a small "peel" to a separate address. Investigators identify these by the consistent ratio of forward vs. peel amounts and the sequential timing. Each hop is still traceable — the pattern itself is a signature.

⚡ Practice Exercise
Pick any Bitcoin transaction hash on Blockchair. Identify: (1) how many inputs are there? (2) are multiple input addresses present — suggesting CIUA clustering? (3) which output is likely change vs. payment based on amount and address type? This three-question framework applies to every BTC tracing task.
📋 Ethereum Internal Transactions — The Hidden Layer What Etherscan Doesn't Show By Default ›

In Ethereum, a single external transaction (visible as one row on Etherscan) can trigger dozens of internal transactions — value transfers between contracts, contract-to-contract calls, and token movements — that are not visible on the main transaction view.

This is critical for investigating DeFi exploits: the "exploit transaction" may appear to send 0 ETH on the surface, while internally it borrows $100M via flash loan, drains a liquidity pool, repays the loan, and pockets the profit — all in a single block. Investigators must always check the "Internal Transactions" tab on Etherscan and use Tenderly or Phalcon for full execution trace decoding.

Token transfers: ERC-20 token transfers (USDT, USDC, etc.) are also invisible in the main ETH transaction view — they appear only in the "Token Transfers" tab. An investigator who only reads the main ETH value will miss the entire economic substance of most DeFi transactions.

Core Investigation Terms

🏷️ Attribution The Primary Goal ›

The process of labeling or assigning specific blockchain addresses to specific entities — and ultimately, to specific people. Attribution sources include exchange records, court filings, law enforcement databases, open-source intel, and commercial blockchain analytics platforms.

FACT distinction: The FACT Framework separates technical identification (which device/account acted?) from investigative attribution (which person acted?). Identifying that address 0x123… belongs to Binance is technical identification. Linking that account to a named individual via KYC is attribution.

🏦 VASP / KYC / Counterparty Legal Leverage Points ›

VASP (Virtual Asset Service Provider): Any platform used to buy, sell, trade, or exchange virtual currency. VASPs often maintain KYC records that can identify the real-world controller of an address.

KYC (Know Your Customer): Real-world identification an entity may collect — government ID, selfie, address, date of birth. When a VASP is served with legal process and provides KYC data, this is often the case-breaking moment that links a wallet to a person.

Counterparty: The party on the opposite side of a transaction. Identifying counterparties — especially when they are known VASPs or labeled entities — is a core tracing strategy. If a wallet's counterparty is Coinbase, that's a legal chokepoint.

🚧 Obfuscation Techniques Chain-Hopping, Mixers, Peeling Chains ›

Chain-Hopping: Moving assets between different blockchains (e.g., BTC→ETH→TRON via bridges) many times to obscure asset control. Forces investigators to use different block explorers and bridge-tracking tools for each chain. The Lazarus Group's primary post-hack laundering technique.

Mixers & Tumblers: Services that pool and redistribute crypto to break the chain of association. Examples: Tornado Cash (ETH), Wasabi Wallet (BTC). OFAC has sanctioned several mixers. Using them does not make tracing impossible — exposure analysis still identifies tainted funds entering and exiting.

Peeling Chains: Sending funds through many sequential addresses, each time peeling off a slightly smaller amount, to confuse investigators following the trail.

Instant Exchanges: No-KYC swap services (ChangeNow, FixedFloat) used to exchange one currency for another without identity verification. Frequent layering stop. No subpoena target — but the on-chain swap record is still visible.

DeFi Protocols: Running funds through decentralized exchanges or lending protocols. No central operator to subpoena, but all transactions are on-chain and fully traceable.

📊 Exposure, Pass-Through & Transaction Hash Core Investigative Concepts ›

Exposure: A measure of how directly illicit proceeds traveled from one address to another. Direct: A sent to B. Indirect: A→B→C, where C has indirect exposure to A. Commercial tools quantify exposure as a percentage — over 10–15% is typically flagged as significant.

Pass-Through Address: An address that receives a large sum and immediately sends the entire amount out, leaving a $0 balance. A major red flag for illicit layering. The controller paid a transaction fee for a non-economic step — a classic sign of money laundering infrastructure.

Transaction Hash (TXID): A unique alphanumeric identifier for a specific blockchain transaction — the receipt number. Essential for looking up any specific transaction on a block explorer. Example BTC TXID: 4a5e1e4baab89f3a32518a88c31bc87f618f76673e2cc77ab2127b7afdeda33b

Blockchain Tracing in Practice

The step-by-step methodology for tracing illicit funds — from acquiring an address to identifying a real person. Based on TRM Labs' official investigator methodology.

🗺️ The Investigative Goal

The goal of blockchain tracing is to identify the actual controller of an otherwise pseudo-anonymous address. Trace blockchain transactions to find counterparty exposure with an entity or individual that can provide real-world identity. More simply: follow the money to or from an entity that keeps records.

The Three-Step Framework

1
Identify Illicit Conduct

Acquire the address/transaction from SAR, complaint, OSINT, or source. Confirm illicit activity is present.

2
Trace Proceeds

Follow the flow of funds through the blockchain, mapping the transaction network and identifying leverage points.

3
Seek Disruption

Serve legal process on third parties (VASPs) to obtain KYC data. Seek seizure, forfeiture, or arrest.

Detailed Tracing Steps

A Input Transaction into Blockchain Tool First Step ›

Copy/paste the transaction hash, incoming/outgoing addresses, or other information into a blockchain tracing tool (TRM Labs, Arkham, Etherscan). Best practice: acquire the address electronically to avoid transcription errors. Confirm the blockchain first — an Ethereum-format address starting with 0x could be on Ethereum, BNB Chain, Polygon, Avalanche, or dozens of other EVM chains.

B Map the Network — Identify Leverage Points Key Questions to Ask ›

Once the address is loaded in a tool, conduct a structured assessment:

  • How many transactions was the address involved in?
  • When were the most recent transactions? Is the address still active?
  • How much value is currently held in the address?
  • Is the address part of a larger cluster? Does it appear in a known entity's network?
  • Does the address have attribution labels or risk flags in the tool?
  • Does it have direct or indirect exposure to a VASP that keeps KYC records?

Pass-through red flag: Only two transactions, large sums moved within minutes, $0 current balance — all indicators of illicit layering infrastructure rather than legitimate use.

C Identify Third Parties for Legal Process Finding the Subpoena Target ›

After mapping the address and its counterparties, identify which VASPs or custodians have exposure to the traced funds. Screenshot the graph as evidence to present to a prosecutor or supervisor. Use a blockchain tool's "Know Your VASP" feature to find each exchange's jurisdiction, legal contact information, and KYC availability.

D Serve Legal Process & Address Clustering Getting Identity Data ›

Issue subpoenas or court orders to identified VASPs requesting: KYC documents, subscriber info, email associations, IP address logs, full transaction history, device IDs, and all associated accounts.

Address clustering — the investigator's force multiplier: One person typically controls many wallets. Two core heuristics:

  • Common Input Ownership (CIUA): If two addresses both sign inputs in the same Bitcoin transaction, they share the same private key holder — likely the same person.
  • Change Address Detection: Bitcoin transactions often send change back to the sender; identifying the change address reveals additional wallets controlled by the same entity.

Always request omnibus "all records associated with the account and registrant" to capture data you haven't specifically anticipated.

OSINT Techniques

Open Source Intelligence (OSINT) is how investigators go from a wallet address to a real person. These are the exact methods used by ZachXBT and professional investigators daily.

The OSINT Investigation Workflow

1️⃣ Phase 1 — On-Chain Pivot Points First Foothold in the Real World ›

Start with what the blockchain gives you: wallet addresses, transaction hashes, amounts, timing. Look for exchange deposits (which require KYC), NFT mints (public records), ENS names (human-readable Ethereum names), or any address already labeled in blockchain analytics tools. These are your first pivot points from the on-chain world into the real world.

2️⃣ Phase 2 — Social Media Intelligence Criminals Brag ›

Criminals brag. Search X/Twitter, Telegram, Discord, and Reddit for mentions of wallet addresses, transaction amounts, project names, or usernames. ZachXBT identified the $243M BTC theft suspects partly through their social media spending patterns — luxury purchases and nightclub appearances that were inconsistent with any legitimate income source.

3️⃣ Phase 3 — Username & Email Pivots Cross-Platform Identity Tracking ›

A username used in one context often reappears elsewhere. Use OSINT Industries, Sherlock, and IntelX to find all accounts associated with a username, email, or phone number across hundreds of platforms. Breach databases (Snusbase, LeakPeek) may reveal passwords, IPs, or real names from historical data breaches.

4️⃣ Phase 4 — Domain & Infrastructure Analysis Linking Scam Sites to Real Identities ›

WHOIS records, SSL certificate history, Shodan/Censys scans, and domain registration data can link a scam website to a real identity or to other criminal infrastructure. The Lazarus Group registered bybit-assessment[.]com using the email [email protected] hours before the $1.5B hack — a traceable footprint.

Certificate Transparency logs (crt.sh) reveal all SSL certificates ever issued for a domain or email, often uncovering a network of related criminal infrastructure registered under the same identity.

5️⃣ Phase 5 — Timing Analysis Correlating On-Chain & Off-Chain Events ›

Matching the exact time of a blockchain transaction to a social media post, login event, or real-world activity. Key timing red flags:

  • Funds moving within minutes of a hack announcement
  • Transactions at unusual hours — indicating specific timezone
  • Rapid cascading transactions suggesting automated laundering scripts
  • Dormant addresses activating after months — criminal cashing out
6️⃣ Phase 6 — Archive & Document Everything Preserving Evidence Before It Disappears ›

Use Wayback Machine and Archive.today to preserve evidence before suspects delete it. Screenshot with timestamps. Organize everything in Obsidian or a similar link-chart tool. Your documentation becomes the chain of custody.

Critical rule: Archive immediately when you find evidence — before you do anything else. Suspects routinely delete social media posts, Telegram accounts, and websites within hours of becoming aware of an investigation.

Bridge & Cross-Chain Reconciliation

Cross-chain movement is the most common advanced laundering technique. Every bridge creates investigable records — but only if you know where to look and how to reconcile across ledgers.

🌉 Why Criminals Chain-Hop

Moving assets across chains serves two laundering goals: it breaks the continuity of the on-chain trail (forcing investigators to switch tools and chain-specific explorers), and it can move funds into lower-liquidity chains where compliant exchanges are fewer. The Lazarus Group's post-Bybit laundering involved at least four chains within 72 hours of the theft.

The good news: every bridge transaction creates a permanent, public record on both chains. The two records are linked by amount, timing, and often a unique message ID. Cross-chain tracing is complex but never a dead end.

Bridge Transaction Anatomy

🔬 How a Bridge Transaction Creates Two Records Lock-and-Mint vs Burn-and-Mint vs Atomic Swap ›

Lock-and-Mint (most common): User sends Asset X to a bridge contract on Chain A. The bridge locks the asset. On Chain B, the bridge mints a wrapped equivalent (e.g., WBTC on Ethereum). Investigator sees: deposit TX on Chain A + mint TX on Chain B, linked by the bridge contract address and often a unique nonce or message ID.

Burn-and-Mint: User burns the wrapped asset on Chain B; bridge mints native asset on Chain A. Common for Circle's CCTP (USDC cross-chain). Range Protocol tracks these specifically.

Atomic Swap: Peer-to-peer exchange across chains with no bridge custodian. Rarer but used for privacy — harder to trace because there is no central bridge contract to identify.

Reconciliation method: Match the departure TX on Chain A (amount, timestamp, bridge contract) to the arrival TX on Chain B (same amount minus fees, within a few minutes, bridge mint contract). The bridge contract address itself is a unique investigative anchor — all transactions through the same bridge share that anchor.

🛠️ Cross-Chain Investigation Workflow Step-by-Step Reconciliation Protocol ›
  • Step 1 — Identify the bridge: When a wallet sends funds to a known bridge contract (Stargate, Across, Synapse, cBridge, Hop Protocol, Wormhole), note the bridge name, destination chain encoded in the contract calldata, and the departure TX hash.
  • Step 2 — Use Pulsy or Socketscan: Input the departure TX. Pulsy aggregates bridge activity across protocols and returns the corresponding arrival TX on the destination chain automatically.
  • Step 3 — Manual fallback (CCTP/Range Protocol): For USDC moves via Circle's CCTP, use Range Protocol. Search by the source chain TX hash — it returns the destination chain and arrival TX.
  • Step 4 — Switch explorers: Open the destination chain explorer (Tronscan, Solscan, Arbiscan, etc.) and load the arrival wallet. Continue tracing from there.
  • Step 5 — Document each hop: Record departure chain, departure TX, departure amount, arrival chain, arrival TX, arrival amount, bridge protocol, and timestamp delta for every cross-chain move. This chain-of-custody log is your evidence for prosecutors.
⚠️ Amount Discrepancy Note
Bridge fees mean the arrival amount will always be slightly less than the departure amount. A 0.1–0.5% discrepancy is normal and does not indicate a chain break. Larger discrepancies may indicate additional layering steps within the bridge protocol itself.

Smart Contract Forensics

DeFi exploits are entirely on-chain — every step of the attack is a permanent public record. This module covers how to read and investigate smart contract transactions, flash loan attacks, MEV, and NFT fraud.

📋 Reading a Smart Contract Transaction Etherscan → Tenderly → Phalcon ›

A smart contract transaction on Etherscan shows the raw calldata — encoded function calls and parameters that look like hexadecimal strings. To understand what actually happened, investigators use execution trace decoders:

  • Tenderly: Paste a TX hash. Tenderly decodes the full execution trace — every internal call, every state change, every token balance update — in human-readable form. Free tier available. Essential for DeFi exploit analysis.
  • Phalcon (by BlockSec): Specialized in DeFi attack analysis. Automatically classifies exploit patterns (flash loan attack, price manipulation, reentrancy) and traces the full fund flow in a visual graph.
  • Etherscan "Internal Transactions" tab: Shows contract-to-contract calls but without decoded function names. First stop before moving to Tenderly.
  • 4byte.directory: Decodes raw function signatures (0xa9059cbb = "transfer(address,uint256)"). Useful when Tenderly doesn't have the ABI.
⚡ Flash Loan Attack Investigation Anatomy of a DeFi Exploit ›

A flash loan is an uncollateralized loan that must be borrowed and repaid within the same blockchain transaction. Attackers use them to temporarily control enormous sums for price manipulation.

Attack anatomy (Euler Finance, $197M, March 2023):

  • Attacker borrows $30M DAI via flash loan from Aave
  • Deposits into Euler Finance, receives eDAI (deposit tokens)
  • Takes out 10x leveraged position — exploiting a donation function bug that inflates their collateral
  • Liquidates their own position at favorable rate, draining Euler's reserves
  • Repays flash loan — entire sequence in one TX, profits extracted to attacker wallet

Investigation steps:

  • Identify the exploit TX (sudden large balance change in a protocol's contract)
  • Load in Phalcon — it automatically identifies flash loan source and profit extraction
  • Trace the attacker's wallet backward to find funding source (often Tornado Cash or a freshly funded exchange withdrawal)
  • Forward-trace profit wallet to mixing or VASP deposits
  • Check deployer wallet of the attacker's contract — often used across multiple exploits
🤖 MEV — Maximal Extractable Value Front-Running, Sandwich Attacks & On-Chain Investigation ›

MEV (Maximal Extractable Value) refers to profit extracted by controlling the ordering of transactions within a block. Validators and searchers can insert, reorder, or censor transactions to profit at users' expense.

Front-running: A bot sees a large pending swap in the mempool, submits the same trade with a higher gas fee to execute first, then sells back at a higher price. Result: user's trade executes at a worse price. Investigable on Eigenphi.io and mevboost.pics.

Sandwich attack: Bot places a buy order before a victim's large swap and a sell order after — "sandwiching" the victim to extract the price impact as profit. On-chain signature: three transactions in sequence in the same block, same token pair, third TX directly after victim's TX.

Investigative relevance: MEV bots are sometimes used to launder funds by intentionally losing to a controlled "victim" wallet — a form of on-chain money laundering. Identifying coordinated MEV between related wallets (same deployer, same profit extraction address) is a growing investigative domain.

🖼️ NFT Fraud — Wash Trading, Counterfeits & Phishing Drains Three Distinct NFT Crime Typologies ›

Wash trading: An actor buys their own NFT from themselves (using different wallets) to artificially inflate reported sale prices and volume. Creates false market signal and can be used to "laundering" crypto by establishing a fake cost basis. On-chain signature: seller and buyer wallets funded by the same source, circular payment patterns, no third-party buyers.

Counterfeit collections: Scammers mint pixel-for-pixel copies of popular NFT collections (Bored Apes, CryptoPunks) with slightly different contract addresses. Victims buy on secondary markets believing they hold the original. Investigation: verify the official contract address against the project's verified channels before any other analysis.

Phishing drains: Victim is lured to a malicious website that prompts them to sign a "setApprovalForAll" or "Permit" transaction — granting the attacker unlimited access to their entire NFT wallet. The drainer executes the approved transfers immediately. On-chain: look for ApprovalForAll events immediately preceding transfers of all NFTs from a wallet to an unknown address.

Airdrop scams: Unknown tokens or NFTs are airdropped to wallets. When the victim interacts with the token contract to check or sell it, the interaction triggers a malicious transfer. Prevention and investigation: never interact with unsolicited tokens; trace the deployer of the malicious contract.

Malware Analysis for Crypto Investigators

Extracting crypto-relevant artifacts from malware payloads — wallet addresses, C2 infrastructure, and victim credentials — is the technical bridge between Pillar 1 (Cybersecurity) and Pillar 2 (Financial Intelligence).

🔗 SANS Connection

🔬 Static Analysis — Extracting Wallet Addresses Without Running the Malware FLOSS, Strings, PE Analysis ›

Static analysis examines malware without executing it — safe for the investigator's machine, and often sufficient to extract key artifacts.

  • FLOSS (FireEye/Mandiant): Automatically extracts obfuscated strings from malware binaries — including XOR-encoded wallet addresses that "strings" alone would miss. Run floss malware.exe and grep the output for BTC/ETH address patterns.
  • YARA rules: Write or apply signature rules targeting cryptocurrency address patterns. A YARA rule matching the regex /1[a-km-zA-HJ-NP-Z1-9]{25,34}/ will flag any file containing a Bitcoin address. The FBI publishes YARA rules for known ransomware families including their embedded wallet addresses.
  • PE Studio / CFF Explorer: Inspect PE headers, import tables, and embedded resources. Ransomware often embeds its ransom note (containing wallet addresses) as a resource within the executable.
  • VirusTotal Intelligence: Submit the hash. VT surfaces previously extracted IOCs, related samples, contacted domains, and any known attribution tags from the research community.
💾 Memory Forensics — Capturing Live Artifacts (Volatility) Extracting Keys, Wallets & C2 from RAM ›

Memory forensics captures the state of a running system — including decrypted malware payloads, active C2 connections, and sometimes in-memory private keys that were never written to disk.

  • Volatility 3: The industry-standard memory analysis framework. Key plugins for crypto investigations: windows.pslist (identify suspicious processes), windows.cmdline (capture command-line arguments including wallet addresses passed as parameters), windows.netscan (identify C2 connections — IP:port of the attacker's server).
  • Dumping process memory: windows.memmap --pid [PID] --dump extracts the full memory of a process. Run FLOSS and grep against the dump for wallet address patterns.
  • Bybit investigative relevance: The Safe{Wallet} developer machine infection was identified through infostealer log analysis — a form of memory artifact recovery. The log captured browser session cookies, saved credentials, and the exact moment the malicious JS was downloaded, connecting it to the Lazarus Group C2 domain.
  • Clipboard hijackers: A common crypto malware class that monitors the clipboard and replaces copied wallet addresses with attacker-controlled addresses. Memory forensics can capture both the legitimate and substituted addresses simultaneously.
🕵️ Infostealer Logs — The Investigator's Intelligence Windfall Cavalier, Hudson Rock & What a Log Contains ›

Infostealers (RedLine, Raccoon, Vidar, LummaC2) silently harvest credentials, cookies, saved passwords, browser history, and crypto wallet files from infected machines. The logs are sold on darknet markets — and are accessible to investigators via commercial platforms.

What an infostealer log contains:

  • Saved browser passwords (exchanges, email, Discord, Telegram)
  • Browser cookies (session tokens that bypass 2FA)
  • Autofill data (name, address, phone, credit card)
  • Crypto wallet files (wallet.dat, seed phrase screenshots)
  • System info (IP address, hardware ID, installed software, geolocation)
  • Screenshots at time of infection

Investigative platforms: Cavalier (Hudson Rock) and similar commercial services index infostealer logs and make them searchable by email, domain, or IP. An investigator who discovers that a suspect's email appears in a Raccoon log from the same week as a hack can cross-reference the captured IP with the VASP subpoena return — linking the human to the on-chain action.

📡 C2 Infrastructure Pivoting From Domain to Wallet to Actor ›

Command-and-Control (C2) infrastructure — the servers that receive stolen data and issue commands to malware — often links multiple campaigns to a single threat actor. C2 domains and IPs are extracted from malware samples and can be pivoted into broader attribution.

  • Passive DNS (RiskIQ/Farsight): Shows all IPs a domain has ever resolved to, and all domains that have ever resolved to an IP. A C2 domain used in one ransomware campaign may resolve to an IP that also hosted a domain registered with the same email used to set up a VASP account.
  • Shodan/Censys: Search for C2 servers by banner fingerprint (Cobalt Strike, Metasploit, custom C2 frameworks leave distinctive HTTP headers). Returns all servers globally running that C2 software.
  • Certificate Transparency (crt.sh): C2 operators often issue TLS certificates that reveal the registrant email — directly linking the infrastructure to a real-world identity.
  • Bybit example: The domain bybit-assessment[.]com resolved to an IP that Certificate Transparency records linked to trevorgreer9312@gmail.com — the same email used in prior Lazarus Group "Contagious Interview" phishing domains.

The Fraud Triangle

🔺 Cressey's Fraud Triangle — Why Fraud Happens

Developed by criminologist Donald Cressey in the 1950s, the Fraud Triangle is the foundational model for understanding why otherwise ordinary people commit occupational fraud. It identifies three conditions that must all be present simultaneously — remove any one side of the triangle and fraud becomes significantly less likely.

Applied across all fraud categories in this guide — from insider exchange theft to pig butchering operators to rug pull developers.

⚡
Perceived Pressure
Incentive / Motivation

The financial or emotional driver pushing the actor toward fraud. In crypto crime: debt, greed, lifestyle inflation, gambling losses, coercion by criminal networks, or state-level resource pressure (Lazarus Group). The actor perceives a financial problem they cannot share with others.

🔓
Perceived Opportunity
Access / Weak Controls

The actor believes they can commit fraud without detection. In crypto: pseudonymous wallets, weak exchange KYC, no-KYC instant swaps, insider access to cold wallet signing, unaudited smart contracts, and victims who lack blockchain literacy. Opportunity is the factor investigators most directly influence by improving controls.

🧠
Rationalization
Justification / Neutralization

The actor constructs a moral justification. Common rationalizations: "Crypto is unregulated so it's not really illegal," "victims are greedy speculators who deserve it," "I'll pay it back," "exchanges are insured anyway," or "the government would just steal it too." Understanding rationalization helps investigators build psychological profiles and anticipate recidivism.

MODULE 08B // EXPERT — PRIVACY COIN INVESTIGATIONS
Privacy Coins — Monero, Zcash & Beyond

Monero and Zcash hide sender, recipient, and amount by default. They are not untraceable — but they demand different techniques and realistic expectations about what on-chain analysis can achieve.

⚠️ Investigator Expectation Reset
Unlike Bitcoin and Ethereum investigations where on-chain tracing is primary, privacy coin investigations depend almost entirely on off-chain intelligence — exchange chokepoints, metadata leaks, infostealer logs, and behavioural OSINT. The blockchain itself provides minimal direct evidence. Adjust your methodology accordingly.
🔒 Monero (XMR) — How It Hides Everything Ring Signatures, Stealth Addresses & RingCT ›

Monero achieves privacy through three layered cryptographic mechanisms:

  • Ring Signatures: A Monero transaction is signed by a group of keys (a "ring") — the actual sender is one of them, but an observer cannot tell which. Default ring size is 16, meaning any given output has a 1-in-16 chance of being the real input. This collapses CIUA clustering — you cannot determine which inputs belong to which senders.
  • Stealth Addresses: The recipient generates a one-time address for each transaction. No two payments to the same person share a visible address on the blockchain — eliminating address clustering entirely.
  • RingCT (Confidential Transactions): Transaction amounts are encrypted using cryptographic commitments. Amounts are verified as valid (sender has enough funds) without revealing the actual value to observers.

Result: A Monero block explorer shows transactions occurring, but reveals no sender, no recipient, and no amount. On-chain analysis provides near-zero direct intelligence.

🔍 Investigating Monero — What Actually Works Chokepoints, Metadata & Timing Attacks ›

Despite strong on-chain privacy, Monero investigations succeed through off-chain techniques:

  • Exchange chokepoints: Criminals must convert XMR to spendable currency somewhere. Any exchange that accepts XMR deposits is a subpoena target. The conversion event (XMR in, BTC/ETH/fiat out) is the critical investigable moment — the exchange has KYC on the account that received the converted funds.
  • Network-level timing analysis: When a Monero transaction is broadcast, the originating node's IP can be captured if law enforcement or researchers are monitoring the peer-to-peer network. The FBI and Europol have operated Monero nodes specifically to capture IP metadata. This is a covert technique, not available to civilian investigators.
  • CipherTrace (Mastercard) Monero tracing: DARPA-funded research produced probabilistic tracing tools for Monero under a DHS contract. The tools use statistical analysis of ring member selection to narrow probable real inputs. Results are probabilistic, not definitive — not yet admissible as primary evidence in most jurisdictions, but useful for generating investigative leads.
  • Operational security failures: The majority of Monero-using criminals are caught through OSINT, infostealer logs, exchange KYC, or conventional law enforcement techniques — not through Monero tracing. Monitoring the suspect's communications is more productive than monitoring the chain.
  • Dusting attacks on XMR: Sending tiny amounts of XMR to a suspect address can sometimes trigger wallet consolidation behavior that reveals patterns, but this technique has significant limitations and legal constraints depending on jurisdiction.
🛡️ Zcash (ZEC) — Shielded vs Transparent The Opt-In Privacy Model ›

Zcash uses zk-SNARKs (zero-knowledge proofs) to allow fully private "shielded" transactions between z-addresses. However, unlike Monero, privacy in Zcash is opt-in — and the majority of Zcash transactions use transparent t-addresses that are fully traceable, identical to Bitcoin.

Investigative reality: Most Zcash usage is in transparent mode. Check first whether the addresses in question are t-addresses (traceable, starts with "t") or z-addresses (shielded, starts with "zs"). If t-addresses, treat identically to Bitcoin investigation. If z-addresses, apply the same off-chain chokepoint strategy as Monero.

Shielded pool analysis: Even with z-addresses, the amount of ZEC entering and exiting the shielded pool is visible — only the internal routing is hidden. Large, timed deposits and withdrawals can be correlated statistically, similar to Tornado Cash analysis.

The Money Laundering Lifecycle

The three-stage AML model — Placement, Layering, Integration — is the universal framework for understanding how illicit crypto funds are cleaned. Mapping each investigation to these stages tells you exactly where in the lifecycle the suspect is, and which intervention is most likely to succeed.

🔄 Why the Three Stages Matter for Investigators

The stage of laundering determines your investigative strategy. Funds in Placement are most vulnerable — they're still closely linked to the crime. Funds in Layering are moving fast but leaving a trail. Funds that have reached Integration are hardest to recover but the actor is most exposed — lifestyle evidence is now available. Knowing which stage you're in shapes your urgency, your legal tools, and your evidence-gathering priorities.

Crypto-Specific AML Stages in Detail

💥 Placement — First Contact With the Financial System When Funds Are Most Traceable & Most Freezable ›

In traditional AML, placement involves physically depositing cash — the highest-risk moment for the launderer. In crypto, placement has already occurred the moment the crime completes: the stolen ETH, the ransomware payment, or the pig butchering deposit lands in the attacker's first wallet.

Investigative opportunity: Funds at placement are directly linked to the crime event with no intervening hops. A freeze at this stage recovers 100% of proceeds. Speed is the defining factor — the Bybit funds were moving within minutes of the theft.

Crypto placement variants:

  • Direct theft receipt: Hacked exchange wallet receives stolen ETH/BTC (Bybit, WazirX)
  • Ransomware payment receipt: Victim sends BTC/XMR to attacker wallet — unique per victim, enabling multi-victim clustering
  • Fraud deposit receipt: Pig butchering victim deposits to scam platform wallet
  • Rug pull LP drain: Developer removes all liquidity — instant placement of investor funds

Action at placement: Emergency freeze request to any VASP where funds land within minutes of identification. Coordinate directly with exchange compliance hot-lines. The Bybit response team froze $40M+ within hours by having pre-established emergency contacts at major exchanges.

🏡 Integration — Where Blockchain Meets Real Life OTC Brokers, Real Estate, Tax Returns & Lifestyle Evidence ›

Integration is where crypto wealth re-enters the legitimate economy as apparent income or assets. This stage creates the richest real-world evidence — bank records, property deeds, luxury purchase receipts, and tax filings that can corroborate on-chain tracing for prosecution.

Common crypto integration methods:

  • OTC broker cash-out: Large-volume crypto sold for cash through over-the-counter desks with minimal scrutiny. The Khinkis ransomware case (March 2026) showed BTC→Tron conversion before OTC cash-out in a documented 3-chain integration pattern.
  • Real estate purchases: Crypto converted to fiat, then used to purchase real estate — especially prevalent in jurisdictions with weak beneficial ownership disclosure (shell companies, nominee directors).
  • Luxury goods: Direct crypto purchase of watches, vehicles, art. Many luxury dealers now accept crypto — creating a paper trail when KYC is collected. Cross-reference suspect lifestyle (social media) against timing of on-chain large withdrawals.
  • Business invoicing: Crypto "consulting income" invoiced through shell companies — especially common for North Korean IT workers acting as front men for Lazarus Group revenue.
  • Tax declaration as "trading profits": Some criminals self-report crypto gains (at a loss) on tax returns to establish a legal income explanation. This tax record becomes evidence of knowledge of the funds.

Investigative approach at integration: Once you have a named suspect from on-chain tracing + VASP KYC, request bank records, property records, luxury dealer records, and tax filings. The lifestyle evidence confirming unexplained wealth — the $243M Bitcoin theft suspects' nightclub spending, Haby's Instagram posts — is the integration-stage evidence that makes the case undeniable in court.

The FACT Attribution Framework

A jurisprudence-centered attribution methodology for digital forensics and incident response. By Brett Shavers (v1.1, December 2025). The legal scaffold that converts technical findings into defensible person-level attribution.

📖 Source
FACT Attribution Framework v1.1 — Brett Shavers, December 2025. DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.18005597. © 2025 Brett Shavers. Used for educational reference.
🧩 Why FACT Exists — The Attribution Gap

Digital forensics has become highly effective at answering what happened, on which device, how, and when. What has been far less developed is a structured, legally grounded way to answer the next question:

"Which specific person, among the realistic candidates, is most likely responsible for those actions — and how confident are we in that conclusion?"

FACT fills this gap. It explicitly separates identification ("what acted?") from attribution ("who acted?") — a distinction that many investigations silently collapse, creating legally vulnerable findings.

Core Distinction: Identification vs Attribution

Technical Identification

"This device / this account / this session performed action X." Does not answer who the real-world actor is. Within the core expertise of digital forensic examiners.

Example: "Wallet 0x1a2b… sent 401,347 ETH to wallet 0x9f3c… on February 21, 2025 at 13:47 UTC."

Investigative Attribution

"Given all available evidence and tested alternatives, Person X is the most plausible actor for Action Y." Requires multi-stream convergence, active falsification of alternatives, and explicit uncertainty.

Example: "Based on KYC records from Binance, timing analysis, and OSINT correlation, the person controlling this wallet is [Name], with strong evidentiary support."

The FACT Lifecycle: F → A → C → T

F
Stage 01
Forensic Authority & Compliance
Document legal authority. Define scope, jurisdiction, proportionality. Establish chain-of-custody. Prevent premature identity assumptions.
A
Stage 02
Analyze Evidence
Identify specific digital actions. Acquire and examine evidence. Evaluate artifact reliability. Generate candidate actors and alternative explanations.
C
Stage 03
Correlate & Sequence
Correlate evidence across identity layers: artifact → device → account → session → person. Test each alternative hypothesis. Determine if convergence threshold is met.
T
Stage 04
Testify & Transfer
Finalize attribution with explicit uncertainty. Prepare legally defensible reports and testimony explaining the reasoning pathway and handling of alternatives.
🔄 FACT is Iterative, Not Linear
FACT is intentionally iterative. New evidence may require revisiting authority in F. Convergence work in C often exposes gaps that send the case back to A. Investigators often iterate A↔C several times before T is even contemplated.

Deep Dives — Each Stage

F Forensic Authority & Compliance Legal Bookend #1 — Before You Touch Anything ›

The F stage establishes the legal foundation that makes all subsequent work admissible. Technical capability does not authorize overreach — the examiner must document the legal basis for every action before beginning analysis.

  • Document legal authority: Warrant, consent, statute, contract, or employment policy.
  • Define scope: Which devices, accounts, date ranges, and data types are in scope?
  • Jurisdiction: Which legal system governs? For crypto cases, this often means multiple jurisdictions.
  • Proportionality: Is the investigative action proportionate to the suspected offense?
  • Preservation and chain-of-custody: Every screenshot, blockchain export, and archived web page must be timestamped and documented.
⚠️ Crypto-Specific F Considerations
For blockchain investigations: Is public blockchain data subject to any collection restrictions? Does your VASP subpoena comply with the exchange's jurisdiction's legal requirements?
A Analyze Evidence Generating Candidate Actors — Not Confirming Assumptions ›

The A stage is about generating a complete picture of the evidence and a complete list of possible actors — not confirming a pre-existing assumption.

  • Identify the specific digital action: What exactly happened? Be precise.
  • Evaluate artifact reliability: Is the blockchain timestamp accurate? Could logs have been manipulated?
  • Generate candidate actors: Who could have controlled this wallet? Consider: the named account holder, a straw identity, a stolen identity, a shared account, a professional money launderer, remote compromise by a third party, an automated process.
  • For crypto investigations: The named account holder on a VASP could be: (1) the actual subject, (2) a straw identity, (3) a stolen identity, or (4) a professional money launderer. Always investigate deeper.
C Correlate & Sequence — Evidence Convergence Multi-Stream Convergence on a Person ›

FACT recommends attribution as justified when evidence from multiple, independent streams converges on the same person while substantially weakening reasonable alternatives.

Identity layers the investigator must traverse:

  • Artifact identification → Device identification → Account/credential identity → Session context → Person

Examples of independent streams in crypto cases:

  • On-chain wallet activity + KYC records from exchange
  • Blockchain timestamps + social media post timestamps
  • IP address from VASP subpoena + residential ISP subscriber records
  • Infostealer logs + court filing records

Falsification duty: FACT treats falsification as a methodological duty. Actively search for evidence that would support alternative actors.

Likelihood language: Weak support / Moderate support / Strong support / Very strong support / Support for alternative / Inconclusive.

T Testify & Transfer — Legally Defensible Reporting Court-Ready Attribution (or Explicit Non-Attribution) ›

The T stage structures how person-level attribution is framed in reporting and testimony so that it withstands legal scrutiny.

As a fact witness: "Based on the evidence we collected and reviewed, our investigation focused on [Name] as the primary suspect, and I forwarded that case for charging."

As an expert witness: "In my opinion, based on my training, experience, and review of the evidence, [Name] is more likely than the other realistic candidates to have performed the acts in question; I considered [A, B, C] as alternatives and ruled them out for the following reasons."

Non-attribution is a correct outcome. When conditions are not met, FACT directs the practitioner to issue an explicit non-attribution finding: "Based on the available evidence and applying the FACT Framework, person-level attribution for Action Y cannot be made."

Canadian Legal Framework for Crypto Investigations

Canada has one of the most developed regulatory frameworks for crypto investigations outside the United States. This module covers the key statutes, reporting obligations, privacy constraints, and asset seizure tools available to Canadian investigators and compliance professionals.

⚠️ Disclaimer
This module is educational context, not legal advice. Consult qualified Canadian legal counsel for case-specific guidance. Statutes and regulatory guidance are current as of March 2026.
📜 PCMLTFA — Canada's Primary AML Statute Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act ›

The Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act (PCMLTFA) is Canada's foundational AML legislation, administered by FINTRAC. Key provisions for crypto investigators:

  • VASP registration as MSBs: All crypto asset trading platforms, exchangers, and ATM operators serving Canadians must register with FINTRAC as Money Services Businesses (MSBs), regardless of where they are incorporated. A foreign exchange serving Canadian customers is subject to PCMLTFA.
  • Travel Rule ($1,000 CAD threshold): VASPs must collect and transmit originator/beneficiary information for crypto transfers of $1,000 CAD or more — equivalent to FATF's Travel Rule. This creates a paper trail for transfers above the threshold.
  • Record retention (5 years): All KYC records, transaction records, and compliance documentation must be retained for at least 5 years. Investigators can subpoena records going back 5 years even if the VASP is no longer operating the specific account.
  • Suspicious Transaction Reports (STRs): VASPs must file STRs with FINTRAC within 30 days of any transaction where there are reasonable grounds to suspect it is related to money laundering or terrorist financing. There is no minimum dollar amount.
  • Large Cash Transaction Reports (LCTRs): Cash transactions of $10,000 CAD or more (in a single transaction, or multiple transactions within 24 hours) must be reported regardless of suspicion.
🏛️ FINTRAC — Canada's Financial Intelligence Unit Disclosure Provisions & Intelligence Value ›

FINTRAC receives financial intelligence reports from MSBs and can disclose this information to law enforcement when specific legal thresholds are met:

  • Voluntary disclosure: FINTRAC may proactively disclose to RCMP, CSIS, CBSA, CRA, and other designated agencies when it has reasonable grounds to suspect the information is relevant to an investigation of money laundering or terrorist financing — without requiring a formal request.
  • Requested disclosure: Designated law enforcement agencies can formally request a FINTRAC disclosure. The request must meet the reasonable grounds threshold.
  • Intelligence value: A FINTRAC disclosure may contain: the identities of parties to suspicious transactions, patterns of transactions across multiple reporting entities, and cross-referenced STRs from different VASPs — potentially revealing laundering patterns that no single institution sees.
  • Limitation: FINTRAC intelligence is financial intelligence — not evidence. It can seed an investigation and direct investigators toward evidence sources, but FINTRAC records themselves are generally not admissible as evidence without further investigation.
🔐 PIPEDA — Privacy Constraints for OSINT What Canadian Investigators Can & Cannot Collect ›

PIPEDA (Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act) governs how private sector organizations collect, use, and disclose personal information. Key investigative implications:

  • Publicly available information exemption: Information that is publicly available — including social media posts, public blockchain transactions, domain registration records, and publicly accessible court filings — is not subject to PIPEDA consent requirements. This is the legal basis for most OSINT collection by civilian investigators.
  • Law enforcement exemption: Organizations may disclose personal information to law enforcement without consent when responding to a lawful subpoena, warrant, or court order — or when they have reasonable grounds to believe disclosure is necessary for national security or the administration of justice.
  • Private investigators: Licensed private investigators in Canada operate under provincial legislation (e.g., Ontario's Private Security and Investigative Services Act). PIPEDA still constrains what personal information they can collect and use — they do not have the same exemptions as law enforcement.
  • Data minimization principle: Even when collection is lawful, PIPEDA requires collecting only the information necessary for the stated purpose. Investigators should document their necessity justification for each category of personal data collected.
🍁 RCMP Asset Seizure — Criminal Code Provisions Restraint Orders, Seizure Warrants & Civil Forfeiture ›

Canadian law provides multiple tools for seizing crypto assets at different stages of an investigation:

  • Restraint Order (s. 462.33 Criminal Code): Court order prohibiting the disposal or transfer of property believed to be proceeds of crime. Available before charges are laid. The most time-sensitive tool — applies at the Placement stage before funds move.
  • Seizure Warrant (s. 462.32 Criminal Code): Authorizes law enforcement to physically seize property. For crypto: requires seizure of the private key or transfer of funds to a government-controlled wallet. Practical challenge — the suspect must be compelled to disclose the private key, or the device containing it must be physically seized and forensically examined.
  • Forfeiture Order (post-conviction): Following a conviction, the court orders permanent forfeiture of proceeds of crime. The standard of proof is balance of probabilities (civil standard) even in a criminal proceeding for the forfeiture hearing itself.
  • Civil Forfeiture (provincial): Most provinces (Ontario, BC, Alberta) have civil forfeiture legislation allowing seizure of property connected to unlawful activity without a criminal conviction. The standard of proof is balance of probabilities. Useful when criminal prosecution is not pursued but assets can be identified.

Private key seizure — practical note: Compelling disclosure of a private key raises Charter s. 7 (right against self-incrimination) and s. 8 (unreasonable search) issues that are still being litigated in Canadian courts. Coordinate with Crown counsel before any compelled private key disclosure demand.

Asset Seizure & Forfeiture

The legal and practical mechanics of seizing, holding, and forfeiting cryptocurrency.

📜 Theories of Forfeiture Legal Frameworks & Statutes ›

To seize and forfeit assets, the government must rely on a forfeiture statute:

  • Simple statutes: NY Penal Law Article 480 — "when any person is convicted of a felony offense, property constituting the proceeds and instrumentalities are subject to forfeiture."
  • Complex statutes: US federal civil forfeiture (18 USC 981) — requires specific nexus to listed predicate offenses.
  • Civil forfeiture: Action against the property itself — can proceed even without a criminal conviction in some jurisdictions.

Best practice: Work with a prosecuting attorney on forfeiture strategy concurrent with the investigation — not as an afterthought.

🔐 Custody Options for Seized Assets Hardware Wallets, VASPs, Third-Party Custodians ›
  • Online Software Wallet: Quick and cheap. Higher theft risk through software compromise. Not recommended for large seizures.
  • Hardware Wallet: Industry standard. More secure. Upfront purchase cost.
  • VASP Custody: Efficient for transferring assets. Risk: commingling, VASPs have been hacked. Requires confidentiality safeguards.
  • Third-Party Custodian: Expert custody, institutional-grade security. Transfer friction and potential costs.
📋 Five Pre-Seizure Considerations Planning Before You Act ›
  • A — Custody Type: What wallet/custodian will the agency use? Match security level to asset value.
  • B — Access Control: Who has access? Implement need-to-know. Track every access event.
  • C — Asset Form: Accept crypto as-is or convert to fiat equivalent? Consider price volatility and liquidation difficulty.
  • D — Contingencies: Software becomes obsolete. VASPs get hacked. New currencies fork. Maintain a "living document" policy reviewed annually.
  • E — Liquidation Plan: VASP market-rate sale, third-party auction, or return as-is? Returning crypto to fraud victims unfamiliar with crypto requires careful planning.

Mutual Legal Assistance Treaty. International agreement enabling legal cooperation between countries for obtaining evidence from foreign-jurisdiction VASPs.
Multisig Wallet
Wallet requiring multiple private key signatures. Used for exchange security — exploited in Bybit and WazirX hacks via supply chain attacks on signing infrastructure.
NFT (Non-Fungible Token)
A unique digital asset on a blockchain representing ownership of a specific item. NFT fraud includes wash trading (circular self-sales to inflate price), counterfeit collections (copies of popular projects with different contract addresses), phishing drains (ApprovalForAll exploits), and airdrop scams with malicious token contracts.
OFAC
U.S. Office of Foreign Assets Control. Sanctions cryptocurrency addresses linked to Lazarus Group, Tornado Cash, and other sanctioned entities.
OSINT
Open Source Intelligence. Information from publicly available sources — social media, domain registrations, court filings. Combined with on-chain data for full attribution.
OTC Broker
Over-the-counter broker facilitating large crypto trades outside of exchanges. Sometimes used for money laundering cash-out.
Pass-Through Address
An address that receives and immediately sends all funds, leaving $0 balance. Major red flag for illicit layering infrastructure.
PCMLTFA
Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act. Canada's primary AML legislation. Requires VASPs to report suspicious transactions to FINTRAC.
PIPEDA
Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act. Canada's federal private-sector privacy law. Publicly available information (blockchain data, social media, court filings) is exempt from consent requirements. Law enforcement has additional exemptions when acting under lawful authority.
Privacy Coin
Cryptocurrency designed to obscure transaction details. Monero (XMR) hides sender, recipient, and amount via ring signatures, stealth addresses, and RingCT. Zcash (ZEC) uses zk-SNARKs for optional shielded transactions. Investigations rely on exchange chokepoints and metadata rather than on-chain tracing.
Peeling Chain
Funds moved through many sequential addresses, each time peeling off a slightly smaller amount, to confuse investigators tracing the trail.
Private Key
The secret cryptographic key controlling a wallet. Whoever has the private key controls the funds — and is the person FACT's C stage must attribute.
Pseudonymous
Not fully anonymous. Blockchain addresses don't contain names, but behavioral patterns and links to real-world identity make them investigable.
Rug Pull
Scam where developers drain a token's liquidity pool and abandon the project after attracting investor funds.
SANS Institute
The world's largest cybersecurity training organization. Offers DFIR, cybercrime, and incident response courses (FOR500, FOR508, FOR509, FOR589, SEC504) directly relevant to crypto investigation.
SAR (Suspicious Activity Report)
Report filed by financial institutions for suspicious transactions. Common investigative lead source for crypto cases (FINTRAC in Canada, FinCEN in USA).
Social Engineering
Psychological manipulation of individuals into performing actions or divulging information. In crypto crime: phishing, fake exchange support impersonation, Lazarus Group's "Contagious Interview" fake job offers, and romance/pig butchering relationship building. The human vector behind the majority of high-value exchange compromises.
Sybil Attack
Creating multiple fake identities (wallets, accounts, nodes) to gain disproportionate influence. In fraud: generating artificial transaction volume, faking DeFi protocol user counts, or manipulating governance votes using many wallets controlled by a single actor. Detected by funding-source clustering analysis.
SEC504
SANS/GIAC course: Hacker Tools, Techniques, and Incident Handling. Covers adversary TTPs, network forensics, and incident scoping — foundational for understanding exchange hacks and ransomware delivery.
Smart Contract
Self-executing code on a blockchain. DeFi protocols run on smart contracts; exploits target their logic. All execution is publicly visible on-chain.
Straw Identity
A person used to open an account on behalf of the real criminal. Common in money laundering. FACT's A stage requires this as a candidate actor.
Supply Chain Attack
Compromising a target by attacking a trusted third-party supplier. E.g., the Safe{Wallet} developer machine in the Bybit hack.
Timing Analysis
Cross-referencing blockchain timestamps against external events (social media, login logs) to correlate identities or behaviors.
Tornado Cash
Ethereum-based mixing protocol. Sanctioned by OFAC in 2022. Lazarus Group's preferred ETH mixing tool. Developer Roman Storm convicted in 2024.
Transaction Hash (TXID)
Unique identifier for a specific blockchain transaction. The receipt number — used to look up exact transactions on block explorers.
UTXO (Unspent Transaction Output)
Bitcoin's transaction model — tracking discrete unspent coins rather than account balances. Spending a UTXO consumes it entirely and creates new outputs. Enables powerful clustering: Common Input Ownership (CIUA) and change address detection. Contrasts with Ethereum's account-based model where balances are simply debited/credited.
VASP
Virtual Asset Service Provider. Any platform for buying, selling, or trading crypto. Most hold KYC data investigators can obtain via legal process.
Zero-Knowledge Proof (ZK-Proof)
Cryptographic method proving knowledge of a value without revealing it. Used in Zcash (zk-SNARKs) for private transactions and in Layer 2 chains (zkSync, StarkNet) for scaling. Investigators must understand ZK systems to assess traceability on ZK-based chains — on-chain analysis is limited but exchange chokepoints still apply.
