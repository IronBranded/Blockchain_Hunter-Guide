🎯 What is an E-Crime Investigator?

An electronic crime (e-crime) investigator—also called a blockchain forensic analyst, crypto investigator, or digital asset investigator—traces the movement of illicit funds through cryptocurrency networks. They combine on-chain blockchain analysis with open-source intelligence (OSINT) to unmask criminals who exploit the pseudo-anonymous nature of crypto.

The field sits at the intersection of traditional financial crime investigation, cybersecurity, and data analysis. Today's best investigators, like the anonymous ZachXBT, have helped recover over $350 million for victims and been first to identify state-sponsored hackers like North Korea's Lazarus Group.

MODULE 01 // BEGINNER
Blockchain Foundations

You cannot investigate what you don't understand. This module builds your foundational knowledge of how blockchains work and why they're investigable.

⛓️ What is a Blockchain?

A blockchain is a publicly available, immutable ledger of every transaction ever made on that network. Think of it like a global spreadsheet that anyone can read, but no one can delete rows from.

When Person A sends Bitcoin to Person B, that transaction is cryptographically verified by thousands of computers worldwide and permanently written into a "block" — which is chained to every previous block. This is why we call it a blockchain.

This transparency is what makes blockchain investigation possible. Unlike traditional financial crime where investigators need warrants to access bank records, on-chain data is publicly visible to everyone.

How Transactions Work

STEP 01
Wallet Creation
A user creates a crypto wallet, which generates a private key (a secret password) and a public address (like a bank account number). The address is pseudonymous — it doesn't contain the user's name, but it's traceable.
STEP 02
Transaction Broadcast
When sending crypto, the sender broadcasts a signed transaction to the network: "I'm sending 0.5 BTC from address 1ABC... to address 3XYZ...." This is signed with the private key, proving the sender owns the funds.
STEP 03
Network Verification
Network nodes (computers) verify the transaction is valid — the sender actually has the funds and the signature is correct. Valid transactions join the mempool (pending queue).
STEP 04
Block Confirmation
Miners or validators bundle pending transactions into a block and add it to the chain. On Bitcoin, this takes ~10 minutes. On Ethereum, ~12 seconds. Once confirmed, the transaction is permanent and irreversible.
STEP 05
Public Record
The transaction is now visible to anyone using a block explorer. Every investigator can see: the sender address, recipient address, amount, timestamp, and transaction hash (ID).

Why Blockchain IS Traceable

🔎 The Investigator's Advantage

Criminals often believe crypto is "anonymous." This is a dangerous misconception for them — and an advantage for investigators. Here's why:

  • Every transaction is public — unlike cash, there's a permanent audit trail
  • Addresses cluster together — one person typically controls multiple addresses that behave in identifiable patterns
  • Exchanges require KYC — eventually, criminals must cash out to real money, and that requires identity verification
  • Metadata leaks — IP addresses, device IDs, timing patterns, and error messages reveal real-world identity
  • Social media bragging — criminals frequently post about wealth or use methods that leave OSINT trails

MODULE 02 // BEGINNER
Key Terminology

Master the language of blockchain investigation. Sourced directly from TRM Labs' Blockchain Investigator's Flip Book and industry standards.

📚 Source
Core definitions sourced from the TRM Labs Blockchain Investigator's Flip Book — the field manual used by law enforcement worldwide — supplemented with current industry terminology.
Attribution

The process of labeling or assigning specific addresses to specific entities. For example: identifying that address bc12345 belongs to Binance, an exchange. Attribution is the primary goal of blockchain investigation.

Attribution sources include: exchange records, court filings, law enforcement databases, open-source intel, and commercial blockchain analytics platforms.

Blockchain Tracing

Tracking the source and destination of cryptocurrency transactions recorded on the currency's blockchain. The investigative goal is to identify the actual controller of an otherwise pseudo-anonymous address by following money to/from entities that hold real-world identity data.

Chain-Hopping

Moving assets between different blockchains or cryptocurrencies many times to obscure asset control. Example: sending illicit Bitcoin to a cross-chain bridge in exchange for "clean" Ether. A key obfuscation tactic investigators must track across multiple chains.

Counterparty

The party on the opposite side of a transaction. If "A" sends Bitcoin to "B," then B is A's counterparty. Identifying counterparties — especially when they are known VASPs or labeled entities — is a core tracing strategy.

Exposure

A measure of how directly proceeds may have traveled from one address to another. Direct exposure: A sent to B. Indirect exposure: A→B→C, where C has indirect exposure to A. Commercial tools quantify exposure as a percentage.

Transaction Hash (TXID)

A unique alphanumeric sequence identifying a specific transaction on a blockchain. Think of it as the receipt number for a transaction. Essential for looking up any specific transaction on a block explorer.

Example BTC TXID: 4a5e1e4baab89f3a32518a88c31bc87f618f76673e2cc77ab2127b7afdeda33b

VASP (Virtual Asset Service Provider)

A platform used to buy, sell, trade, or exchange virtual currency. VASPs often maintain KYC records that can identify the real-world controller of an address. Despite being worldwide, many VASPs comply with law enforcement requests. The term used by FATF (Financial Action Task Force) — often used interchangeably with "exchange."

KYC (Know Your Customer)

Real-world identification that an entity may collect to do business with an individual. When a VASP is served with legal process and provides KYC data, it typically includes: name, date of birth, address, government ID, email, phone, and sometimes selfies. This is often the break that cracks a case.

Mixers & Tumblers

Services that obfuscate the origins and association of funds by creating commingled pools, then sending proceeds to a destination address — breaking the linear chain of association. Examples: Tornado Cash (ETH), Wasabi Wallet (BTC). OFAC has sanctioned several mixers. Using them does not make tracing impossible.

Block Explorer

A software tool that lets investigators query and visualize blockchain data — including transaction history, address balances, and relationships. Examples: Etherscan, Blockchair, Solscan. Commercial tools like TRM Labs and Arkham Intelligence add entity labels and risk scores on top of raw explorer data.

Obfuscation Techniques

🚧 How Criminals Try to Hide Funds

Peeling Chains

Sending funds through many sequential addresses, each time moving slightly less to confuse investigators tracking the trail.

Layering

Moving funds through multiple transactions, services, and blockchains to create distance between illicit source and final destination.

Instant Exchanges

Using no-KYC swap services (like ChangeNow, FixedFloat) to exchange one currency for another without identity verification.

DeFi Protocols

Running funds through decentralized exchanges (DEXs) or lending protocols. No central operator to subpoena, but all on-chain and traceable.

Blockchain Tracing in Practice

The step-by-step methodology for tracing illicit funds — from acquiring an address to identifying a real person. Based on TRM Labs' official investigator methodology.

🗺️ The Investigative Goal

The goal of blockchain tracing is to identify the actual controller of an otherwise pseudo-anonymous address. To do so, trace blockchain transactions to find counterparty exposure with an entity or individual that can provide the identity of the beneficial controller. More simply: follow the money to or from an entity that can provide real-world identity.

The Three-Step Framework

1
Identify Illicit Conduct

Acquire the address/transaction from SAR, complaint, OSINT, or source. Confirm illicit activity is present.

2
Trace Proceeds

Follow the flow of funds through the blockchain, mapping the transaction network and identifying leverage points.

3
Seek Disruption

Serve legal process on third parties (VASPs) to obtain KYC data. Seek seizure, forfeiture, or arrest.

Detailed Tracing Steps

A
Input Transaction into Blockchain Tool
Copy/paste the transaction hash, incoming/outgoing addresses, or other information into a blockchain tracing tool (TRM Labs, Arkham, Etherscan). Best practice: acquire the address electronically to avoid transcription errors. Many tools auto-populate even partial addresses (first 7 of 34 characters).
B
Identify Obvious Leverage Points — Map the Network
Ask these questions:
  • How many transactions was the address involved in?
  • When were the most recent transactions? (Is the address still active?)
  • How much value is held in the address right now?
  • Is the address part of a larger cluster of addresses?
  • Does the address have attribution or risk flags?
  • Does it have exposure to a third party that keeps records (VASP)?
C
Identify Third Parties for Service
After plotting an address and its direct counterparties, identify which VASPs or custodians have exposure. Screenshot the graph as evidence to explain to a prosecutor or supervisor why legal process should be served on a third party such as Binance or Coinbase.
D
Serve Legal Process
Issue subpoenas or court orders to identified VASPs requesting KYC documents, subscriber info, associated emails, IP addresses, full transaction history, device IDs, and associated accounts. Use a blockchain tool's "Know Your VASP" feature to find each exchange's jurisdiction, contact info, and KYC availability.

Red Flags: Pass-Through Addresses

🚩 Identifying Suspicious Patterns

A "pass-through" address is a major red flag. Key indicators:

  • Only two transactions: one in, one out (the entire sum received was immediately sent)
  • Large sums moved in rapid succession (e.g., 39 minutes apart)
  • $0 current balance after significant value passed through
  • Part of a large cluster of addresses

A pass-through address is generally a non-economic trade — the controller paid a transaction fee for an unnecessary step — which is a red flag of illicit activity. Example from TRM's Flip Book: bc1q5spf8v3lwxcnxy9ep0mcxppy3qzdynk53vs9k7 — an address linked to a romance fraud scheme that received and immediately sent $154,814.

Terrorism Financing Example

Real Case
Al-Sadaqah Terrorism Financing (US DOJ)
A terror financing entity "Al-Sadaqah" published a Bitcoin donation address on social media. An investigator inputs the address (15K9Zj…) into a blockchain analytics tool and immediately sees the address sent Bitcoin directly to a compliant exchange. The investigator requests records from that exchange to learn who controls the "Al-Sadaqah" fundraising wallets.
Records Requested From Exchange

Account balances and full transaction history
Subscriber information (name, DOB, address)

OSINT Techniques

Open Source Intelligence (OSINT) is how investigators go from a wallet address to a real person. These are the methods used by ZachXBT and professional investigators every day.

"ZachXBT's investigative approach combines blockchain forensics with OSINT techniques... cross-referencing on-chain data with public records such as domain registrations, court filings, and social media activity. He also monitors online forums and chat groups on Telegram and Discord where cybercriminals congregate." — Wikipedia profile of ZachXBT (March 2026)

The OSINT Investigation Workflow

PHASE 1
On-Chain Pivot Points
Start with what the blockchain gives you: wallet addresses, transaction hashes, amounts, timing. Look for exchange deposits (which require KYC), NFT mints (public records), ENS names, or any address with a label. These are your first pivot points into the real world.
PHASE 2
Social Media Intelligence
Criminals brag. Search Twitter/X, Telegram, Discord, and Reddit for mentions of wallet addresses, transaction amounts, project names, or usernames. ZachXBT famously identified the $243M BTC theft suspects partly through their social media spending patterns (luxury purchases, nightclub appearances).
PHASE 3
Username & Email Pivots
A username used in one context often reappears elsewhere. Use OSINT Industries, Sherlock, and IntelX to find all accounts associated with a username, email, or phone number across hundreds of platforms. Breach databases (Snusbase, LeakPeek) may reveal passwords, IPs, or real names.
PHASE 4
Domain & Infrastructure
WHOIS records, SSL certificate history, Shodan/Censys scans, and domain registration data can link a scam website to a real identity or to other criminal infrastructure. The Lazarus Group registered bybit-assessment[.]com using the email trevorgreer9312@gmail.com hours before the $1.5B hack — a traceable footprint.
PHASE 5
Physical Evidence Correlation
Instagram story locations, timestamps on photos (EXIF data), check-ins, flight patterns, and purchases can place a suspect in a location. ZachXBT confirmed a suspect's location in Abbotsford, British Columbia, BC, through OSINT performed on story posts. OSINT on Lazarus Group's Khinkis revealed he travels to Southeast Asia and Australia.
PHASE 6
Archive & Document Everything
Use Wayback Machine and Archive.today to preserve evidence before suspects delete it. Screenshot with timestamps. Organize everything in Obsidian or a similar link-chart tool. Your documentation becomes the chain of custody.

Timing Analysis

⏱️ Using Timing to Correlate Identities

One powerful OSINT technique is timing analysis: matching the exact time of a blockchain transaction to a social media post, login event, or real-world activity. ZachXBT used timing analysis to match Bitcoin transfers to specific Tron wallet addresses in the Khinkis ransomware investigation — cross-referencing blockchain timestamps against exchange withdrawal times.

Key timing red flags:

  • Funds moving within minutes of a hack announcement
  • Transactions at unusual hours (3AM local time) indicating specific timezone
  • Rapid cascading transactions suggesting automated laundering scripts
  • Dormant addresses suddenly activating after months — often a sign criminal is cashing out

Address Clustering

🕸️ Identifying Wallet Networks

One person typically controls many wallet addresses. Address clustering is the process of grouping these together using heuristics:

  • Common Input Ownership (CIUA): If two addresses both sign inputs in the same Bitcoin transaction, they're likely controlled by the same entity — because you need the private key for each input.
  • Change Address Detection: Bitcoin transactions often send one output to the recipient and one "change" output back to the sender. Identifying change addresses reveals more addresses controlled by the same person.
  • Reuse patterns: Addresses that receive funds from the same counterparty, or send funds together repeatedly, often belong to the same cluster.

Commercial tools like TRM Labs and Arkham automatically cluster addresses. On Ethereum, all addresses from the same private key are the same address — simpler for clustering but still requires OSINT to identify the real person.

Crypto Scams & Fraud

Understanding the major categories of crypto fraud — how they work, how they're identified, and how investigations unfold.

Fraud Typologies

💔 Romance Scams (Pig Butchering / SHA ZHU PAN)

The most financially devastating fraud category. Criminals build a romantic or investment relationship online (often months), then convince victims to invest in a fake crypto platform. When the victim tries to withdraw, they're told they need to pay more fees. Also called "Sha Zhu Pan" (killing pig scam) — originating from Southeast Asia.

Key indicators: Fake exchange websites, pressure tactics, "guaranteed returns," unreachable withdrawal, fake customer support numbers.

Investigation: Trace victim funds through wallet addresses. Look for common deposit addresses across multiple victims (multiple pigs, same sty). Often flows to TRON USDT wallets before cashing out.

High Volume
State-Sponsored (SE Asia)
🧸 Rug Pulls

Developers create a token, build hype, attract investors, then suddenly drain the liquidity pool and abandon the project. Common on DEX platforms (Uniswap, PancakeSwap). Variants include "soft rugs" (slow abandonment) and "hard rugs" (immediate drain).

Red flags: Anonymous team, no audit, locked liquidity expiring soon, excessive dev wallet allocation, mint function retained, copied whitepaper.

Investigation: On-chain analysis of smart contract; trace LP removal to CEX deposits; check deployer wallet history for previous rugs.

🎭 Social Engineering / Impersonation Scams

Criminals impersonate exchange support teams, celebrities, or officials to steal credentials or convince victims to send funds. A major 2025 trend: Coinbase impersonation scams using data from a May 2025 insider breach. Attackers hired overseas support agents to steal customer data, then impersonated "Elite Support" to redirect funds.

Case: ZachXBT exposed "Haby" (Abbotsford, BC) — a scammer who stole $2M+ by calling Coinbase users claiming to be support, directing them to "secure vaults" he controlled. Identified via timing analysis and social media OSINT on his Instagram stories.

📈 Pump and Dump

Coordinated buying of a low-cap token to inflate its price, followed by mass selling at peak. Often coordinated in private Telegram/Discord groups. The 2025 political memecoin era saw multiple high-profile pump-and-dump schemes targeting retail investors.

Investigation: Analyze on-chain token distribution; identify wallet clusters that coordinated buys; trace founders' wallets selling at peak.

Investigation Framework for Fraud Cases

1
Victim Report Triage
Collect victim's wallet address, the address they sent funds to, screenshots of the scam platform, and any communications. Get the exact transaction hash. Is the scammer wallet still active?
2
Address Clustering & Fund Flow
Plot the scammer's receiving address. Map outgoing transactions. Look for: (a) common deposit addresses shared across multiple victims, (b) exchanges where funds land, (c) conversion to stablecoins (USDT/USDC).
3
OSINT on Scammer Identity
Research scam website WHOIS, social media accounts linked to the scam, and any usernames in communications. Check breach databases for the email used in the scam platform registration.
4
VASP Identification & Legal Process
Identify which exchange(s) received the funds. Issue legal process (subpoena/court order) for KYC, transaction records, IP addresses, and device IDs.

Exchange Hacks & DeFi Exploits

How crypto exchanges are hacked, how forensic investigators respond in real time, and what the Bybit hack reveals about the state of blockchain forensics in 2025.

LANDMARK CASE
Bybit Exchange Hack — The $1.5 Billion Heist
$1.5 BILLION
📅 DATE: February 21, 2025
🎯 TARGET: Bybit (Dubai-based exchange)
🕵️ ATTRIBUTION: Lazarus Group / TraderTraitor (DPRK)
💰 STOLEN: 401,347 ETH (~$1.5B)
The largest single cryptocurrency theft in history. Hackers exploited a vulnerability in Bybit's multi-signature cold wallet infrastructure by compromising a developer machine at Safe{Wallet} — Bybit's third-party transaction signing platform. Malicious JavaScript was injected into the Safe{Wallet} UI on February 19, 2025 at 15:29:25 UTC, specifically designed to activate only during Bybit transactions, silently redirecting the cold-to-warm wallet transfer.
Within 48 hours, ZachXBT identified Lazarus Group attribution by linking Bybit outflows to Ethereum addresses previously used in the Phemex, BingX, and Poloniex hacks. This was confirmed by Elliptic, TRM Labs, and the FBI, who released a formal PSA with 51 Ethereum addresses used in laundering.

Submitted evidence to Arkham Intelligence within hours

Cycle through DEXs and instant exchanges (no KYC)

The Attribution Process

🔬 How Investigators Attribute State-Sponsored Attacks

Lazarus Group attribution wasn't guesswork. Lazarus has a documented fingerprint: specific wallet clustering behaviors, timing patterns, and mixing service preferences. By comparing Bybit outflows against these known signatures, investigators established linkage with high confidence before any official statements.

  • Pattern matching: Compare new hack addresses against a reference database of known Lazarus Group addresses
  • Infrastructure overlap: The domain bybit-assessment[.]com was registered using email trevorgreer9312@gmail.com — previously used in Lazarus Group's "Contagious Interview" campaign
  • Behavioral fingerprinting: Lazarus willingly sacrifices value (accepts losses) to launder funds — a unique state-actor behavioral pattern
  • Historical graph comparison: Arkham had already documented Lazarus Group's prior operations — investigators weren't starting from zero

DeFi Exploit Investigation

⚡ Flash Loan & Smart Contract Exploits

DeFi protocols are vulnerable to smart contract exploits, flash loan attacks, and oracle manipulation. Unlike exchange hacks, DeFi exploits are entirely on-chain — every step of the attack is publicly visible and traceable.

Investigation process for DeFi exploits:

  • Identify the exploit transaction (usually a massive, unusual trade or balance change)
  • Trace the attacker's wallet backward to find their funding source (often a Tornado Cash or exchange deposit)
  • Forward-trace fund flow from exploit profits to mixing or exchange deposits
  • Analyze the attacker's prior on-chain behavior for patterns or reuse
  • Search for the smart contract that executed the exploit — its deployer wallet is often investigable

Ransomware & Nation-State Threats

Ransomware investigations and advanced persistent threat (APT) tracking — where cybercrime meets geopolitics and blockchain forensics meets intelligence work.

💀 Ransomware Crypto Payments

Ransomware groups demand payment in cryptocurrency (primarily Bitcoin, sometimes Monero) as a condition of restoring access to encrypted systems. Every payment creates an on-chain record. Investigators track these payments from victim wallets to laundering infrastructure.

Typical ransomware payment flow:

STAGE 1
Ransom Payment
Victim sends BTC/XMR to attacker-controlled address. Payment address is unique per victim. Investigators document the TXID and amounts.
STAGE 2
Mixing / Consolidation

Legal Process & VASP Cooperation

How to translate blockchain evidence into legal action — subpoenas, court orders, international cooperation, and working with VASPs. Based on TRM Labs' Flip Book and DOJ guidelines.

📌 Source
This section incorporates guidance from the TRM Labs Blockchain Investigator's Flip Book, U.S. DOJ Asset Forfeiture Program documentation, and current law enforcement best practices.

Issuing Legal Process to VASPs

⚖️ What You Can Request

When issuing legal process (subpoena, court order, MLA request) to a VASP, an investigator may obtain:

  • KYC documents — government ID, selfies, address
  • Subscriber information — name, DOB, registration date
  • Email address associations
  • IP address logs — login history with timestamps
  • Full transaction history — all deposits/withdrawals
  • Device ID (IMEI) — phone fingerprints
  • Account balance — current holdings
  • Associated accounts — linked family/business accounts

Best practice: request an omnibus "all records associated with the account and registrant" to capture anything you haven't specifically anticipated.

Using "Know Your VASP" Tools

🗂️ Finding How and Where to Serve Process

TRM Labs' "Know Your VASP" database (and similar tools) includes: exchange location/jurisdiction, legal contact information, KYC availability, and financial profile. This is critical because:

  • Many VASPs operate across multiple jurisdictions
  • Some claim no geographic domicile but still comply with legal requests
  • VASPs differ significantly in response time and KYC quality
  • Understanding jurisdiction determines what legal instrument to use

Key principle: Many VASPs will accept data requests if legal process has been followed, regardless of jurisdiction, provided it is lawful and clearly communicated.

Downstream Investigative Actions

1
Email Search Warrants
Most VASPs send transaction confirmation emails. The email provider (Google, Outlook, etc.) may be served with a search warrant for email content — establishing that the account holder was aware of and participated in transactions. This independently corroborates the blockchain evidence.
2
IP Address Lookups & Geolocation
IP addresses from VASP subpoena returns can be cross-referenced with Spur.us to determine if they are residential, VPN, or TOR. Residential IPs can lead to ISP subpoenas for subscriber identity (name and address of the account holder).
3
Asset Freeze Requests
If funds remain on a VASP at the time of legal process, request an emergency freeze simultaneous with the data request. Speed is critical — funds may be withdrawn between subpoena and compliance. Some VASPs have direct freeze hotlines for law enforcement.
4
International Cooperation (MLA Treaties)
For VASPs in foreign jurisdictions, use Mutual Legal Assistance Treaties (MLATs) or direct law enforcement cooperation channels (FBI, Europol, INTERPOL). Some countries have streamlined crypto-specific cooperation agreements. Maintain contact with counterparts in major VASP jurisdictions (Cayman Islands, Malta, Seychelles, etc.).
🚨 Straw Identities Warning
The named account holder on a VASP could be: (1) the actual subject, (2) a straw identity (friend/family used to open account), (3) a stolen identity, or (4) a professional money launderer. Always investigate deeper — the KYC return is a lead, not a conclusion.

Asset Seizure & Forfeiture

The legal and practical mechanics of seizing, holding, and forfeiting cryptocurrency — directly from the TRM Labs Flip Book and DOJ guidelines.

🔐 Why Seize Crypto Assets?

Asset forfeiture serves multiple purposes:

  • Punish criminal behavior and remove ill-gotten gains
  • Return assets to victims of the crime
  • Deter future illegal activity by removing the profit motive
  • Remove tools and facilities that facilitate illegal behavior
  • Disrupt criminal organizations' financial infrastructure
  • Protect the community from ongoing criminal activity

Theories of Forfeiture

📜 Legal Frameworks

To seize and forfeit assets, the government must rely on a forfeiture statute. Statutes vary in complexity:

  • Simple statutes: New York Penal Law Article 480 — "when any person is convicted of a felony offense, property constituting the proceeds and instrumentalities are subject to forfeiture."
  • Complex statutes: US federal North Korea financing civil forfeiture (18 USC 981(a)(1)(I)) — requires specific nexus to DPRK activities.
  • Civil forfeiture: Action against the property itself, not the person — can proceed even without a criminal conviction in some jurisdictions.

Best practice: Work with a prosecuting attorney on forfeiture strategy concurrent with the investigation — not as an afterthought.

Custody Options for Seized Assets

Online Software Wallet

Pros: Quick, cheap, holds wide variety of assets including thinly traded currencies.

Cons: Higher risk of theft through intrusions into wallet software. Not recommended for large seizures.

Hardware Wallet

Pros: More secure than software wallets. Industry standard for most seizures.

Cons: Upfront purchase cost. May not support all virtual currencies (especially thinly traded).

VASP Custody

Pros: Efficient for transferring assets, especially when seizing from that VASP.

Cons: Assets may be commingled, not segregated. VASPs have been hacked. Requires confidentiality safeguards.

Third-Party Custodian

Pros: Expert custody, strong security, institutional-grade storage.

Cons: Transfer friction. Third-party custodians may have their own limits and costs.

The Five Pre-Seizure Considerations

A
Custody Type
What wallet/custodian will the agency use? Online wallet, hardware wallet, VASP account, or third-party custodian? Match security level to asset value and nature.
B
Access Control
Who at the agency will have access? Implement need-to-know access. Track every access event. Avoid single point of failure. Ensure business continuity if key personnel leave.
C
Asset Form
Accept crypto as-is or convert to fiat equivalent? Consider price volatility. Thinly-traded currencies may be difficult to liquidate later. Work with prosecutors on timing of liquidation.
D
Contingencies
Virtual currencies are nascent. Software becomes obsolete. VASPs get hacked. New currencies fork. Have a "living document" policy for seizing and forfeiting. Review annually.
E
Liquidation Plan
Use a VASP for market-rate sale, a third-party auction, or return as-is? Each has tradeoffs. Returning to fraud victims unfamiliar with crypto requires careful planning. VASPs provide market-rate but require vetting.

QUESTION 01 / BEGINNER A wallet address that receives a large sum and immediately sends the entire amount out within an hour, then shows a $0 balance — what should an investigator suspect?
A) A legitimate retail investor who quickly changed their mind
B) A "pass-through" address used to layer illicit funds
C) A cold wallet moving to a hardware device
D) Normal exchange behavior
✅ Correct — B: A pass-through address is a major red flag. The controller paid a transaction fee for an unnecessary movement of funds, which is generally a non-economic trade indicating illicit activity. Per TRM Labs' Flip Book, this pattern suggests the address is being used to create distance between the source and destination of funds.
QUESTION 02 / INTERMEDIATE An investigator traces illicit Bitcoin to a Binance deposit address. What is the MOST valuable legal process to serve on Binance?
A) Request only the KYC documents (government ID)
B) Request only the transaction history
C) Omnibus request: all records including KYC, IP logs, email, device IDs, transaction history, and associated accounts — plus an emergency freeze
D) No need to serve — the blockchain data is sufficient for prosecution
✅ Correct — C: TRM Labs' Flip Book recommends an omnibus "all records associated with the account and registrant" request. This captures KYC documents, subscriber info, email associations, IP address logs, full transaction history, device IDs, and associated accounts. The simultaneous emergency freeze is critical — funds may be withdrawn during the compliance window.
QUESTION 03 / INTERMEDIATE How did ZachXBT attribute the $1.5B Bybit hack to Lazarus Group so quickly (within hours)?
A) He accessed private law enforcement databases
B) He found WHOIS records showing North Korean addresses
C) He matched Bybit outflows to Ethereum addresses previously linked to Lazarus Group in prior hacks (Phemex, BingX, Poloniex)
D) North Korea publicly claimed responsibility
✅ Correct — C: ZachXBT identified address overlap — some Bybit outflow wallets were the same Ethereum addresses previously used in Phemex, BingX, and Poloniex hacks already attributed to Lazarus Group. Investigators weren't starting from zero — they had a reference dataset of Lazarus behavioral fingerprints and prior addresses. This pre-existing intelligence was the key.
QUESTION 04 / BEGINNER What does "KYC" stand for, and why does it matter for blockchain investigators?
A) Key Your Currency — a wallet encryption method
B) Keep Your Credentials — an exchange password policy
C) Know Your Customer — real-world identification that exchanges collect; when obtained via legal process, it links a wallet address to a real person
D) Keep Your Coins — a cold storage strategy
✅ Correct — C: KYC (Know Your Customer) is the real-world identification — government ID, selfie, address, date of birth — that exchanges collect to comply with anti-money laundering regulations. When an investigator traces funds to an exchange and serves legal process for KYC records, this is often the breakthrough that identifies the real person controlling an otherwise anonymous wallet.
QUESTION 05 / ADVANCED An investigator finds that two Bitcoin addresses both appear as inputs in the same transaction. What does this suggest under common input ownership heuristics?
A) The addresses are on the same exchange
B) Both addresses are likely controlled by the same entity (same private key holder), making them part of the same cluster
C) The transaction is definitely illicit
D) The addresses are CoinJoin outputs
✅ Correct — B: Common Input Ownership (CIUA) is the foundational Bitcoin address clustering heuristic. If two addresses both sign inputs in the same Bitcoin transaction, the same entity must hold both private keys (since you need the private key to sign). This means both addresses are almost certainly controlled by the same person — a powerful method for expanding the scope of an investigation across multiple wallets.
QUESTION 06 / INTERMEDIATE What is "chain-hopping" and why is it a challenge for investigators?
A) Rapidly moving funds between wallets on the same blockchain
B) Splitting a large transaction into many small ones
C) Moving assets between different blockchains (e.g., BTC→ETH→TRON) through bridges, forcing investigators to track across multiple chains and tool sets
D) Staking crypto on multiple protocols simultaneously
✅ Correct — C: Chain-hopping involves moving assets between different blockchains (e.g., sending illicit Bitcoin to a bridge in exchange for "clean" Ether) many times to obfuscate asset control. It forces investigators to use different block explorers and bridge-tracking tools (Pulsy, Socketscan) for each chain, significantly complicating the investigation. This is exactly the technique the Lazarus Group used after the Bybit hack.

Glossary & Key Resources

Glossary

🔍
Address Clustering
The process of grouping multiple blockchain addresses that are likely controlled by the same entity, using heuristics like common input ownership.
APT (Advanced Persistent Threat)
A sophisticated, state-sponsored threat actor that conducts long-term targeted attacks. Lazarus Group (North Korea) is an example. APTs often use crypto for financing and laundering.
Attribution
Assigning a blockchain address to a specific real-world entity. The primary goal of blockchain investigation.
Block Explorer
A tool for querying blockchain data. Examples: Etherscan (ETH), Blockchair (BTC), Solscan (SOL).
Blockchain
A publicly available, immutable ledger of completed transactions between cryptocurrency addresses.
Blockchain Tracing
Tracking the source and destination of cryptocurrency transactions recorded on a blockchain.
Chain-Hopping
Moving assets between different blockchains multiple times to obscure the trail. E.g., BTC→ETH→TRON via bridges.
Change Address
In Bitcoin, the address receiving the "change" portion of a transaction back to the sender. Identifying change addresses reveals more wallets controlled by the same entity.
Cold Wallet
A cryptocurrency wallet stored offline, disconnected from the internet. Considered more secure but became a target in the Bybit hack via supply chain compromise.
Counterparty
The party on the opposite side of a transaction. If A sends to B, B is A's counterparty.
DEX (Decentralized Exchange)
A cryptocurrency exchange that operates without a central authority or KYC requirements. Funds can be swapped without identity verification, complicating investigations.
DeFi (Decentralized Finance)
Financial services built on blockchain smart contracts with no central operator. Increasingly used for money laundering due to lack of KYC.
Direct Exposure
When an address directly transacted with an illicit source (one hop away).
EXIF Data
Metadata embedded in photos, often including GPS coordinates, device info, and timestamps. Can reveal suspect location.
Exposure
A measure of how directly proceeds have traveled from one address to another. Commercial tools quantify this as a percentage.
FATF
Financial Action Task Force. International body setting AML/CFT standards. Coined the term "VASP."
Flash Loan Attack
A DeFi exploit using uncollateralized instant loans to manipulate prices and drain protocols in a single transaction.
Fork
When a blockchain splits into two versions (e.g., Bitcoin / Bitcoin Cash). Investigators must know which chain evidence is on.
Hot Wallet
A cryptocurrency wallet connected to the internet. More convenient but more vulnerable to hacking.
Indirect Exposure
When an address received funds that passed through an intermediate address from an illicit source (two or more hops away).
Instant Exchange
A service for swapping one cryptocurrency for another with no KYC requirements. E.g., ChangeNow, FixedFloat. Frequently used for money laundering.
KYC (Know Your Customer)
Real-world identification that exchanges collect — government ID, selfie, address. The key data investigators request via legal process.
Layer 2
Blockchain networks built on top of a base layer (e.g., tokens on Ethereum). Each requires different investigation tools.
Lazarus Group
North Korea's elite state-sponsored hacking unit. Stole $2B+ in crypto in 2025 alone, including the $1.5B Bybit hack.
Mempool
The queue of unconfirmed transactions waiting to be included in the next block. Investigators can monitor the mempool for real-time transaction alerts.
Mixer / Tumbler
Services that pool and redistribute crypto to break the chain of association. Examples: Tornado Cash (ETH), Wasabi Wallet (BTC). OFAC has sanctioned several.
MLAT
Mutual Legal Assistance Treaty. International agreement enabling legal cooperation between countries, used for obtaining evidence from foreign-jurisdiction VASPs.
Multisig Wallet
A wallet requiring multiple private key signatures to authorize a transaction. Used for exchange security but exploited in Bybit and WazirX hacks via supply chain attacks.
OFAC
U.S. Office of Foreign Assets Control. Sanctions cryptocurrency addresses and mixers linked to sanctioned entities (e.g., Lazarus Group, Tornado Cash).
OSINT
Open Source Intelligence. Information gathered from publicly available sources — social media, domain registrations, court filings, etc. Combined with on-chain data for full investigative picture.
OTC Broker
Over-the-counter broker facilitating large cryptocurrency trades outside of exchanges. Sometimes used for money laundering (e.g., the Khinkis ransomware case).
Pass-Through Address
An address that receives and immediately sends all received funds, leaving a $0 balance. A major red flag for illicit activity.
Peeling Chain
Sending funds through many sequential addresses, each time peeling off a small amount, to confuse investigators following the trail.
Private Key
The secret cryptographic key that controls a wallet. Whoever has the private key controls the funds. Never share it.
Pseudonymous
Not fully anonymous. Blockchain addresses don't contain names, but behavioral patterns and links to real-world identity make them investigable.
Rug Pull
A scam where developers drain a token's liquidity pool and abandon the project after attracting investor funds.
SAR (Suspicious Activity Report)
Report filed by financial institutions when suspicious transactions are detected. A common source of investigative leads for crypto cases.
Smart Contract
Self-executing code on a blockchain that automatically enforces the terms of an agreement. DeFi protocols run on smart contracts; exploits target their logic.
Straw Identity
A person used to open an account on behalf of the real criminal to provide a false identity layer. Common in money laundering operations.
Supply Chain Attack
Compromising a target indirectly by attacking a trusted third-party supplier (e.g., the Safe{Wallet} developer machine in the Bybit hack).
Timing Analysis
Cross-referencing blockchain transaction timestamps against external events (social media, login logs) to correlate identities or behaviors.
Token
A virtual currency asset, often running on another blockchain's infrastructure (e.g., USDT is an ERC-20 token on Ethereum).
Tornado Cash
An Ethereum-based mixing protocol. Sanctioned by OFAC in 2022. Lazarus Group's preferred ETH mixing tool. Developer Roman Storm convicted in 2024.
Transaction Hash (TXID)
The unique identifier for a specific blockchain transaction. Used to look up exact transactions on block explorers.
VASP
Virtual Asset Service Provider. Any platform for buying, selling, or trading crypto. Most hold KYC data investigators can obtain via legal process.
Wallet
A software or hardware tool for storing cryptocurrency. Technically stores the private key, not the currency itself (funds are on-chain).
