Pillar 1: Cybersecurity Tradecraft

🔬 DFIR & Malware Analysis Extracting C2 & Wallet Artifacts ›

Digital Forensics and Incident Response (DFIR) is the first line of evidence collection. For e-crime investigators, the key is extracting crypto-relevant artifacts from malware payloads — not just understanding what the malware did, but identifying the financial infrastructure behind it.

  • Ransomware payloads contain hardcoded Bitcoin addresses and wallet references in their ransom notes or configuration files. These are extracted via static analysis (Ghidra, IDA Pro) or memory forensics (Volatility).
  • Infostealer logs (recovered via Cavalier, Hudson Rock) capture saved browser credentials, crypto wallet seeds, and even keylogged exchange login sessions — revealing the attacker's own crypto infrastructure.
  • C2 infrastructure often reuses the same IP ranges or domains across campaigns, creating pivot points that connect a wallet address to a known threat actor group via network intelligence.
  • The Bybit breakthrough: Hudson Rock identified a Safe{Wallet} developer machine infected with infostealer malware weeks before the $1.5B hack — the malware log was the missing link between Lazarus Group's social engineering campaign and the eventual exploit.
💡 Analyst Tip
When analyzing ransomware, always extract the full ransom note as a text artifact. Threat actors frequently reuse wallet addresses across victims — a single address can link dozens of separate victim payments into a single financial trail.
🎯 Threat Hunting & IoC Development Proactive On-Chain & Cloud Detection ›

Threat hunting moves the investigator from reactive to proactive. For crypto investigations, this means developing on-chain Indicators of Compromise (IoCs) — behavioral signatures that flag illicit activity before a victim reports it.

  • On-chain IoCs: Wallet addresses linked to known threat actors (FBI PSA lists), bridge contracts associated with prior hacks, mixer contract addresses, and instant-exchange deposit addresses.
  • Cloud IoCs: Unusual API calls to exchange endpoints, credential stuffing patterns against exchange login pages, and abnormal withdrawal request volumes.
  • Cross-domain correlation: Matching network logs (firewall, DNS, VPN) against blockchain timestamps to identify the precise moment a compromise led to a crypto transfer.
  • Dune Analytics dashboards can be built to continuously monitor specific wallet clusters for activity — automating the hunt across millions of on-chain events.
📡 Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI) Adversary Profiling & TTP Mapping ›

CTI is the structured discipline of understanding, documenting, and anticipating adversary behavior. For e-crime investigators, CTI converts raw attribution into actionable intelligence that predicts the next strike.

  • MITRE ATT&CK: Map every observed behavior to a TTP (Tactic, Technique, Procedure). Lazarus Group's "Contagious Interview" campaign maps to T1566 (Phishing) + T1204 (User Execution) + T1041 (Exfiltration). Once mapped, defenders can detect future intrusions using the same pattern.
  • Diamond Model: Structure adversary intelligence around four vertices — Adversary, Infrastructure, Capability, and Victim. Useful for connecting multiple hack incidents to a single threat actor group.
  • Behavioral fingerprinting: Lazarus Group's documented signature includes accepting value losses to expedite laundering (an economic behavior unique to state-sponsored actors with infinite patience). This fingerprint enabled ZachXBT's rapid Bybit attribution.
  • Threat feeds: CISA KEV, FBI PSA wallet lists, OFAC SDN list crypto addresses, and commercial feeds (Recorded Future, Mandiant) provide the reference database for CTI-driven attribution.

Pillar 2: Financial Crime Investigation

💰 FININT & OSINT Convergence From Wallet to Real-World Identity ›

Financial Intelligence (FININT) converges blockchain data with open-source intelligence to build a complete picture of the adversary. No single source is sufficient — the power comes from the intersection of multiple independent evidence streams.

  • On-chain → VASP: Tracing funds to an exchange creates a legal chokepoint where KYC data can be obtained.
  • On-chain → social media: Blockchain timestamps matched against bragging posts, Instagram story locations, and Discord activity — the technique that identified the $243M BTC theft suspects.
  • On-chain → domain registrations: The Bybit hack's attribution trail included a domain registered with a Gmail address previously used in Lazarus Group's "Contagious Interview" campaign.
  • Breach data: Infostealer logs and breach databases (IntelX, Snusbase) often reveal the real email, IP, and password hash behind a pseudonymous crypto wallet — transforming a chain address into a person.
⚖️ AML, PCMLTFA & Regulatory Frameworks Canadian & International Compliance ›

Findings must be legally defensible to drive consequences. Investigators must navigate both Canadian and international regulatory frameworks when building cases that cross borders — as virtually all crypto cases do.

  • PCMLTFA (Canada): The Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act requires Canadian VASPs to collect and report suspicious transactions. FINTRAC (Canada's FIU) receives SARs that can initiate or supplement crypto investigations.
  • FATF Travel Rule: Requires VASPs to share originator/beneficiary data for transfers above thresholds. Non-compliant exchanges are investigative chokepoints — they can be served via MLAT.
  • OFAC sanctions: Any interaction with OFAC-sanctioned wallets (Lazarus Group, Tornado Cash) creates legal exposure. Tracking exposure percentage is a key part of presenting evidence to prosecutors.
  • Chain of custody: Every on-chain screenshot, export, and archived web page must be timestamped and documented to be admissible. Commercial blockchain analytics tools (TRM Labs, Chainalysis) produce court-ready reports with embedded timestamps and entity labels.
🔐 Fraud Investigation & Asset Recovery From Evidence to Seizure ›

The ultimate goal of financial crime investigation is disruption — freezing assets before they vanish, securing KYC records that identify perpetrators, and building the case for criminal prosecution or civil forfeiture.

  • Peeling chain tracking: Following funds through sequential wallets requires documenting each hop with its TXID, amount, timestamp, and any attribution label — building a complete financial trail for prosecutors.
  • Bridge analysis: Cross-chain moves (BTC→AVAX, ETH→TRON) are traced using specialist tools (Pulsy, Socketscan). Each bridge transaction creates a new investigable record on a different chain.
  • Emergency freeze: Coordinating with exchange compliance teams and law enforcement to freeze funds before the criminal's withdrawal window closes. Speed is the defining factor — funds can move within minutes of a hack.
  • Civil forfeiture: In some jurisdictions, assets can be seized even without a criminal conviction. The blockchain evidence itself can establish the nexus between stolen funds and specific wallets.

Blockchain Foundations

You cannot investigate what you don't understand. This module builds foundational knowledge of how blockchains work and why they're investigable.

⛓️ What is a Blockchain?

A blockchain is a publicly available, immutable ledger of every transaction ever made on that network. Think of it like a global spreadsheet anyone can read, but no one can delete rows from.

When Person A sends Bitcoin to Person B, that transaction is cryptographically verified by thousands of computers and permanently written into a "block" — which is chained to every previous block. This transparency is what makes blockchain investigation possible. Unlike traditional financial crime where investigators need warrants to access bank records, on-chain data is publicly visible to everyone.

How Transactions Work

CLICK EACH STEP TO EXPAND

🔑 Step 1 — Wallet Creation Public Key / Private Key ›

A user creates a crypto wallet, which generates a private key (a secret cryptographic password) and a public address (like a bank account number). The address is pseudonymous — it doesn't contain the user's name, but it is permanently traceable on the blockchain.

Whoever controls the private key controls the funds. This is why investigators focus on identifying the controller of an address, not just the address itself — the private key may be held by a different person than the one named in KYC records.

📡 Step 2 — Transaction Broadcast Signed & Submitted to Network ›

When sending crypto, the sender broadcasts a signed transaction to the network: "I'm sending 0.5 BTC from address 1ABC… to address 3XYZ…." This is signed with the private key, cryptographically proving the sender controls the funds.

This signature is the on-chain equivalent of a handwritten signature on a bank transfer — and it cannot be forged. For investigators, this means every transaction is authenticated evidence of the controller's intent.

✅ Steps 3–5 — Verification, Confirmation & Public Record Immutable & Permanent ›

Network nodes verify the transaction is valid (the sender has the funds, the signature is correct) and add it to the mempool (pending queue). Miners or validators bundle pending transactions into a block and add it to the chain.

On Bitcoin, this takes ~10 minutes. On Ethereum, ~12 seconds. Once confirmed, the transaction is permanent and irreversible — a core principle investigators rely on. The transaction is now visible to anyone using a block explorer: sender address, recipient address, amount, timestamp, and transaction hash (ID).

🔎 The Investigator's Advantage — 5 Reasons Why Crypto Isn't Anonymous ›
  • Every transaction is public — unlike cash, there's a permanent, searchable audit trail
  • Addresses cluster together — one person typically controls multiple addresses with identifiable behavioral patterns (Common Input Ownership heuristic)
  • Exchanges require KYC — eventually, criminals must cash out to real money, requiring identity verification at VASPs

Core Investigation Terms

🏷️ Attribution The Primary Goal ›

The process of labeling or assigning specific blockchain addresses to specific entities — and ultimately, to specific people. Attribution sources include exchange records, court filings, law enforcement databases, open-source intel, and commercial blockchain analytics platforms.

FACT distinction: The FACT Framework separates technical identification (which device/account acted?) from investigative attribution (which person acted?). Identifying that address 0x123… belongs to Binance is technical identification. Linking that account to a named individual via KYC is attribution.

🏦 VASP / KYC / Counterparty Legal Leverage Points ›

VASP (Virtual Asset Service Provider): Any platform used to buy, sell, trade, or exchange virtual currency. VASPs often maintain KYC records that can identify the real-world controller of an address. The FATF term — used interchangeably with "exchange."

KYC (Know Your Customer): Real-world identification an entity may collect — government ID, selfie, address, date of birth. When a VASP is served with legal process and provides KYC data, this is often the case-breaking moment that links a wallet to a person.

Counterparty: The party on the opposite side of a transaction. Identifying counterparties — especially when they are known VASPs or labeled entities — is a core tracing strategy. If a wallet's counterparty is Coinbase, that's a legal chokepoint.

🚧 Obfuscation Techniques Chain-Hopping, Mixers, Peeling Chains ›

Chain-Hopping: Moving assets between different blockchains (e.g., BTC→ETH→TRON via bridges) many times to obscure asset control. Forces investigators to use different block explorers and bridge-tracking tools for each chain. The Lazarus Group's primary post-hack laundering technique.

Mixers & Tumblers: Services that pool and redistribute crypto to break the chain of association. Examples: Tornado Cash (ETH), Wasabi Wallet (BTC). OFAC has sanctioned several mixers. Using them does not make tracing impossible — exposure analysis still identifies tainted funds entering and exiting.

Peeling Chains: Sending funds through many sequential addresses, each time peeling off a slightly smaller amount, to confuse investigators following the trail.

Instant Exchanges: No-KYC swap services (ChangeNow, FixedFloat) used to exchange one currency for another without identity verification. Frequent layering stop. No subpoena target — but the on-chain swap record is still visible.

DeFi Protocols: Running funds through decentralized exchanges or lending protocols. No central operator to subpoena, but all transactions are on-chain and fully traceable.

📊 Exposure, Pass-Through & Transaction Hash Core Investigative Concepts ›

Exposure: A measure of how directly illicit proceeds traveled from one address to another. Direct: A sent to B. Indirect: A→B→C, where C has indirect exposure to A. Commercial tools quantify exposure as a percentage — over 10–15% is typically flagged as significant.

Pass-Through Address: An address that receives a large sum and immediately sends the entire amount out, leaving a $0 balance. A major red flag for illicit layering. The controller paid a transaction fee for a non-economic step — a classic sign of money laundering infrastructure.

Transaction Hash (TXID): A unique alphanumeric identifier for a specific blockchain transaction — the receipt number. Essential for looking up any specific transaction on a block explorer. Example BTC TXID: 4a5e1e4baab89f3a32518a88c31bc87f618f76673e2cc77ab2127b7afdeda33b

Blockchain Tracing in Practice

The step-by-step methodology for tracing illicit funds — from acquiring an address to identifying a real person. Based on TRM Labs' official investigator methodology.

🗺️ The Investigative Goal

The goal of blockchain tracing is to identify the actual controller of an otherwise pseudo-anonymous address. Trace blockchain transactions to find counterparty exposure with an entity or individual that can provide real-world identity. More simply: follow the money to or from an entity that keeps records.

The Three-Step Framework

1
Identify Illicit Conduct

Acquire the address/transaction from SAR, complaint, OSINT, or source. Confirm illicit activity is present.

2
Trace Proceeds

Follow the flow of funds through the blockchain, mapping the transaction network and identifying leverage points.

3
Seek Disruption

Serve legal process on third parties (VASPs) to obtain KYC data. Seek seizure, forfeiture, or arrest.

Detailed Tracing Steps

A Input Transaction into Blockchain Tool First Step ›

Copy/paste the transaction hash, incoming/outgoing addresses, or other information into a blockchain tracing tool (TRM Labs, Arkham, Etherscan). Best practice: acquire the address electronically to avoid transcription errors. Many tools auto-populate even partial addresses (first 7 of 34 characters).

Confirm the blockchain first — an Ethereum-format address starting with 0x could be on Ethereum, BNB Chain, Polygon, Avalanche, or dozens of other EVM chains. The wrong explorer will return empty results.

B Map the Network — Identify Leverage Points Key Questions to Ask ›

Once the address is loaded in a tool, conduct a structured assessment by answering these questions:

  • How many transactions was the address involved in? (A single two-transaction address is more suspicious than a high-volume address)
  • When were the most recent transactions? Is the address still active?
  • How much value is currently held in the address?
  • Is the address part of a larger cluster? Does it appear in a known entity's network?
  • Does the address have attribution labels or risk flags in the tool?
  • Does it have direct or indirect exposure to a VASP that keeps KYC records?

Pass-through red flag: Only two transactions, large sums moved within minutes, $0 current balance — all indicators of illicit layering infrastructure rather than legitimate use.

C Identify Third Parties for Legal Process Finding the Subpoena Target ›

After mapping the address and its counterparties, identify which VASPs or custodians have exposure to the traced funds. Screenshot the graph as evidence to present to a prosecutor or supervisor, explaining why legal process should be served on a specific third party.

Use a blockchain tool's "Know Your VASP" feature to find each exchange's jurisdiction, legal contact information, and KYC availability. This determines what legal instrument is required — a domestic subpoena, court order, or MLAT request.

D Serve Legal Process & Address Clustering Getting Identity Data ›

Issue subpoenas or court orders to identified VASPs requesting: KYC documents, subscriber info, email associations, IP address logs, full transaction history, device IDs, and all associated accounts.

Address clustering — the investigator's force multiplier: One person typically controls many wallets. Two core heuristics:

  • Common Input Ownership (CIUA): If two addresses both sign inputs in the same Bitcoin transaction, they share the same private key holder — likely the same person.
  • Change Address Detection: Bitcoin transactions often send change back to the sender; identifying the change address reveals additional wallets controlled by the same entity.

Commercial tools (TRM Labs, Arkham) automate clustering. Always request omnibus "all records associated with the account and registrant" to capture data you haven't specifically anticipated.

OSINT Techniques

Open Source Intelligence (OSINT) is how investigators go from a wallet address to a real person. These are the exact methods used by ZachXBT and professional investigators daily.

"ZachXBT's investigative approach combines blockchain forensics with OSINT techniques… cross-referencing on-chain data with public records such as domain registrations, court filings, and social media activity." — Multiple sources on ZachXBT methodology, March 2026

The OSINT Investigation Workflow

1️⃣ Phase 1 — On-Chain Pivot Points First Foothold in the Real World ›

Start with what the blockchain gives you: wallet addresses, transaction hashes, amounts, timing. Look for exchange deposits (which require KYC), NFT mints (public records), ENS names (human-readable Ethereum names), or any address already labeled in blockchain analytics tools. These are your first pivot points from the on-chain world into the real world.

2️⃣ Phase 2 — Social Media Intelligence Criminals Brag ›

Criminals brag. Search X/Twitter, Telegram, Discord, and Reddit for mentions of wallet addresses, transaction amounts, project names, or usernames. ZachXBT identified the $243M BTC theft suspects partly through their social media spending patterns — luxury purchases and nightclub appearances that were inconsistent with any legitimate income source.

Key searches: wallet address (quoted), transaction amounts (especially unusual ones like "243m" or "$154,814"), project names, and any handles associated with the crime.

3️⃣ Phase 3 — Username & Email Pivots Cross-Platform Identity Tracking ›

A username used in one context often reappears elsewhere. Use OSINT Industries, Sherlock, and IntelX to find all accounts associated with a username, email, or phone number across hundreds of platforms. Breach databases (Snusbase, LeakPeek) may reveal passwords, IPs, or real names from historical data breaches.

The discovery of a criminal's real email from a breach database — showing their password and IP alongside their crypto-world pseudonym — is one of the most common case-breaking moments in crypto OSINT.

4️⃣ Phase 4 — Domain & Infrastructure Analysis Linking Scam Sites to Real Identities ›

WHOIS records, SSL certificate history, Shodan/Censys scans, and domain registration data can link a scam website to a real identity or to other criminal infrastructure. The Lazarus Group registered bybit-assessment[.]com using the email trevorgreer9312@gmail.com hours before the $1.5B hack — a traceable footprint.

Certificate Transparency logs (crt.sh) reveal all SSL certificates ever issued for a domain or email, often uncovering a network of related criminal infrastructure registered under the same identity.

5️⃣ Phase 5 — Timing Analysis Correlating On-Chain & Off-Chain Events ›

Matching the exact time of a blockchain transaction to a social media post, login event, or real-world activity. ZachXBT used timing analysis to match Bitcoin transfers to specific Tron wallet addresses in the Khinkis ransomware investigation — cross-referencing blockchain timestamps against exchange withdrawal times.

Key timing red flags:

  • Funds moving within minutes of a hack announcement
  • Transactions at unusual hours — indicating specific timezone
  • Rapid cascading transactions suggesting automated laundering scripts
  • Dormant addresses activating after months — criminal cashing out
6️⃣ Phase 6 — Archive & Document Everything Preserving Evidence Before It Disappears ›

Use Wayback Machine and Archive.today to preserve evidence before suspects delete it. Screenshot with timestamps. Organize everything in Obsidian or a similar link-chart tool. Your documentation becomes the chain of custody.

Critical rule: Archive immediately when you find evidence — before you do anything else. Suspects routinely delete social media posts, Telegram accounts, and websites within hours of becoming aware of an investigation. The "Haby" case showed this: the suspect deleted their Telegram account just 2 days before ZachXBT published — but archived screenshots preserved the evidence.

Crypto Scams & Fraud

The major categories of crypto fraud — how they work, how they're identified, and how investigations unfold.

💔 Romance Scams — Pig Butchering (SHA ZHU PAN) Most Financially Devastating Category ›

Criminals build a romantic or investment relationship online (often months), then convince victims to invest in a fake crypto platform. When the victim tries to withdraw, they're told to pay more fees. Also called "Sha Zhu Pan" (killing pig scam) — originating from Southeast Asia, often run by human trafficking victims forced to operate scam call centers.

Key indicators: Fake exchange websites, pressure tactics, "guaranteed returns," unreachable withdrawal functions, fake customer support numbers.

Investigation: Trace victim funds through wallet addresses. Look for common deposit addresses across multiple victims — many pigs, same sty. Often flows to TRON USDT wallets before cashing out. Even a single shared deposit address can link dozens of victims to the same criminal infrastructure.

High Volume
SE Asia Operations
🧸 Rug Pulls — DeFi Developer Fraud Liquidity Drain Schemes ›

Developers create a token, build hype, attract investors, then suddenly drain the liquidity pool and abandon the project. Common on DEX platforms (Uniswap, PancakeSwap). Variants: "soft rugs" (slow abandonment) and "hard rugs" (immediate drain).

Red flags: Anonymous team, no audit, locked liquidity expiring soon, excessive dev wallet allocation, mint function retained, copied whitepaper.

Investigation: On-chain analysis of smart contract; trace LP removal to CEX deposits; check deployer wallet history for previous rugs — serial ruggers frequently deploy from the same wallet infrastructure.

🎭 Social Engineering & Impersonation Coinbase Case Study ›

Criminals impersonate exchange support teams, celebrities, or officials to steal credentials or convince victims to send funds. A major 2025 trend: Coinbase impersonation scams using data from a May 2025 insider breach. Attackers hired overseas support agents to steal customer data, then impersonated "Elite Support" to redirect funds.

Case — "Haby" (Abbotsford, BC): A scammer who stole $2M+ by calling Coinbase users claiming to be support, directing them to "secure vaults" he controlled. Identified via timing analysis and Instagram story OSINT revealing his location in British Columbia.

📈 Pump & Dump Schemes Coordinated Market Manipulation ›

Coordinated buying of a low-cap token to inflate price, followed by mass selling at peak. Often coordinated in private Telegram/Discord groups. The 2025 political memecoin era saw multiple high-profile schemes targeting retail investors.

Investigation: Analyze on-chain token distribution — identify wallet clusters that coordinated buys (same timing, similar amounts from related wallets); trace founders' wallets selling at peak; cross-reference wallet activity against Telegram group message timestamps.

Fraud Investigation Framework

1
Victim Report Triage
Collect victim's wallet address, the address they sent funds to, screenshots of the scam platform, and any communications. Get the exact transaction hash. Is the scammer wallet still active?
2
Address Clustering & Fund Flow
Plot the scammer's receiving address. Map outgoing transactions. Look for common deposit addresses shared across multiple victims and exchanges where funds land.
3
OSINT on Scammer Identity
Research scam website WHOIS, social media accounts, and any usernames in communications. Check breach databases for the email used in the scam platform registration.
4
VASP Identification & Legal Process
Identify which exchange(s) received the funds. Issue legal process (subpoena/court order) for KYC, transaction records, IP addresses, and device IDs.

Bybit Exchange Hack — The $1.5 Billion Heist
$1.5 BILLION

The FACT Attribution Framework

A jurisprudence-centered attribution methodology for digital forensics and incident response. By Brett Shavers (v1.1, December 2025). The legal scaffold that converts technical findings into defensible person-level attribution.

📖 Source
FACT Attribution Framework v1.1 — Brett Shavers, December 2025. DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.18005597. © 2025 Brett Shavers. Used for educational reference. Contact: permissions@suspectbehindthekeyboard.com
🧩 Why FACT Exists — The Attribution Gap

Digital forensics has become highly effective at answering what happened, on which device, how, and when. What has been far less developed is a structured, legally grounded way to answer the next question:

"Which specific person, among the realistic candidates, is most likely responsible for those actions — and how confident are we in that conclusion?"

FACT fills this gap. It explicitly separates identification ("what acted?") from attribution ("who acted?") — a distinction that many investigations silently collapse, creating legally vulnerable findings.

Core Distinction: Identification vs Attribution

Technical Identification

"This device / this account / this session performed action X." Does not answer who the real-world actor is. Within the core expertise of digital forensic examiners.

Example: "Wallet 0x1a2b… sent 401,347 ETH to wallet 0x9f3c… on February 21, 2025 at 13:47 UTC."

Investigative Attribution

"Given all available evidence and tested alternatives, Person X is the most plausible actor for Action Y." Requires multi-stream convergence, active falsification of alternatives, and explicit uncertainty.

Example: "Based on KYC records from Binance, timing analysis, and OSINT correlation, the person controlling this wallet is [Name], with strong evidentiary support."

The FACT Lifecycle: F → A → C → T

F
Stage 01
Forensic Authority & Compliance
Document legal authority. Define scope, jurisdiction, proportionality. Establish chain-of-custody. Prevent premature identity assumptions.
A
Stage 02
Analyze Evidence
Identify specific digital actions. Acquire and examine evidence. Evaluate artifact reliability. Generate candidate actors and alternative explanations.
C
Stage 03
Correlate & Sequence
Correlate evidence across identity layers: artifact → device → account → session → person. Test each alternative hypothesis. Determine if convergence threshold is met.
T

Testify & Transfer
Finalize attribution with explicit uncertainty. Prepare legally defensible reports and testimony explaining the reasoning pathway and handling of alternatives.
🔄 FACT is Iterative, Not Linear
FACT is intentionally iterative. New evidence may require revisiting authority in F. Convergence work in C often exposes gaps that send the case back to A. Investigators often iterate A↔C several times before T is even contemplated. New evidence can move the matter back into earlier stages at any time.

Deep Dives — Each Stage

F Forensic Authority & Compliance Legal Bookend #1 — Before You Touch Anything ›

The F stage establishes the legal foundation that makes all subsequent work admissible. Technical capability does not authorize overreach — the examiner must document the legal basis for every action before beginning analysis.

  • Document legal authority: Warrant, consent, statute, contract, or employment policy. What specific authority allows you to access this data?
  • Define scope: Which devices, accounts, date ranges, and data types are in scope? Out-of-scope evidence collected accidentally must be handled carefully.
  • Jurisdiction: Which legal system governs? For crypto cases, this often means multiple jurisdictions (the VASP's jurisdiction, the victim's jurisdiction, the suspect's jurisdiction).
  • Proportionality: Is the investigative action proportionate to the suspected offense? Overbroad collection creates legal risk.
  • Preservation and chain-of-custody: How will evidence be preserved? Every screenshot, blockchain export, and archived web page must be timestamped and documented.
⚠️ Crypto-Specific F Considerations
For blockchain investigations: Is public blockchain data subject to any collection restrictions? Are you querying commercially licensed intelligence tools under appropriate terms? Does your VASP subpoena comply with the exchange's jurisdiction's legal requirements?
A Analyze Evidence Generating Candidate Actors — Not Confirming Assumptions ›

The A stage is about generating a complete picture of the evidence and a complete list of possible actors — not confirming a pre-existing assumption. The FACT framework treats the failure to explicitly address alternative actors as a methodological departure from proper attribution practice.

  • Identify the specific digital action: What exactly happened? A specific wallet transfer, a specific login event, a specific smart contract call. Be precise.
  • Evaluate artifact reliability: Is the blockchain timestamp accurate? Could logs have been manipulated? Is the VASP's KYC data authentic or potentially a straw identity?
  • Generate candidate actors: Who could have controlled this wallet? Consider: the named account holder, a straw identity, a stolen identity, a shared account, a professional money launderer, remote compromise by a third party, an automated process.
  • For crypto investigations: The named account holder on a VASP could be: (1) the actual subject, (2) a straw identity, (3) a stolen identity, or (4) a professional money launderer. Always investigate deeper.
C Correlate & Sequence — Evidence Convergence Multi-Stream Convergence on a Person ›

FACT recommends attribution as justified when evidence from multiple, independent streams converges on the same person while substantially weakening reasonable alternatives. Independence is critical — two artifacts from the same logging mechanism are one stream, not two.

Identity layers the investigator must traverse:

  • Artifact identification → Device identification → Account/credential identity → Session context → Person

Examples of independent streams in crypto cases:

  • On-chain wallet activity + KYC records from exchange (technical vs institutional)
  • Blockchain timestamps + social media post timestamps (on-chain vs OSINT)
  • IP address from VASP subpoena + residential ISP subscriber records (exchange data vs telecom data)
  • Infostealer logs + court filing records (breach data vs public records)

Falsification duty: FACT treats falsification as a methodological duty. Actively search for evidence that would support alternative actors. The absence of evidence about an alternative is not proof against it — if a reasonable alternative remains plausible, person-level attribution is not supportable.

Likelihood language: Weak support / Moderate support / Strong support / Very strong support / Support for alternative / Inconclusive. Every attribution conclusion must identify the specific action, state which person is attributed, describe the evidence streams, and disclose uncertainties.

T Testify & Transfer — Legally Defensible Reporting Court-Ready Attribution (or Explicit Non-Attribution) ›

The T stage structures how person-level attribution is framed in reporting and testimony so that it withstands legal scrutiny and does not overstate what the evidence supports.

As a fact witness (investigator): "Based on the evidence we collected and reviewed, our investigation focused on [Name] as the primary suspect, and I forwarded that case for charging." No comparative probability opinions.

As an expert witness: "In my opinion, based on my training and experience and my review of the digital, documentary, and testimonial evidence, [Name] is more likely than the other realistic candidates to have performed the acts in question; I considered [A, B, C] as alternatives and ruled them out for the following reasons, and these are the uncertainties that remain."

Non-attribution is a correct outcome. When conditions are not met, FACT directs the practitioner to issue an explicit non-attribution finding: "Based on the available evidence and applying the FACT Framework, person-level attribution for Action Y cannot be made. Several reasonable alternative explanations remain, and device or account association alone is insufficient."

FACT Lite — Triage Profile

⚡ FACT Lite — For Time-Boxed Triage

FACT Lite (F + A only) is designed for early uncertainty — preserving authority and hypothesis discipline without permitting person-level conclusions.

  • Lite includes: F + A (authority/scope + initial analysis)
  • Lite excludes: C + T (cross-stream convergence + reporting/testimony) unless escalated
  • Lite output: Candidate hypotheses, evidence preservation plan, and escalation decision — not attribution

Critical rule: FACT Lite may not be used to justify person-level attribution or action. Escalate to full FACT before naming, disciplining, accusing, or reporting externally.

Legal Process & VASP Cooperation

How to translate blockchain evidence into legal action — subpoenas, court orders, international cooperation, and working with VASPs.

⚖️ What You Can Request from a VASP Full Subpoena Target List ›

When issuing legal process (subpoena, court order, MLA request) to a VASP, an investigator may obtain:

  • KYC documents — government ID, selfies, proof of address
  • Subscriber information — name, DOB, registration date, email
  • IP address logs — login history with timestamps (pivot to ISP subpoena)
  • Full transaction history — all deposits/withdrawals
  • Device ID (IMEI) — phone fingerprints for device attribution
  • Account balance — current holdings (freeze if funds still present)
  • Associated accounts — all linked family/business accounts

Best practice: Request an omnibus "all records associated with the account and registrant" to capture anything you haven't specifically anticipated.

🌐 International Cooperation & MLATs Cross-Border Evidence Requests ›

For VASPs in foreign jurisdictions, use Mutual Legal Assistance Treaties (MLATs) or direct law enforcement cooperation channels (FBI, Europol, INTERPOL). Some countries have streamlined crypto-specific cooperation agreements.

Key principle: Many VASPs will accept data requests if legal process has been followed, regardless of jurisdiction, provided it is lawful and clearly communicated. Use a blockchain tool's "Know Your VASP" feature to find each exchange's jurisdiction, contact info, and KYC availability before drafting the request.

Emergency freeze requests: If funds remain on a VASP at the time of legal process, request an emergency freeze simultaneous with the data request. Speed is critical — funds may be withdrawn between subpoena and compliance. Some VASPs have direct freeze hotlines for law enforcement.

📧 Downstream Investigative Actions Building the Full Evidence Package ›
  • Email search warrants: Most VASPs send transaction confirmation emails. Serve the email provider (Google, Outlook) to establish that the account holder received and read transaction confirmations — corroborating knowing participation.
  • IP → ISP subpoena: Residential IP addresses from VASP returns can lead to ISP subpoenas for the subscriber's name and address.
  • Spur.us IP evaluation: Before serving the ISP, check if the IP is residential, VPN, or TOR. A TOR exit node IP does not lead to a subscriber — pivot to other evidence streams instead.
🚨 Straw Identity Warning
The named account holder could be: the actual subject, a straw identity (friend/family), a stolen identity, or a professional money launderer. The KYC return is a lead, not a conclusion. Always investigate deeper — this is the FACT Framework's A stage applied to the investigative context.

Asset Seizure & Forfeiture

The legal and practical mechanics of seizing, holding, and forfeiting cryptocurrency.

📜 Theories of Forfeiture Legal Frameworks & Statutes ›

To seize and forfeit assets, the government must rely on a forfeiture statute. Statutes vary significantly in complexity and requirements:

  • Simple statutes: NY Penal Law Article 480 — "when any person is convicted of a felony offense, property constituting the proceeds and instrumentalities are subject to forfeiture."
  • Complex statutes: US federal civil forfeiture (18 USC 981) — requires specific nexus to listed predicate offenses.
  • Civil forfeiture: Action against the property itself — can proceed even without a criminal conviction in some jurisdictions. The blockchain evidence can establish the nexus between stolen funds and specific wallets.

Best practice: Work with a prosecuting attorney on forfeiture strategy concurrent with the investigation — not as an afterthought.

🔐 Custody Options for Seized Assets Hardware Wallets, VASPs, Third-Party Custodians ›
  • Online Software Wallet: Quick and cheap. Holds wide variety of assets. Higher theft risk through software compromise. Not recommended for large seizures.
  • Hardware Wallet: Industry standard. More secure. Upfront purchase cost. May not support all thinly-traded currencies.
  • VASP Custody: Efficient for transferring assets. Risk: commingling, VASPs have been hacked. Requires confidentiality safeguards.
  • Third-Party Custodian: Expert custody, institutional-grade security. Transfer friction and potential costs.
📋 Five Pre-Seizure Considerations Planning Before You Act ›
  • A — Custody Type: What wallet/custodian will the agency use? Match security level to asset value.
  • B — Access Control: Who has access? Implement need-to-know. Track every access event. Avoid single point of failure.
  • C — Asset Form: Accept crypto as-is or convert to fiat equivalent? Consider price volatility and liquidation difficulty for thinly-traded currencies.
  • D — Contingencies: Software becomes obsolete. VASPs get hacked. New currencies fork. Maintain a "living document" policy reviewed annually.
  • E — Liquidation Plan: VASP market-rate sale, third-party auction, or return as-is? Returning crypto to fraud victims unfamiliar with crypto requires careful planning.

Address Clustering

Sophisticated, state-sponsored threat actor. Lazarus Group (DPRK) is the most active crypto-focused APT.

Bitcoin address clustering heuristic: if two addresses both sign inputs in the same transaction, they share the same private key holder.
Counterparty
The party on the opposite side of a transaction. If A sends to B, B is A's counterparty. Identifying VASP counterparties creates legal chokepoints.
DEX (Decentralized Exchange)
A cryptocurrency exchange without a central authority or KYC requirements. Funds swapped without identity verification — complicates investigations but all trades are on-chain.
Direct Exposure
When an address directly transacted with an illicit source (one hop away).
EXIF Data
Metadata embedded in photos, often including GPS coordinates, device info, and timestamps. Can reveal suspect location via photo OSINT.
Exposure
How directly proceeds have traveled from one address to another. Commercial tools quantify as a percentage. 10%+ is typically flagged as significant.

Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act. Canada's primary AML legislation. Requires VASPs to report suspicious transactions to FINTRAC.
Peeling Chain
Funds moved through many sequential addresses, each time peeling off a slightly smaller amount, to confuse investigators tracing the trail.
Private Key
The secret cryptographic key controlling a wallet. Whoever has the private key controls the funds — and is the person FACT's C stage must attribute.
Pseudonymous
Not fully anonymous. Blockchain addresses don't contain names, but behavioral patterns and links to real-world identity make them investigable.
Rug Pull
Scam where developers drain a token's liquidity pool and abandon the project after attracting investor funds.
SAR (Suspicious Activity Report)
Report filed by financial institutions for suspicious transactions. Common investigative lead source for crypto cases (FINTRAC in Canada, FinCEN in USA).
Smart Contract
Self-executing code on a blockchain. DeFi protocols run on smart contracts; exploits target their logic. All execution is publicly visible on-chain.
Straw Identity
A person used to open an account on behalf of the real criminal. Common in money laundering. FACT's A stage requires this as a candidate actor.
Supply Chain Attack
Compromising a target by attacking a trusted third-party supplier. E.g., the Safe{Wallet} developer machine in the Bybit hack.
Timing Analysis
Cross-referencing blockchain timestamps against external events (social media, login logs) to correlate identities or behaviors.
Tornado Cash
Ethereum-based mixing protocol. Sanctioned by OFAC in 2022. Lazarus Group's preferred ETH mixing tool. Developer Roman Storm convicted in 2024.
Transaction Hash (TXID)
Unique identifier for a specific blockchain transaction. The receipt number — used to look up exact transactions on block explorers.
VASP
Virtual Asset Service Provider. Any platform for buying, selling, or trading crypto. Most hold KYC data investigators can obtain via legal process.
