Glossary & Key Resources

Comprehensive searchable glossary and curated resources for every stage of your investigator development.

Glossary

🔍
Account Takeover (ATO)
Unauthorized access to and control of another person's online account — typically via stolen credentials, phishing, or SIM swap. In crypto contexts, ATO of exchange accounts leads to immediate unauthorized withdrawals.
Address Clustering
Grouping multiple blockchain addresses likely controlled by the same entity using heuristics such as Common Input Ownership (CIUA) and change address detection. A core technique for expanding investigation scope.
Address Poisoning
An attack in which the adversary sends a zero-value or dust transaction from a wallet address that closely resembles the victim's regular counterparty. The goal is to contaminate the victim's transaction history so they copy-paste the malicious lookalike address during a future transfer, sending real funds to the attacker. Identified by Binance security researchers as responsible for millions in losses in 2024. Detection: always verify the full address — not just the first and last four characters — before confirming a transaction.
AI-Generated KYC Bypass
The use of synthetic identity media — AI-generated "liveness" videos, deepfake selfies, or manipulated government ID scans — to pass exchange Know Your Customer (KYC) verification checks using a stolen or fabricated identity. Increasingly documented by identity verification vendors Onfido and Au10tix in their 2025 threat reports. Enables criminals to open VASP accounts attributed to innocent third parties, creating a straw identity layer that complicates attribution. Investigators should treat KYC returns as a lead, not a conclusion, and request additional verification signals (IP, device fingerprint, behavioral analytics) from the VASP.
APT (Advanced Persistent Threat)
A sophisticated, typically state-sponsored threat actor conducting long-term targeted attacks. Lazarus Group (DPRK) is the most prominent APT in crypto crime, having stolen $2B+ in 2025 alone.
Approval Phishing
A phishing variant that tricks victims into signing a malicious ERC-20 token approval transaction — granting an attacker's smart contract unlimited permission to spend the victim's tokens. Unlike credential phishing, the victim's wallet is not directly compromised; instead, the victim unwittingly authorises the attacker to drain their holdings at any future time without further interaction. The FBI IC3 issued a dedicated advisory on approval phishing in November 2023. Mitigation: use wallet tools (Revoke.cash, Etherscan Token Approvals) to audit and revoke outstanding approvals. Distinct from standard phishing — see also Token Approval / ERC-20 Allowance.
ATO
See Account Takeover.
Atomic Transaction
A blockchain transaction in which all operations either succeed completely or revert entirely — there is no partial execution. Atomicity is a fundamental property of EVM smart contract calls and is the mechanism that makes flash loan exploits possible: a malicious contract can borrow funds, manipulate prices, drain a protocol, and repay the loan all within a single transaction. If any step fails, the entire sequence reverts, leaving no trace. Investigators identifying a DeFi exploit should expect the entire attack — including flash loan, manipulation, drain, and repayment — to appear as a single transaction hash on the block explorer.
Attribution
Assigning a blockchain address to a specific real-world entity or individual. The primary goal of blockchain investigation. Sources include: exchange KYC records, court filings, law enforcement databases, commercial analytics platforms, and OSINT.
AUSTRAC
Australian Transaction Reports and Analysis Centre — Australia's financial intelligence unit and AML/CTF regulator. Equivalent to FINTRAC (Canada) and FinCEN (USA). Receives Suspicious Matter Reports (SMRs) from reporting entities including designated service providers in the digital currency exchange sector. Australia's Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorism Financing Act 2006 (AML/CTF Act) governs reporting obligations. Relevant to international crypto investigations given Australia's active crypto market and documented involvement in OTC broker networks — ZachXBT's Khinkis investigation noted travel patterns to Australia.
BEC (Business Email Compromise)
Fraud targeting organizations via compromised or spoofed business email accounts to redirect payments — increasingly to cryptocurrency wallets. Consistently among the highest-loss categories in FBI IC3 annual reports.
Beneficial Owner
The natural person who ultimately owns or controls an account, wallet, entity, or asset — as distinct from the named account holder, KYC identity, or registered legal entity. In crypto investigations, the beneficial owner of a VASP account is frequently not the person whose ID was submitted: mules, straw identities, stolen identities, and AI-generated KYC bypasses all insert false identity layers. FATF Recommendation 10 requires financial institutions to identify beneficial owners of legal entities. For investigators: the KYC return from a subpoena identifies the registered identity, not necessarily the beneficial owner — always investigate further using IP logs, device fingerprints, and behavioral analysis.
Bitcoin ATM
A physical kiosk allowing cash-to-cryptocurrency conversion. A common money laundering placement method. ATM operators are VASPs subject to AML/KYC regulations; their records are subpoenable.
Block Explorer
A tool for querying blockchain data — transaction history, address balances, and relationships. Examples: Etherscan (ETH), Blockchair (BTC), Solscan (SOL). The foundation of every on-chain investigation.
Blockchain
A publicly available, immutable distributed ledger of all completed transactions between addresses. The transparency that makes blockchain investigation possible — unlike cash, every movement is permanently recorded.
Blockchain Tracing
Tracking the source and destination of cryptocurrency transactions to identify the real-world controller of a pseudo-anonymous address. The primary methodology of crypto financial investigation.
Cash-Intensive Business (CIB)
A legitimate business that generates large volumes of cash receipts — food service, car washes, nail salons, parking, laundromats — used by money launderers to commingle illicit funds with genuine revenue before depositing to a bank and converting to cryptocurrency. The CIB provides a plausible explanation for large cash deposits that would otherwise trigger AML reporting. A primary placement technique. Red flags: cash deposits inconsistent with business size, geography, or industry norms; rapid conversion of deposited funds to crypto; structured deposits just below reporting thresholds.
Certificate Transparency (CT) Logs
A public, append-only audit log of every TLS/SSL certificate issued by a Certificate Authority (CA). Searchable via crt.sh, Censys, and other tools. Investigators use CT logs to discover infrastructure pivots: a domain used for a phishing campaign or C2 server may share a certificate with other domains operated by the same threat actor, revealing previously unknown infrastructure. CT logs also reveal the registration timeline of malicious infrastructure — the Lazarus Group registered bybit-assessment[.]com hours before the $1.5B Bybit hack, visible in CT logs. CT log analysis is a standard step in OSINT Phase 4 (Domain and Infrastructure Intel).
Chain-Hopping
Moving assets between different blockchains (e.g., BTC→ETH→TRON) through cross-chain bridges to obscure asset control and complicate investigation. A primary layering technique documented in Lazarus Group post-hack operations.
Change Address
In Bitcoin's UTXO model, the output returning unspent funds back to the sender in a transaction. Identifying change addresses reveals additional wallet addresses controlled by the same entity — extending the investigation cluster.
Checkm8
An unpatchable bootrom vulnerability affecting Apple A5 through A11 chips (iPhone 4S through iPhone X). Because it resides in read-only boot ROM, it cannot be patched via software update. Exploited by forensic acquisition tools including Cellebrite UFED and Elcomsoft iOS Forensic Toolkit to perform file system-level extraction of locked iOS devices without the passcode — bypassing iOS Full Disk Encryption via the Secure Enclave by attacking below the OS layer. Critical for mobile forensics in exchange ATO, SIM swap, and pig butchering investigations involving locked iPhones on affected chipsets. Devices with A12 chips (iPhone XS/XR and later) are not vulnerable.
CIUA (Common Input Ownership)
Address clustering heuristic: if two addresses both sign inputs in the same Bitcoin transaction, the same private key holder controls both. The most reliable Bitcoin clustering method.
Civil Forfeiture
A legal mechanism allowing government authorities to seize assets alleged to be proceeds of crime or instruments of crime without requiring a criminal conviction — the proceeding is brought against the property itself (in rem) rather than against a person (in personam). Civil forfeiture is particularly valuable in crypto investigations where the beneficial owner is beyond extradition (e.g., Lazarus Group) or where criminal prosecution is impractical. In Canada, civil forfeiture proceeds under provincial Civil Remedies Acts. In the US, the DOJ Asset Forfeiture Program uses both civil and administrative forfeiture. Because the standard of proof is preponderance of evidence (lower than criminal beyond reasonable doubt), investigators can proceed even when a conviction is unlikely. See also: In Rem Forfeiture, Proceeds of Crime.
Clipboard Hijacker
Malware that monitors the clipboard for crypto wallet address formats and replaces copied addresses with the attacker's address. Victims unknowingly send funds to the attacker.
Cobalt Strike Beacon
The payload component of Cobalt Strike, a commercially licensed adversary simulation framework widely abused by ransomware groups, APTs, and criminal networks as a post-exploitation Command and Control (C2) tool. A beacon is a small agent installed on a compromised host that communicates with the attacker's team server at configurable intervals ("beaconing"), enabling remote command execution, lateral movement, credential harvesting, and data exfiltration. Default Cobalt Strike configurations leave detectable artifacts: specific malleable C2 profiles, default SSL certificate fingerprints, and characteristic sleep jitter patterns. Threat hunters scan for these defaults using tools like cs-detect and JARM fingerprinting. Cobalt Strike beacons are present in the pre-encryption dwell phase of nearly every major ransomware incident.
Cold Wallet
A cryptocurrency wallet stored offline, disconnected from the internet. Considered more secure, but Bybit's $1.5B hack demonstrated that supply chain attacks on signing infrastructure can compromise cold wallet security without accessing the device directly.
Cold-to-Warm Wallet Transfer
A scheduled custody operation in which funds are moved from offline cold storage to an online hot wallet to enable exchange liquidity or operational needs — requiring a signing ceremony involving multiple authorized keyholders and the cold wallet device. This transfer event is a high-value, predictable target for sophisticated attackers: Lazarus Group's Bybit hack was timed to activate malicious JavaScript precisely during a cold-to-warm signing session, silently redirecting 401,347 ETH. Investigators and exchange security teams should treat cold-to-warm ceremonies as elevated-risk events requiring enhanced integrity checks on signing infrastructure, software hashes, and transaction display verification against an independent trusted interface.
Contagious Interview
A Lazarus Group sub-campaign (also tracked as Famous Chollima by CrowdStrike) targeting software developers — particularly those in the cryptocurrency sector — through fake technical job interviews. Victims are contacted via LinkedIn with lucrative developer job opportunities, then asked to complete a "coding test" by cloning and running a GitHub repository. The repository contains a trojanized npm or Python package that executes a malware payload on install, establishing persistent access to the developer's machine. This technique was used to compromise a Safe{Wallet} developer machine, providing the initial access that led to the $1.5B Bybit hack. See also: TraderTraitor, Supply Chain Attack.
Counterparty
The party on the opposite side of a transaction. If "A" sends Bitcoin to "B," then B is A's counterparty. Identifying counterparties — especially known VASPs — is a core tracing strategy. The counterparty is often the leverage point for legal process.
Credential Dumping
The extraction of authentication credentials from a compromised system — including plaintext passwords, NTLM password hashes, and Kerberos tickets — to enable lateral movement and privilege escalation. Primary targets: the Windows Local Security Authority Subsystem Service (LSASS) process (attacked using tools such as Mimikatz, ProcDump, or comsvcs.dll), the SAM registry hive, and NTDS.dit on domain controllers. In crypto-targeting intrusions, credential dumping enables attackers to pivot from a compromised developer workstation to exchange signing infrastructure, cold wallet management systems, or custody platforms. Forensic indicators: access to lsass.exe from non-system processes, creation of memory dump files (.dmp), and use of sekurlsa::logonpasswords or equivalent. Detection via Windows Event 4656 (lsass handle request) and EDR telemetry.
Cross-Chain Bridge Exploit
An attack targeting the smart contracts or validator infrastructure of a cross-chain bridge — a protocol that locks assets on one blockchain and mints equivalent tokens on another. Bridges aggregate enormous value in their lock contracts, making them high-value targets. Major exploits include: Ronin Network ($625M, March 2022 — validator private key compromise), Wormhole ($320M, February 2022 — signature verification bypass), Nomad ($190M, August 2022 — flawed message validation), and Harmony Horizon ($100M, June 2022). Distinct from chain-hopping (the legitimate use of bridges for layering). Investigators: the exploit transaction is always on-chain; trace the bridge lock contract address forward and the attacker's receive address on the destination chain backward to the funding source.
Cryptojacking
Unauthorized use of a victim's computing resources to mine cryptocurrency for the attacker. Monero (XMR) is the most common target. Victims experience degraded performance and elevated cloud/energy costs. Mining pool records provide investigable chokepoints.
Dead-Drop Resolver
A technique in which malware retrieves its live Command and Control (C2) server address at runtime from a publicly accessible, attacker-controlled third-party service — rather than hardcoding the C2 IP or domain in the binary. Common dead-drop locations: Steam profile biography fields, Telegram channel descriptions, Pastebin posts, GitHub repositories, and image metadata. Documented in Vidar and Lumma Stealer families. Advantages for the attacker: the malware binary itself contains no network IOCs, blocking the C2 domain does not prevent re-tasking (attacker simply updates the profile), and the dead-drop platform is a legitimate service that cannot be taken down. Investigative response: capture the full dead-drop URL and its content at time of analysis; platform abuse reports to Steam/Telegram/GitHub can disrupt the C2 retrieval chain.
DEX (Decentralized Exchange)
A cryptocurrency exchange operating without a central authority or KYC requirements — Uniswap, PancakeSwap, Curve. No central operator to subpoena, but all activity is on-chain and traceable. Commonly used in layering phases.
Deepfake Fraud
AI-generated synthetic video, audio, or images used to impersonate real individuals for financial deception. Applications include KYC bypass at exchanges, fake investment endorsements, real-time CEO impersonation fraud, and romance scam personas.
DeFi (Decentralized Finance)
Financial services built on blockchain smart contracts without central operators. Increasingly used for laundering due to lack of KYC — but all activity is on-chain and traceable. Smart contract exploits are a distinct attack surface.
Direct Exposure
When an address directly transacted with an illicit source — one hop away. Distinct from indirect exposure (two or more hops). Commercial tools quantify exposure as a percentage. Used in legal process justification.
Double Extortion
A ransomware tactic in which attackers both encrypt victim files and exfiltrate sensitive data before encryption, threatening to publish the stolen data on a dedicated leak site unless the ransom is paid. This creates two independent leverage points: restoring operations (requiring the decryption key) and preventing reputational damage or regulatory exposure from published data. Double extortion became standard practice among major ransomware groups from 2020 onwards, pioneered by Maze ransomware. Triple extortion adds a DDoS attack as a third lever. For investigators, data exfiltration precedes encryption — network monitoring for large outbound transfers in the hours before encryption events is a key detection opportunity. See also: RaaS.
Drainer-as-a-Service (DaaS)
A criminal business model offering subscription or revenue-share access to automated wallet drain infrastructure — phishing kits, smart contracts, and backend panels — enabling non-technical operators to conduct approval phishing and token drain campaigns. Prominent DaaS operators include Inferno Drainer (shut down December 2023, responsible for $80M+ in losses), Angel Drainer, Pink Drainer (shut down May 2024), and Venom Drainer. Chainalysis estimated DaaS operations stole over $300M across 2023–2024. Analogous to Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) — the drainer developer takes a 20–30% cut of stolen funds. Investigative approach: the drainer smart contract is deployed on-chain; trace the deployer wallet backward to identify the operator, and forward to map all victim wallets and stolen assets.
Dust Attack
A blockchain surveillance technique — used by both investigators and adversaries — in which tiny amounts of cryptocurrency ("dust," typically below transaction fee value) are sent to a large number of wallet addresses. When the wallet owner consolidates dust with other UTXOs in a future transaction, the Common Input Ownership (CIUA) clustering heuristic links the previously separate addresses together, potentially deanonymizing the wallet cluster. Threat actors use dust attacks to track high-value targets; chain analytics companies have used similar techniques for attribution. Privacy-conscious users should avoid spending dust, use coin control to exclude it, or use a wallet that labels and quarantines dust inputs.
EDL Mode (Emergency Download Mode)
A low-level hardware diagnostic mode built into Qualcomm chipsets (used in most Android devices) that allows direct communication with the device's flash memory via the Qualcomm Sahara/Firehose protocol — bypassing the operating system entirely. Exploited by mobile forensic tools (UFED, OXYGEN, Magnet) and specialist services to perform physical acquisition of locked Android devices, including extraction of full disk images regardless of PIN or password protection. Not all devices support EDL extraction — availability depends on OEM implementation, bootloader lock state, and whether a working Firehose programmer is available for the specific device model. See also: Checkm8 (the iOS equivalent).
ENS (Ethereum Name Service)
A decentralized domain naming system on Ethereum mapping human-readable names (e.g., vitalik.eth) to wallet addresses, smart contracts, and other on-chain identifiers. ENS names are publicly registered on-chain — an investigative pivot point when a criminal uses a vanity ENS name that can be traced through their on-chain activity.
ERC-20
The Ethereum token standard defining a common interface for fungible tokens. Most stablecoins (USDT, USDC, DAI) and exchange tokens are ERC-20. Investigators must query token transfer events (not just ETH transactions) when tracing ERC-20 fund flows on Etherscan — the standard ETH transaction view does not show ERC-20 movements.
EXIF Data
Metadata embedded in photo files including GPS coordinates, device model, and timestamps. Can reveal a suspect's physical location when photos are shared without stripping metadata. Used in multiple ZachXBT investigations.
Exposure
A quantified measure of how directly proceeds have traveled between two addresses. Commercial tools express this as a percentage. 15%+ exposure to ransomware or sanctioned entities is considered a significant investigative indicator.
FATF
Financial Action Task Force. International AML/CFT standard-setting body. Coined the term "VASP" and established the Travel Rule requiring VASPs to transmit originator/beneficiary information on crypto transactions above thresholds.
FinCEN
Financial Crimes Enforcement Network — the US Treasury bureau responsible for AML/CFT regulation. Collects SARs from US financial institutions and VASPs. Issues guidance on crypto AML obligations. Equivalent to FINTRAC (Canada) and AUSTRAC (Australia). FinCEN data sharing with law enforcement provides investigative leads not visible on-chain.
FINTRAC
Financial Transactions and Reports Analysis Centre of Canada. Canada's financial intelligence unit. Receives Suspicious Transaction Reports (STRs) from VASPs and financial institutions. Equivalent to FinCEN (US) and AUSTRAC (Australia).
Flash Loan Attack
A DeFi exploit using uncollateralized instant loans to manipulate oracle prices and drain protocols in a single atomic transaction. Entirely on-chain and traceable from the first transaction. The exploit contract deployer wallet is always investigable.
Fraud Diamond
An extension of the Fraud Triangle adding "Capability" as a fourth element. The four elements required for fraud: Pressure/Incentive, Opportunity, Rationalization, and Capability. All four must be present for fraud to occur.
Fraud Triangle
The original three-element model of fraud causation by criminologist Donald Cressey: Pressure, Opportunity, and Rationalization. The Fraud Diamond adds Capability as a fourth element.
Gas / Gas Price
The unit measuring computational effort required to execute a specific operation on the Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM). Gas price (denominated in Gwei, a subdivision of ETH) is the amount a user pays per unit of gas — set by the user and subject to market demand for block space. Total transaction fee = gas used × gas price. Investigators use gas price analysis to: identify DeFi exploiters who paid elevated gas to front-run or prioritize their attack transaction (a forensic signature of planned attacks); detect MEV bots; and establish transaction ordering within a block. Unusual gas pricing — far above or below prevailing rates — is a red flag warranting investigation of the transaction's purpose.
Geolocation OSINT
The use of open-source information to determine a subject's physical location — without access to carrier data, warrants, or technical surveillance. Techniques include: extracting GPS coordinates from photo EXIF metadata, analysing background details in images (landmarks, signage, architecture), identifying timezone-consistent posting patterns, correlating check-ins and tagged locations on social platforms, and reviewing flight tracking or public event attendance records. ZachXBT confirmed a suspect's location in Abbotsford, British Columbia, through Instagram story metadata in the Haby investigation. EXIF data is stripped by most major social platforms (Instagram, Twitter/X) but persists in images shared via messaging apps, email, or direct download. A primary technique in OSINT Phase 5 (Physical Evidence Correlation).
Hawala
An informal value transfer system used predominantly in South Asian and Middle Eastern countries. Value transferred via a network of brokers (hawaladars) with settlement later. Increasingly combined with crypto to create hawala-crypto hybrid laundering systems.
HD Wallet (Hierarchical Deterministic)
A wallet that generates all addresses from a single seed phrase using a deterministic derivation path (BIP-32/44). A single 12/24-word seed phrase controls potentially thousands of addresses across multiple blockchains. Forensically significant: seed phrase compromise = total loss of all derived wallets across all supported chains.
Honeypot Contract
A malicious token smart contract that allows purchases but disables sells — trapping investor funds. A common rug pull variant on DEX platforms. Detectable via smart contract review tools (HoneyPot.is).
In Rem / In Personam Forfeiture
The two legal theories under which asset forfeiture proceedings may be brought. In rem forfeiture ("against the thing") proceeds against the asset itself — the property is the legal defendant. It does not require identifying or convicting the beneficial owner, making it particularly useful when assets can be traced but the owner is abroad or unidentified (e.g., Lazarus Group wallets). In personam forfeiture ("against the person") proceeds against an identified individual as part of or following criminal conviction. In crypto investigations, in rem civil forfeiture is the dominant tool for seizing identified illicit wallets when criminal prosecution is impractical or the subject is beyond jurisdiction. See also: Civil Forfeiture, Proceeds of Crime.
Infostealer
Malware designed to silently harvest saved credentials, browser cookies, crypto wallet files, and session tokens. Major families: Redline, Vidar, Racoon, Lumma. Sold on darknet forums via Malware-as-a-Service (MaaS) subscriptions. Logs resold on Telegram markets.
Initial Access Broker (IAB)
A threat actor who specializes in gaining unauthorized access to corporate networks and then selling that access — rather than monetizing it directly — to ransomware affiliates, espionage actors, or other criminal groups. IABs typically gain access via phishing, credential stuffing, VPN exploitation, or infostealer logs, then advertise the access on darknet forums (RAMP, Exploit[.]in, XSS) with details on the victim's industry, revenue, and country. In the crypto sector, IcedID/BokBot is documented as an IAB that has provided initial access to ransomware operators. IABs represent a distinct criminal role in the ransomware supply chain — separate from RaaS developers, affiliates, and money launderers. Identifying the IAB in an intrusion investigation can link multiple separate incidents to the same threat actor.
Integration
The third and final stage of money laundering — re-entering laundered funds into the legitimate economy so they appear to have a lawful origin. Crypto integration methods: OTC broker fiat conversion, real estate, luxury assets, and business investment.
Instant Exchange
A service allowing cryptocurrency swaps without KYC — ChangeNow, FixedFloat, SimpleSwap. No central operator to subpoena, but consistent wallet infrastructure is identifiable across investigations. Commonly used in layering.
IOC (Indicator of Compromise)
Observable artifacts that indicate a system has been compromised — file hashes, IP addresses, domain names, registry keys, mutex names, and behavioral patterns. In e-crime: IOCs extracted from malware analysis (C2 domains, replacement wallet addresses) feed directly into on-chain tracing and network-level detection rules.
KYC (Know Your Customer)
Real-world identification collected by VASPs: government ID, selfie, date of birth, address, phone, email. When obtained via legal process, KYC records typically identify the real person controlling an otherwise anonymous wallet — the most common investigative breakthrough.
Keylogger
Malware recording every keystroke — capturing passwords, seed phrases, and private keys as typed. Often bundled within infostealer packages. Seed phrase capture leads to complete and irreversible wallet compromise.
Lateral Movement
The techniques an attacker uses to progressively move deeper into a network after establishing initial access — pivoting from the initially compromised host to additional systems, escalating privileges, and approaching high-value targets (signing infrastructure, cold wallet management systems, exchange databases). Common lateral movement techniques include Pass-the-Hash, PsExec, WMI remote execution, RDP hijacking, and Kerberoasting. In crypto-sector intrusions, lateral movement from a compromised developer workstation to a signing server or HSM (Hardware Security Module) is the critical pivot enabling large-scale theft. Forensic indicators: unusual authentication events (Event 4624), PsExec artifacts, WMI subscription creation, and SMB lateral movement patterns in network logs. See also: Cobalt Strike Beacon, Credential Dumping.
Layering
The second stage of money laundering — creating distance between funds and their criminal origin through complex transaction chains. Blockchain forensics is most effective at tracing and documenting layering activity, as every hop is permanently on-chain.
Lazarus Group
North Korea's elite state-sponsored hacking unit, controlled by the Reconnaissance General Bureau (RGB). Stole $2B+ in crypto in 2025 including the $1.5B Bybit hack. Uses crypto theft to fund DPRK's nuclear weapons program. Sub-groups: TraderTraitor, Contagious Interview, APT38.
Living-Off-the-Land (LotL)
An attack technique in which threat actors use legitimate, pre-installed operating system tools and utilities — rather than custom malware — to conduct malicious operations. Common LotL tools: PowerShell, WMI, certutil, regsvr32, mshta, bitsadmin, PsExec, and Scheduled Tasks. Because these are legitimate system binaries (sometimes called "LOLBins" — Living Off the Land Binaries), their use is far less likely to trigger antivirus or EDR alerts than deploying custom malware. Lazarus Group and most ransomware operators use LotL techniques extensively during the dwell and lateral movement phases. For threat hunters: baseline normal use of these tools in your environment, then alert on anomalous parent processes, command-line arguments, or timing. MITRE ATT&CK documents specific LotL techniques under T1218 (System Binary Proxy Execution).
MaaS (Malware-as-a-Service)
The sale of malware (especially infostealers and ransomware) on subscription or affiliate models via darknet marketplaces. Enables non-technical criminals to deploy sophisticated malware. RaaS (Ransomware-as-a-Service) is the ransomware-specific variant.
Maximal Extractable Value (MEV) / Sandwich Attack
Maximal Extractable Value (MEV) refers to the maximum value that can be extracted from block production beyond standard block rewards and gas fees, by reordering, inserting, or censoring transactions within a block. A sandwich attack is the most common predatory MEV technique: a bot detects a large pending DEX swap in the mempool, front-runs it with a buy order (driving up the price), allows the victim's transaction to execute at the inflated price, then immediately sells (back-running) to capture the spread — at the victim's expense. While legitimate MEV extraction is a grey area, sandwich attacks and malicious front-running are used in DeFi manipulation schemes and protocol exploits. Investigators seeing anomalous pre-attack transactions milliseconds before an exploit should check for MEV bot involvement and examine block ordering on the specific validator that produced the block.
Mempool
The pool of unconfirmed blockchain transactions awaiting inclusion in the next block. Investigators can monitor the mempool for real-time transaction alerts — critical for rapid response to exchange hacks when funds are still moving.
Mempool Monitoring
The practice of watching unconfirmed transactions in the mempool before they are included in a block — enabling real-time detection of suspicious fund movements before they are confirmed and irreversible. Tools: Blocknative, mempool.space (Bitcoin), Ethereum mempool APIs. In e-crime investigation, mempool monitoring allows responders to identify attacker wallet addresses within seconds of a hack, issue emergency VASP freeze requests before funds reach an exchange deposit, and alert downstream VASPs to flag incoming illicit funds. The window between a transaction entering the mempool and receiving its first confirmation (10 minutes for Bitcoin, 12 seconds for Ethereum) is the primary intervention opportunity in acute crypto theft incidents.
Mixer / Tumbler
Services pooling funds from multiple users and redistributing them to break transaction chains. Examples: Tornado Cash (ETH, OFAC-sanctioned 2022), Wasabi Wallet (BTC CoinJoin). Using a mixer does not make funds untraceable — it creates a fork requiring parallel output analysis.
MLAT
Mutual Legal Assistance Treaty. International agreement enabling countries to share legal process and evidence. Used to obtain records from VASPs in foreign jurisdictions. Speed varies significantly by country — some jurisdictions have streamlined crypto-specific cooperation channels.
Money Mule
An individual who receives and transfers illicit proceeds — knowingly or unwittingly — on behalf of a criminal network. In crypto: account mules (open exchange accounts), transfer mules (forward crypto), and cash-out mules (convert to fiat). The KYC holder is often the mule, not the criminal.
Monero (XMR)
A privacy-focused cryptocurrency using ring signatures and stealth addresses to obscure sender, receiver, and amount. The hardest blockchain to trace. Preferred by ransomware groups for payments. Exchange on/off-ramps remain investigable chokepoints.
Multisig Wallet (Multi-Signature)
A cryptocurrency wallet or smart contract requiring M signatures from a set of N authorized keyholders to approve a transaction (M-of-N scheme — e.g., 3-of-5). Used by exchanges, protocols, and high-value custody arrangements to eliminate single points of failure. Common implementations: Bitcoin native multisig, Ethereum Gnosis Safe (now Safe{Wallet}). Despite their security design, multisig arrangements can be compromised through supply chain attacks targeting the signing infrastructure rather than the keys themselves — as demonstrated in the Bybit hack, where malicious JavaScript injected into the Safe{Wallet} front-end silently altered the transaction being displayed to signers. For investigators: multisig custody does not guarantee security against sophisticated threat actors; the signing process and interface integrity must both be verified.
NFT Wash Trading
Buying NFTs between self-controlled wallets at artificially inflated prices to establish "legitimate" art market transaction records for funds. A placement and layering tactic creating apparent legitimate provenance.
OFAC
U.S. Office of Foreign Assets Control. Sanctions crypto wallet addresses and services linked to sanctioned entities — Lazarus Group wallets, Tornado Cash, Sinbad.io, ChipMixer. Transactions with OFAC-listed addresses create legal liability for VASPs in any jurisdiction.
OEP (Original Entry Point)
The first instruction executed by the legitimate code of a packed or protected executable after the unpacking stub has decompressed and decrypted the original payload into memory. In malware analysis, identifying the OEP is the critical step when analysing packed binaries — execution must be allowed to run until the OEP before the analyst dumps the unpacked code from memory for static analysis. Technique: set a breakpoint at the suspected OEP in x64dbg using the "run until return" approach or OEP-detection plugins (OllyDumpEx, Scylla), then dump and rebuild the import table. Most common packer: UPX (trivially unpacked with upx -d); custom packers require dynamic analysis to locate the OEP.
Operational Security (OPSEC)
The discipline of protecting sensitive information and activities from adversaries by identifying and eliminating unintentional disclosures. In criminal contexts, poor OPSEC — the failure to adequately conceal identity, location, and activities — is the most consistent investigative opening across ZachXBT's case studies: suspects publicly posting luxury purchases inconsistent with legitimate income ($243M BTC theft), Instagram story metadata revealing precise physical location (Haby case), reusing usernames across platforms (linked via OSINT Industries), and accidentally screen-sharing government custody wallet activity in a Telegram dispute (USMS case). For investigators: OPSEC failures are systematic — criminals who brag once typically have a pattern. A single metadata leak, username reuse, or social media post can unravel an otherwise sophisticated operation.
OSINT
Open Source Intelligence. Information gathered from publicly available sources — social media, WHOIS records, court filings, breach databases, and forum posts. Combined with on-chain blockchain data, OSINT bridges the gap from pseudonymous wallet to real-world identity.
OTC Broker
Over-the-counter broker facilitating large crypto-fiat conversions outside formal exchange infrastructure. The most common final cashout mechanism for major criminal networks. Some are licensed MSBs; others are entirely unlicensed. Documented in the Khinkis ransomware case (March 2026).
Pass-Through Address
An address that receives funds and immediately forwards the entire amount, leaving a $0 balance. A significant red flag for layering — the controller paid transaction fees for an economically unnecessary step. Documented in TRM Labs' Flip Book as a primary illicit activity indicator.
Passive DNS
Historical records of DNS resolutions — which IP addresses a domain has pointed to over time, and which domains have resolved to a given IP — collected by security researchers via passive observation of DNS traffic. Searchable via VirusTotal, SecurityTrails, RiskIQ (Microsoft Defender Threat Intelligence), and Farsight DNSDB. Critical for infrastructure pivoting: a domain used in a phishing campaign may have previously pointed to an IP hosting other malicious domains attributed to the same actor; a C2 server IP may have hosted dozens of malware-serving domains over time. Passive DNS also establishes the timeline of infrastructure deployment — useful for correlating attack preparation with known incident dates. A standard tool in OSINT Phase 4 (Domain and Infrastructure Intel).
PCMLTFA
Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act — Canada's primary AML/CFT legislation. Establishes reporting requirements for VASPs (including crypto exchanges) operating in Canada. FINTRAC is the competent authority.
Peeling Chain
A layering technique sending funds through many sequential addresses, each forwarding slightly less than the prior — creating a long trail to exhaust investigative resources. Commercial tools like TRM Labs trace these automatically.
Phishing
Deceptive communications impersonating trusted entities to steal credentials, seed phrases, or private keys. Variants: spear-phishing (targeted), whale phishing (high-value targets), and vishing (voice phishing). The initial access vector for the $1.5B Bybit hack.
Pig Butchering (Sha Zhu Pan)
A long-con romance/investment fraud originating from Southeast Asian cyber-scam compounds. Criminals cultivate trust over months before directing victims to fraudulent crypto investment platforms. The most financially devastating fraud category by total losses. Primarily uses TRON USDT for cashout.
Pig Butchering Compound
Physical facilities — predominantly in Myanmar, Cambodia, Laos, and the Philippines — where trafficked workers are held against their will and forced to operate romance scam and pig butchering fraud operations at scale. Workers are often lured with false job advertisements before being trafficked, confiscated of their passports, and subjected to violence or threats if quotas are not met. The United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC) estimated in 2023 that hundreds of thousands of people are held in such facilities across Southeast Asia. Investigators pursuing pig butchering cases should be alert to the human trafficking dimension — victims of the fraud may themselves be linked to trafficking victims. International cooperation through Interpol and relevant Mutual Legal Assistance Treaties is required for action against compound operators.
Placement
The first stage of money laundering — introducing illicit funds into the financial system. The highest-risk stage for detection. Crypto placement methods: Bitcoin ATMs, P2P exchanges, structuring, and casino chips. Also the least blockchain-visible stage as it often starts with cash.
Predicate Offence
The underlying criminal offence that generates the proceeds subsequently laundered. Money laundering charges — under PCMLTFA in Canada, the Proceeds of Crime Act in the UK, or 18 U.S.C. § 1956 in the USA — require prosecutors to establish both the money laundering conduct and the existence of a predicate offence from which the laundered proceeds derive. In crypto investigations, common predicate offences include ransomware (extortion / criminal mischief), pig butchering (fraud), exchange hacks (theft / computer fraud), and drug trafficking. Establishing the predicate offence chain — from the underlying crime to the wallet to the VASP cashout — is the core narrative of a crypto money laundering prosecution.
Preservation Letter / Hold Notice
An emergency written request to a VASP, email provider, or other digital service to immediately preserve all records associated with a specific account, wallet, or identifier — before formal legal process is served. Preservation letters are time-critical in crypto investigations: VASPs may delete account data upon account closure, and standard legal process timelines (weeks to months) often exceed the window before a criminal withdraws or transfers funds. In the US, 18 U.S.C. § 2703(f) authorizes law enforcement to compel electronic service providers to preserve records for 90 days pending formal legal process. Canadian equivalents exist under s. 487.012 of the Criminal Code. Best practice: send a preservation letter simultaneously with or immediately before serving a production order, and explicitly request an emergency asset freeze if funds remain on the platform.
Private Key
The cryptographic secret that controls a cryptocurrency wallet. Whoever holds the private key controls the funds. Never share it. Seed phrase compromise (12/24 words) is equivalent to private key compromise for HD wallets.
Process Injection
A class of techniques in which malicious code is inserted into the memory space of a legitimate, running process — causing that process to execute attacker-controlled code while appearing benign to defenders. Common variants: DLL injection (LoadLibrary into a target process), process hollowing (spawn a legitimate process suspended, hollow out its memory, replace with malicious payload), reflective DLL injection (DLL loads itself directly from memory without touching disk), and APC injection (queue malicious code via Asynchronous Procedure Calls). Process injection is used to evade process-level detections (the malicious code runs inside a trusted process such as explorer.exe or svchost.exe), bypass application whitelisting, and achieve persistence without creating detectable new processes. Detection: Volatility malfind plugin identifies memory regions with both write and execute permissions containing PE headers — the signature of injected code.
Proceeds of Crime
Property, money, or other assets derived directly or indirectly from the commission of a criminal offence. The legal definition underpinning both money laundering statutes and asset forfeiture law. Under PCMLTFA and equivalent legislation, proceeds of crime are subject to seizure, restraint, and forfeiture regardless of how many times they have been transferred or converted — the taint follows the property. For blockchain investigators, establishing that a specific wallet holds proceeds of crime (by tracing its funding to an identifiable criminal event, such as a ransomware payment or phishing drain) is the threshold for obtaining a restraint or freezing order. Distinct from "instruments of crime" (property used to commit an offence, such as infrastructure or malware development tools). See also: Predicate Offence, Civil Forfeiture.
Production Order
The Canadian legal mechanism — equivalent to a subpoena in US practice — compelling a third party such as a VASP, ISP, or financial institution to produce specified records to law enforcement. Issued under s. 487.012 of the Criminal Code of Canada. Production orders are the primary tool for obtaining VASP KYC records, transaction histories, IP logs, and device identifiers in Canadian crypto investigations. Unlike in the US where "subpoena" is the colloquial term, Canadian practitioners must use the correct terminology when drafting requests to domestic VASPs and when explaining investigative steps to Crown prosecutors. Production orders can be combined with a preservation demand (s. 487.012(3)) requiring the VASP to hold the data pending formal production. International requests use MLAT channels or Mutual Legal Assistance through the Department of Justice's International Assistance Group (IAG).
Pseudonymous
Not fully anonymous. Blockchain addresses contain no names but are traceable through behavioral patterns, VASP chokepoints, and OSINT correlation. "Pseudonymous ≠ anonymous" is the fundamental principle of blockchain investigation.
Pump.fun
A Solana-based token launchpad that enables anyone to create and immediately list a memecoin with a bonding curve mechanism — eliminating the technical barrier to token creation. Launched in January 2024, it facilitated the creation of millions of tokens within its first year and became the dominant venue for memecoin rug pulls on Solana in 2024–2025. The platform's permissionless design, combined with the ease of anonymous token creation and the speed of Solana transactions, created an industrialised rug pull environment: tokens are created, promoted via social media and Telegram, attract retail buyers, and are abandoned by developer-controlled insider wallets within hours or days. Investigators: the deployer wallet on Pump.fun is on-chain and traceable; bundler wallets (multiple insider wallets coordinated to buy at launch) leave a detectable clustering pattern across the Solana transaction graph.
RaaS (Ransomware-as-a-Service)
The business model for major ransomware groups: developers provide the malware and infrastructure; affiliates deploy attacks; proceeds split (typically 20-30% developer / 70-80% affiliate). Examples: LockBit, ALPHV/BlackCat, Cl0p.
Ransomware
Malware encrypting victim files/systems and demanding cryptocurrency for the decryption key. Modern variants use double extortion (encrypt + threaten to publish data). Payments in BTC or XMR create on-chain evidence trails for investigators.
RGB (Reconnaissance General Bureau)
Chongch'al Ch'ongguk — North Korea's primary foreign intelligence service, operating under the Korean People's Army. The RGB controls Lazarus Group and its sub-units (TraderTraitor, Contagious Interview, APT38), directing cryptocurrency theft as a state-sanctioned revenue generation mechanism to fund the DPRK's ballistic missile and nuclear weapons programmes in circumvention of UN Security Council sanctions. The RGB's Unit 180 is specifically assessed by US and South Korean intelligence agencies as responsible for financially motivated cyber operations. Understanding that Lazarus Group operates as a state intelligence unit — not a profit-seeking criminal gang — explains their unique behavioral markers: willingness to accept value losses during laundering, no need to cash out personally, and patience to conduct multi-month intrusion campaigns against single high-value targets.
Rug Pull
A DeFi fraud where developers drain a token's liquidity pool and abandon the project after attracting investor funds. Hard rug = immediate drain; soft rug = gradual abandonment. Common on low-barrier DEX platforms (Uniswap, PancakeSwap, Pump.fun).
SAR (Suspicious Activity Report)
Report filed by financial institutions and VASPs when suspicious transactions are detected. In Canada: Suspicious Transaction Report (STR) filed with FINTRAC. In the US: SAR filed with FinCEN. A common source of investigative leads for crypto cases.
Scattered Spider
A loosely affiliated, English-speaking threat group (also tracked as UNC3944 by Mandiant, Muddled Libra by Unit 42, and Starfraud by Microsoft) composed predominantly of young adults and teenagers operating primarily from the US and UK. Known for sophisticated social engineering: vishing helpdesk staff to reset MFA, SIM swapping executives, and impersonating IT support. Responsible for the September 2023 data extortion attacks against MGM Resorts ($100M+ operational impact) and Caesars Entertainment ($15M ransom paid). In crypto contexts, Scattered Spider has targeted cryptocurrency exchanges, DeFi protocols, and crypto-adjacent technology companies using stolen credentials obtained through vishing and SIM swaps at scale. Distinct from nation-state APTs in that members are primarily financially motivated and have faced arrest (multiple members arrested in US/UK in 2024). CISA and FBI issued a joint advisory on Scattered Spider TTPs in November 2023.
Seed Phrase
A 12 or 24-word mnemonic that provides complete access to all wallets derived from an HD wallet. Equivalent to the private key — compromise of the seed phrase means total, irreversible loss of all associated wallet funds.
Sextortion
Extortion using threats to distribute intimate images unless cryptocurrency payment is made. Variants: mass email campaigns using breach passwords as "proof" of webcam access; actual intimate image theft; AI-generated deepfake imagery as leverage.
SIM Swap
Fraudulent transfer of a victim's mobile phone number to an attacker-controlled SIM card — enabling interception of SMS 2FA codes. Used to bypass two-factor authentication on cryptocurrency exchange accounts to facilitate account takeover.
Smart Contract
Self-executing code on a blockchain automatically enforcing agreement terms. DeFi protocols run on smart contracts. Exploits target smart contract logic errors. All contract interactions are on-chain and traceable; the contract deployer wallet is always investigable.
Sock Puppet Account
A fake online persona — typically with a fabricated identity, profile photograph (often AI-generated or stolen), and manufactured history — used to deceive targets for social engineering, intelligence gathering, or fraud. In crypto crime: sock puppets are the operational backbone of pig butchering campaigns (fake romantic personas on dating apps and social media), pump-and-dump coordination (fake community members manufacturing FOMO in Telegram groups), and rug pull hype (artificial community engagement). Investigators can identify sock puppets through: reverse image search on profile photos, EXIF analysis of shared images, account creation date relative to the fraud timeline, limited authentic engagement history, and OSINT Industries analysis linking the account email to other platform registrations.
Spear-Phishing
Targeted phishing attack against a specific individual, researched in advance for maximum credibility. Used by Lazarus Group (Contagious Interview) against crypto exchange developers. The initial access vector for multiple multi-hundred-million-dollar hacks.
Stablecoin Freeze / Blacklist
The ability of centralised stablecoin issuers — primarily Tether (USDT) and Circle (USDC) — to freeze or blacklist specific on-chain addresses, rendering those tokens permanently unmovable. Tether's blacklist function is implemented in the USDT smart contract; a frozen address cannot send its USDT balance. This is a powerful and near-instantaneous disruption tool: Tether has frozen addresses linked to ransomware, exchange hacks, and sanctioned entities in coordination with law enforcement requests. Unlike traditional asset freezes requiring court orders, stablecoin freezes can be executed within hours of a request. Investigators should contact Tether (USDT: compliance@tether.to) or Circle (USDC) directly with documented evidence when tracing illicit funds to USDT or USDC wallets — emergency freeze requests have been actioned within the same business day in documented cases including the Bybit hack aftermath.
STR (Suspicious Transaction Report)
The Canadian term — under PCMLTFA — for a report filed by a reporting entity (including crypto exchanges registered as Money Services Businesses) with FINTRAC when there are reasonable grounds to suspect a transaction is related to money laundering or terrorist financing. Functionally equivalent to a Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) in the US or a Suspicious Matter Report (SMR) in Australia. Unlike the Large Cash Transaction Report (LCTR, triggered automatically by transactions over $10,000 CAD), STRs are judgment-based and have no monetary threshold — a $100 transaction can warrant an STR if the surrounding circumstances indicate illicit intent. FINTRAC STR data is shared with law enforcement upon production of appropriate legal process and represents a significant source of investigative intelligence not visible through on-chain analysis alone.
Straw Identity
A person used to open an account or provide KYC credentials on behalf of the real criminal — inserting a false identity layer. Common in mule networks and exchange account fraud. The KYC holder at a VASP is often a straw identity; always investigate beyond the named account holder.
Structuring
Breaking large amounts into multiple sub-reporting-threshold transactions to avoid triggering mandatory AML reporting. A criminal offense in itself in most jurisdictions, independent of the underlying predicate crime. Detectable through transaction pattern analysis.
Supply Chain Attack
Compromising a target indirectly by attacking a trusted third-party supplier or tool. The Bybit $1.5B hack began with a supply chain compromise of Safe{Wallet} — a developer machine infection that allowed malicious JavaScript injection into the signing interface.
Timing Analysis
Cross-referencing exact blockchain transaction timestamps against external events — social media posts, exchange login logs, real-world activity — to correlate identities or confirm behavioral patterns. Used by ZachXBT in multiple landmark investigations.
Token Approval / ERC-20 Allowance
A permission granted by a token holder to a smart contract or address to spend up to a specified amount of their ERC-20 tokens on their behalf — without requiring the holder's signature for each individual spend. Implemented via the approve() function in the ERC-20 standard. Unlimited approvals (amount = max uint256) are exploited in approval phishing: victims are tricked into granting unlimited spend permission to a malicious contract, which then drains their entire balance at any future time. Investigators: use Etherscan's Token Approvals tab or Revoke.cash to audit outstanding approvals from any Ethereum address — this reveals whether a victim unwittingly granted drain permissions. A critical investigative step when a victim reports an unexplained wallet drain with no apparent private key compromise.
Tornado Cash
An Ethereum-based cryptocurrency mixing protocol sanctioned by OFAC in August 2022. Lazarus Group's preferred ETH mixing tool post-hack. Developer Roman Storm convicted in 2024. The sanctions established that smart contract addresses themselves can be designated as sanctioned entities.
TraderTraitor
A Lazarus Group sub-unit (also tracked as Jade Sleet by Microsoft and Slow Pisces by Palo Alto Unit 42) specialising in supply chain attacks and social engineering targeting cryptocurrency businesses and their developers. Responsible for the $1.5B Bybit hack (February 2025) and the $235M WazirX hack (July 2024). Primary technique: approaching developers via LinkedIn with attractive employment opportunities, then delivering trojanized coding test repositories or malicious software packages as part of a fake hiring process (see also: Contagious Interview). CISA and FBI formally attributed TraderTraitor activity in an April 2024 advisory. Distinguished from other Lazarus sub-groups by its focus on developer-targeting and software supply chain compromise rather than direct phishing or financial system attacks.
Transaction Hash (TXID)
A unique alphanumeric identifier for a specific blockchain transaction — the digital receipt number. Used to look up any transaction on a block explorer. The anchor for any on-chain investigation. Best practice: copy electronically to avoid transcription errors.
TTP (Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures)
The behavioral patterns and methods used by a threat actor — how they operate rather than what tools they use. TTPs are more stable and valuable for attribution than IOCs (which change frequently). In e-crime: Lazarus Group TTPs (LinkedIn fake job offers, supply chain compromise, specific mixing patterns) are documented in MITRE ATT&CK and enable rapid attribution of new incidents to known actors.
Travel Rule
A FATF regulatory requirement mandating that VASPs transmit originator and beneficiary information alongside cryptocurrency transactions above a threshold (generally $1,000 USD). The crypto equivalent of wire transfer regulations. Designed to prevent anonymous high-value crypto transfers. Compliance gaps in implementation remain a significant AML enforcement challenge globally.
Typology
A documented pattern, method, or scheme used to commit money laundering, terrorist financing, or a related financial crime — as identified and catalogued by regulators, financial intelligence units, or law enforcement. FATF publishes typology reports describing specific criminal methodologies (e.g., "use of cryptocurrency ATMs for placement of cash proceeds of drug trafficking"). Typologies serve as: reference frameworks for AML compliance teams building transaction monitoring rules, training materials for investigators, and analytical categories for FIU analysts assessing suspicious transaction reports. The E-Crimes Typologies modules in NEXUS (Modules 06–08) are structured around typologies — each drilldown represents a distinct documented typology with its own indicators, crypto nexus, and investigative response.
USDT (Tether)
The world's largest stablecoin by volume — a fiat-pegged token (1 USDT = 1 USD) operating primarily on Tron and Ethereum. USDT is the dominant vehicle for crypto money laundering, particularly pig butchering and ransomware OTC cashout operations. Tether Ltd. has the ability to freeze USDT addresses — a rapid disruption tool used in multiple major investigations.
UTXO (Unspent Transaction Output)
The fundamental accounting unit of Bitcoin and several other blockchains. Rather than maintaining account balances, Bitcoin tracks individual unspent outputs from previous transactions — each UTXO records an amount of bitcoin that has been received but not yet spent. When a user spends bitcoin, they consume one or more UTXOs as inputs and create new UTXOs as outputs (including a change output back to themselves). Investigators must understand the UTXO model because it is the foundation of Bitcoin clustering heuristics: the Common Input Ownership (CIUA) heuristic works because multiple UTXOs used as inputs in the same transaction must all be signed by their respective private key holders — if two addresses both sign inputs in one transaction, the same entity controls both. Change address detection also relies on UTXO mechanics. Without understanding UTXO, clustering results cannot be correctly interpreted.
VASP (Virtual Asset Service Provider)
Any platform used to buy, sell, trade, or exchange virtual currency. The FATF term covering all crypto exchanges and custodians. Most VASPs collect KYC data that investigators can obtain via legal process. The primary target for legal process in cryptocurrency investigations.
Virtual Asset (VA)
The FATF-defined term for any digital representation of value that can be digitally traded, transferred, or used for payment or investment purposes — excluding digital representations of fiat currencies, securities, and other financial assets already covered by existing regulatory frameworks. "Virtual Asset Service Provider" (VASP) derives from this definition. The distinction matters for regulatory practitioners: central bank digital currencies (CBDCs), tokenised securities, and stablecoins backed by regulated assets may fall outside the VA definition depending on jurisdiction-specific implementation of FATF standards. Investigators should verify the local regulatory classification of an asset before assuming VASP obligations apply to the platform handling it.
Wallet
Software or hardware tool for storing cryptocurrency private keys. Technically, the funds are on-chain — the wallet stores the key that controls them. Non-custodial wallets (MetaMask, Ledger) have no central operator; custodial wallets (exchange accounts) have VASPs who hold KYC data.
Whale Phishing
Highly targeted spear-phishing against extremely high-value individuals — exchange executives, DeFi protocol administrators, or crypto-wealthy individuals. A single successful whale phishing attack can yield tens or hundreds of millions of dollars.
Zero-Click Exploit
A vulnerability requiring no user interaction to compromise a device — the attacker sends a crafted message (iMessage, WhatsApp) that triggers code execution silently. Used by Pegasus and other commercial spyware to silently compromise high-value targets. In e-crime: zero-click exploits used against exchange executives or DeFi developers could enable keystroke/clipboard capture without any social engineering.

📌 Disclaimer — No Single Credential Is Sufficient
The certifications listed below are references and starting points — not requirements or endorsements. E-Crime Hunting & Disruption is an inherently multidisciplinary field. Effective practitioners typically combine credentials, lived operational experience, and continuous self-directed learning across several domains: financial crime, cybersecurity, legal process, blockchain technology, and open-source intelligence. A seasoned financial crime investigator with deep VASP knowledge and no formal cybersecurity certification may be more effective than a newly certified SANS analyst with no financial crime context — and vice versa. The goal is complementary depth, not credential accumulation.
