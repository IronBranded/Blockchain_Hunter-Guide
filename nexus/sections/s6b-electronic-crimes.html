Electronic Crimes

Cyber-enabled offenses that leverage malware, technical exploitation, and digital coercion to steal cryptocurrency, extract sensitive credentials, or extort victims. Many serve as the initial access vector for subsequent financial crime.

🔗 Connection to Economic Crimes
Electronic crimes are frequently the enabler of economic crimes. Infostealers harvest credentials used in BEC. Banking malware intercepts crypto transactions. Ransomware monetizes through forced crypto payment. Understanding the technical crime is essential to tracing the resulting financial flow.

Ransomware
Malware · Extortion · Crypto payment · Nation-state actors
CriticalNation-State
›
Definition
Crypto Nexus
Key Indicators
Investigation

Ransomware is malicious software that encrypts a victim's files or systems, rendering them inaccessible, and demands payment (typically in cryptocurrency) in exchange for the decryption key. Modern ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) operations involve multiple criminal parties: the RaaS developer, affiliates who deploy the malware, and brokers who launder proceeds.

Double extortion has become standard: beyond encryption, criminals exfiltrate data and threaten public release unless ransom is paid — creating two leverage points. Triple extortion adds DDoS attacks. Nation-state actors (Lazarus Group, Russian RaaS affiliates) use ransomware to fund operations or generate plausible deniability.

Payment Architecture
  • Unique Bitcoin address generated per victim
  • Monero (XMR) preferred for anonymity in some groups
  • Payment portal accessible via TOR hidden service
  • Automatic decryption key delivery upon payment confirmation
Laundering Flow
  • Victim pays → per-victim BTC address
  • Consolidation to group wallet
  • Mixing/chain-hopping cycle
  • OTC broker fiat conversion (documented in Khinkis case)
Why Crypto
Irreversible; censorship-resistant; global; automated payment verification; allows RaaS profit-sharing between developer (20-30%) and affiliates (70-80%) via smart splits
Major Groups
LockBit, ALPHV/BlackCat, Cl0p, REvil, Conti (disbanded), Royal, Lazarus Group (DPRK-state), WannaCry (DPRK). Most operate RaaS models with affiliate programs.
Technical IOCs
  • Encrypted files with unusual extensions (.locky, .ryuk, .crypt)
  • Ransom note dropped in directories
  • Shadow copies deleted (vssadmin delete shadows)
  • RDP brute force in logs preceding infection
  • Cobalt Strike beacon activity pre-encryption
Network IOCs
  • C2 communication to TOR exit nodes
  • Large data exfiltration prior to encryption
  • Lateral movement using PSExec or WMI
  • Kerberoasting or credential dumping activity
Financial IOCs
  • Bitcoin payment demanded within 72 hours
  • Ransom amount calibrated to victim size (negotiated)
  • Payment address with 15%+ exposure to other ransomware victims
Attribution Indicators
  • Ransom note language/format matches known group
  • TTPs match known group playbook (MITRE ATT&CK)
  • Bitcoin payment address cluster matches prior incidents
1
Preserve Payment Evidence
Document the Bitcoin/XMR address from the ransom note. Record the exact TXID of any payment made. This is the anchor for the on-chain investigation — trace from victim payment address forward.
2
Group Attribution
Compare ransom note format, payment address behavior, and TTPs against known ransomware group signatures. TRM Labs and Chainalysis maintain ransomware group address databases for rapid attribution.
3
Exposure Analysis
Run exposure analysis on the payment addresses — 15%+ exposure to other ransomware-linked wallets (as in the Khinkis case) significantly strengthens the case for legal process on downstream VASPs.
4
OTC Broker Identification
Ransomware groups typically cash out through OTC brokers. Identify the broker through on-chain clustering and bridge analysis (BTC→AVA→TRON is documented). Coordinate with VASP for freeze and KYC request simultaneously.

Infostealers, Keyloggers & Banking Malware
Credential theft · Crypto wallet drain · Account takeover
High VolumeInitial Access
›
Definition
Crypto Nexus
Key Indicators
Investigation

Infostealers are malware designed to silently harvest saved credentials, browser cookies, crypto wallet files, session tokens, and personal data. Major families: Redline, Vidar, Racoon, MetaStealer, Lumma. Sold on darknet forums on a subscription/MaaS (Malware-as-a-Service) model. Logs (bundles of stolen data) are resold on Telegram markets.

Keyloggers record every keystroke — capturing passwords, seed phrases, and private keys as typed. Often embedded in infostealer packages.

Banking malware (trojans like TrickBot, Emotet, Dridex) intercepts banking sessions to redirect transactions. Crypto variants specifically target exchange login sessions and hot wallet applications.

Crypto Wallet Targeting
  • Steals wallet.dat files (Bitcoin Core)
  • Extracts seed phrases from browser extension storage (MetaMask)
  • Captures keystrokes when entering seed phrase
  • Steals private keys from clipboard (clipboard hijacker)
Clipboard Hijacker Variant
Monitors clipboard for crypto wallet address format. When victim copies a wallet address to paste, malware replaces it with the attacker's address. Victim unknowingly sends funds to attacker.
Bybit Connection
Hudson Rock reported a developer machine at Safe{Wallet} was infected with an infostealer, providing initial access credentials used in the $1.5B Bybit hack. Corporate infostealer infections enable massive downstream theft.
MaaS Ecosystem
  • Infostealer developer sells subscriptions ($100-500/month)
  • Affiliates deploy malware, collect logs
  • Logs sold on Telegram markets
  • Buyers extract crypto credentials → drain wallets
Infection Vectors
  • Malicious software "cracks" / pirated software
  • Fake game mods or cheats
  • Malicious PDF/Office macros in phishing emails
  • YouTube video descriptions linking to malware
  • Fake VPN or productivity tool downloads
Compromise Indicators
  • Unexpected wallet drain with no user-initiated transaction
  • Exchange account login from unfamiliar IP/location
  • Sent crypto to unknown address (clipboard hijack)
  • Browser session cookies invalid (stolen + used elsewhere)
1
Infostealer Log Search
Search infostealer log databases (Cavalier, Hudson Rock, IntelX) for the victim's email or domain. If logs found, determine what credentials were exposed and when — this establishes the intrusion timeline.
2
Malware Artifact Analysis
If victim machine is available for forensic imaging: extract malware binary, identify C2 infrastructure, and attribute to a specific infostealer family. MITRE ATT&CK mapping enables threat intelligence correlation.
3
On-Chain Drain Tracing
Trace the drained wallet funds immediately. Infostealers typically drain all funds within minutes of credential extraction. Identify receiving wallet, cluster, and VASP deposit for emergency freeze.

Cryptojacking
Unauthorized mining · Resource theft · Cloud exploitation
Resource TheftCloud
›
Definition
Crypto Nexus
Key Indicators
Investigation

Cryptojacking is the unauthorized use of a victim's computing resources to mine cryptocurrency for the attacker's benefit. Unlike ransomware, the goal is not disruption but covert, long-term resource exploitation. Victims experience performance degradation and inflated cloud/energy costs without knowing why.

Cloud cryptojacking targets misconfigured cloud infrastructure (exposed Kubernetes APIs, Lambda functions, stolen cloud credentials) to run mining workloads at the victim's expense. Nation-state actors (Lazarus Group) have used cryptojacking to fund operations without directly stealing crypto.

Preferred Mining Targets
  • Monero (XMR) — CPU-mineable, privacy coin, most common
  • Ethereum Classic (ETC) — GPU-minable
  • Cloud credits converted to mining capacity
Attack Vectors
  • Malicious JavaScript in compromised websites (drive-by)
  • Malware installed via phishing
  • Exposed cloud APIs (AWS, GCP, Azure)
  • Vulnerable containers / Docker misconfiguration
  • Compromised CI/CD pipelines
Financial Impact
Victims bear compute and electricity costs while attacker accumulates XMR. Cloud victims may face tens of thousands in unexpected cloud bills. Mining pool records enable investigation.
Investigation Advantage
Mining pool payouts go to attacker's XMR wallet address. Pool operator may have subscriber information or IP records for the account — a subpoenable chokepoint even for a privacy coin.
System Indicators
  • CPU/GPU sustained at 70-100% with no user workload
  • System overheating / fan noise
  • Browser tab consuming unusual CPU
  • Elevated cloud compute bills
  • Process names mimicking system processes (svchost32.exe, lsasss.exe)
Network Indicators
  • Outbound connections to mining pools (stratum protocol, port 3333/4444)
  • DNS queries to known mining pool domains
  • Large outbound data to unfamiliar IPs
  • Cryptocurrency mining pool traffic signatures
1
Identify Mining Wallet Address
Extract the miner's wallet address from the mining process or configuration files on the compromised system. This is the attacker's XMR (or other coin) wallet — the anchor for financial investigation.
2
Mining Pool Legal Process
Identify the mining pool from network logs (domain/IP of stratum connection). Issue legal process to the pool operator for account records, payout history, and IP addresses associated with the miner wallet.
3
Infrastructure Attribution
Attribute the attack infrastructure — C2 server, dropper hosting, and delivery mechanism. Cross-reference with known threat actor TTPs for group attribution.

Blackmail, Sextortion & Digital Extortion
Coercion · Intimate image abuse · Data extortion
High HarmVictim Sensitive
›
Definition
Crypto Nexus
Key Indicators
Investigation

Sextortion involves threats to distribute intimate images or recordings unless a crypto payment is made. Variations: (a) spear-sextortion via mass email claiming to have compromising webcam footage (credential stuffing to add "proof"), (b) actual intimate image theft via compromised accounts, (c) AI-generated deepfake intimate imagery as leverage.

Data extortion (distinct from ransomware) involves stealing sensitive data from organizations and threatening public release or sale to competitors. The cyber gang "Scattered Spider" used this model against MGM and Caesars in 2023, demanding crypto ransoms without encrypting data.

Payment Methods
  • Bitcoin — most common (easy for victims to acquire)
  • Monero — privacy-focused groups prefer XMR
  • Gift card codes in less sophisticated operations
  • Unique address per victim for tracking payment
Operational Scale
Mass sextortion campaigns use automated scripts sending millions of emails. Even 0.1% response rate is highly profitable. All payments trace to clusters of addresses controlled by same criminal infrastructure.
Organized Crime Groups
Nigerian fraud groups (Yahoo Boys) run large-scale sextortion operations. Scattered Spider (English-speaking teens) operated sophisticated corporate data extortion. DPRK has used corporate blackmail as a state revenue mechanism.
Mass Sextortion Email Red Flags
  • Claims to have compromising webcam recording
    • "Proof": recipient's own old password (from breach database)
  • Urgent deadline (48-72 hours)
  • Bitcoin wallet provided for payment
  • Threatens contacts if not paid
Targeted Sextortion Red Flags
  • Contact has intimate images or screen recordings
  • Met online recently, moved quickly to intimate communication
  • Requests payment to specific wallet
  • Escalating threats with proof of content held
1
Wallet Clustering Across Victims
Mass sextortion operations use unique addresses per victim but consolidate to the same downstream wallets. Aggregate multiple victim addresses to map the full criminal infrastructure and identify VASP deposits.
2
Email Header Analysis
Extract originating IP from email headers (if not proxied). Cross-reference with OSINT — mass campaigns often use bulletproof hosting providers with investigable registration data.
3
Breach Database Correlation
If the email includes a "proof" password, identify which breach that password originated from (using IntelX or Snusbase). The breach source and timing narrows the attacker's data acquisition methods.
🔴 Victim Priority
Sextortion victims are at high risk of serious psychological harm. Prioritize victim safety and support resources before launching investigation steps. Do not underestimate the psychological impact.

Identity Theft & Account Takeover (ATO)
Credential reuse · SIM swap · Exchange account takeover
ATOExchange Focus
›
Definition
Crypto Nexus
Key Indicators
Investigation

Identity theft involves obtaining another person's identifying information (credentials, government ID, SIN/SSN) to assume their identity for financial fraud. In crypto contexts, identity theft primarily enables: (a) exchange account takeover (ATO) — using stolen credentials to access and drain a victim's exchange account; (b) fraudulent VASP account opening — using stolen identities to pass KYC checks; (c) SIM swapping — taking over a victim's phone number to bypass SMS 2FA on exchange accounts.

SIM Swap Attack Chain
  • Attacker gathers victim PII from OSINT/breach data
  • Social engineers mobile carrier to transfer victim's SIM
  • Receives victim's SMS messages (including 2FA codes)
  • Accesses exchange account → drains crypto
Coinbase Impersonation (2025)
A May 2025 insider data breach at Coinbase exposed customer PII. Criminal groups used this data to call customers impersonating "Elite Support," directing them to transfer funds to attacker wallets. ZachXBT exposed "Haby" — who stole $2M+ this way.
Credential Stuffing
Using username/password combinations from breached databases to automate login attempts against crypto exchanges. Victims who reuse passwords across services are vulnerable. Successful logins → automated withdrawal.
Fraudulent KYC Accounts
Stolen government IDs + deepfake video submitted to pass VASP KYC. The resulting account is attributed to the identity theft victim — a critical investigative trap. Always investigate the KYC holder further.
Account Takeover Indicators
  • Login from new IP/device not recognized by victim
  • SMS 2FA no longer working (SIM swapped)
  • Withdrawal to unknown external wallet
  • Password reset not initiated by victim
  • Exchange security alerts victim didn't trigger
SIM Swap Indicators
  • Phone loses signal / shows "SOS only"
  • SMS messages no longer arriving
  • Carrier account shows unauthorized port-out
  • Simultaneous exchange login from unknown location
1
Exchange Account Forensics
Request exchange access logs showing all login IPs, devices, and timestamps around the ATO event. Correlate with Spur.us to identify if VPN/TOR was used. The ATO IP is often the same attacker infrastructure used in other cases.
2
Carrier Investigation
For SIM swaps: obtain mobile carrier records showing who requested the SIM transfer, what credentials they provided, and the account used. Insider-facilitated SIM swaps require carrier employee investigation.
3
On-Chain Withdrawal Tracing
Trace stolen funds from the unauthorized withdrawal immediately. Cross-reference destination address with known ATO clusters — the same criminal network often uses consistent receiving infrastructure across multiple victims.
