Economic Crimes

Financially motivated crimes that exploit cryptocurrency's pseudonymity, global reach, and irreversibility. Each category includes definition, crypto-crime nexus, key indicators, and investigation pointers.

The Fraud Diamond

Fraud Diamond — Four Elements Required for Fraud to Occur
Pressure / Incentive
The motivation driving the fraudster — financial desperation, debt, lifestyle pressure, or greed. In crypto fraud: the promise of "easy money" or the need to cover losses from previous schemes.
Opportunity
The circumstances enabling fraud — weak controls, anonymous systems, or exploitable trust. Crypto's pseudonymity, global reach, and irreversible transactions create abundant opportunity for fraudsters.
Rationalization
How the fraudster justifies the act to themselves — "victims are greedy," "it's a victimless crime," or "the system is rigged." Crypto fraudsters often rationalize by blaming victim FOMO or greed.
Capability
The fourth element (added to the original Fraud Triangle) — the skill, knowledge, and access required to execute the fraud. In crypto: technical knowledge of wallets, smart contracts, or social engineering skills.

Romance Scams / Pig Butchering (Sha Zhu Pan)
Advance-fee fraud · Investment fraud · Social engineering
High Volume Organized Crime
›
Definition
Crypto Nexus
Key Indicators
Investigation

Romance scams / Pig Butchering (Sha Zhu Pan — "killing the pig") are long-con social engineering frauds originating predominantly from Southeast Asian cyber-scam compounds. Criminals build a romantic or investment friendship online over weeks or months, cultivate deep trust, then convince victims to "invest" in a fraudulent crypto trading platform they control.

When the victim attempts to withdraw profits, they are told to pay additional "taxes," "fees," or "release charges." The platform is entirely fake — victim funds are immediately transferred to criminal wallets. Organized crime groups force trafficking victims to staff these operations at scale.

Why Crypto
Irreversible transactions; no chargebacks; global reach; fake platforms mimic legitimate exchanges; USDT on Tron allows near-instant cashout
Preferred Chains
TRON (USDT), Ethereum, BNB Chain; victim funds ultimately swept to TRON for cashout through OTC brokers in Southeast Asia
Typical Flow
  • Victim sends to "investment platform" wallet
  • Funds immediately swept to criminal aggregator
  • Aggregated to TRON USDT
  • OTC broker converts to fiat
Scale
Most financially devastating fraud category — Chainalysis estimates billions lost annually; individual victims frequently lose life savings
Platform Red Flags
  • Domain registered within past 12 months
  • No regulation or license information
  • Fake trading dashboards showing inflated profits
  • Withdrawal requires additional payment
  • Customer support unreachable
Social Red Flags
  • Met via social media / dating app by unsolicited contact
  • Contact escalates intimacy rapidly
  • "Accidentally" contacted wrong number
  • Claims expert crypto trading knowledge
  • Pressure to invest more to unlock withdrawals
On-Chain Indicators
  • Multiple victims sending to same deposit address
  • Funds swept immediately upon receipt
  • Large clusters of similar-sized inflows
  • Conversion to USDT/TRON at aggregator
Victim Profile
Targets: recently divorced/widowed, lonely, financially comfortable, 40-70 years old. Victims often in denial; do not self-report promptly
1
Triage Victim Report
Collect: wallet address victim sent to, exact TXID, screenshot of platform, all communications. Determine if scammer wallet is still active and receiving new victims.
2
Cluster Analysis
Plot scammer receiving address. Look for multiple victim wallets sending to same address — "multiple pigs, same sty." Map downstream flow to aggregator wallets and VASP deposits.
3
Platform OSINT
WHOIS the scam domain. Reverse-search hosting infrastructure. Look for other scam domains on same IP. Archive the fake platform immediately.
4
VASP Legal Process
Identify exchange deposits and issue emergency freeze requests simultaneously with KYC subpoenas. Time-critical — funds move to TRON cashout rapidly.
⚠️ Victim Sensitivity
Pig butchering victims are often deeply traumatized and in denial. Handle with crisis support awareness. Victims may have sent funds multiple times believing they were recovering previous losses.

Corporate fraud · Wire redirect · Invoice fraud
High ValueEnterprise Target
›
Definition
Crypto Nexus
Key Indicators
Investigation

Business Email Compromise (BEC) is a sophisticated fraud targeting organizations that conduct wire transfers or make business payments. Attackers compromise or spoof legitimate business email accounts — typically executives (CEO fraud), vendors, or legal counsel — and impersonate them to redirect payments to criminal-controlled accounts.

BEC has evolved to increasingly request cryptocurrency payments directly, or use compromised accounts to change payment instructions from bank wire to crypto wallet. The FBI's IC3 reports BEC as consistently among the highest-loss cybercrime categories globally.

Crypto Redirect Method
Criminal intercepts legitimate vendor invoice, replaces bank account with crypto wallet address. Victim sends payment to criminal wallet believing it is vendor.
Why Crypto
Irreversibility; speed (minutes vs. days for wire recalls); no central authority to contact for reversal; easier international transfer than SWIFT
Initial Access Methods
  • Phishing compromises executive email
  • Look-alike / typosquat domain spoofing
  • Infostealer malware credential theft
  • VoIP spoofing to accompany fraudulent email
Typical Victims
Accounts payable staff, CFOs, law firm trust accounts, real estate transaction accounts, and any high-value payment workflow without multi-factor verification
Email Red Flags
  • Urgent payment request from executive
  • Slight domain variation (micros0ft.com vs microsoft.com)
  • Request to keep confidential
  • Reply-to address differs from sender
  • Unusual payment method change
Process Red Flags
  • Last-minute payment instructions change
  • Vendor requests crypto instead of wire
  • Phone confirmation to unfamiliar number
  • High urgency / deadline pressure
1
Preserve Email Headers
Extract full email headers — X-Originating-IP, Received headers, and SPF/DKIM results. These establish infrastructure attribution and often reveal the originating VPN or country.
2
Trace Recipient Wallet Immediately
Time-critical — BEC funds typically move within hours. Input recipient crypto address into TRM Labs or Arkham. Identify immediate VASP deposits and request emergency freeze.
3
Digital Forensics on Compromise
Determine how email account was compromised — phishing artifact analysis, infostealer log search (Cavalier), or email provider access logs. Timeline the intrusion.

Phishing / Spear-Phishing / Whale Phishing
Credential theft · Wallet drain · Exchange account takeover
Credential TheftAll Scales
›
Definition
Crypto Nexus
Key Indicators
Investigation

Phishing: Mass deceptive communications (email, SMS, social media) impersonating trusted entities to steal credentials, seed phrases, or private keys.

Spear-phishing: Highly targeted phishing against a specific individual or organization, researched in advance to maximize credibility. Used extensively by Lazarus Group against crypto exchange developers — the initial access vector for the Bybit $1.5B hack.

Whale phishing: Spear-phishing targeting high-net-worth individuals, exchange executives, or DeFi protocol administrators — extremely high-value targets where a single successful attack yields millions.

Crypto-Specific Variants
  • Fake exchange login pages harvesting credentials
  • Fake hardware wallet firmware updates stealing seed phrases
  • Fake "connect wallet" dApp front-ends draining approvals
  • Fake job offers with malware payloads (Lazarus "Contagious Interview")
Token Approval Drains
Victim is tricked into signing a malicious transaction granting unlimited token spend approval to attacker's contract. All tokens are immediately drained without further victim action.
Why High Value
A single exchange admin credential or developer private key can yield hundreds of millions. Lazarus Group's $1.5B Bybit hack began with a targeted phishing attack on a Safe{Wallet} developer machine.
Post-Compromise Flow
  • Credential → Exchange account takeover → Withdraw funds
  • Private key → Direct wallet drain
  • Developer compromise → Smart contract manipulation
URL Red Flags
  • Slight domain misspelling (bïnance.com, coinbáse.com)
  • Subdomain tricks (coinbase.com.login.evil.io)
  • Newly registered domains
  • HTTP instead of HTTPS
Email Red Flags
  • Urgency ("Your account will be closed in 24 hours")
  • Generic greetings
  • Mismatched sender domain
  • Request to enter seed phrase or private key
On-Chain Indicators
  • Unlimited token approval to unknown contract
  • Sudden mass withdrawal from single wallet
  • Funds immediately moved to mixer or instant exchange
1
Phishing Infrastructure Analysis
Archive phishing page immediately. Extract WHOIS, hosting IP, SSL certificate. Identify other domains on same infrastructure — often reveals multiple active phishing campaigns.
2
Credential / Key Exposure Scope
Determine what was captured — credentials only, or private keys/seed phrases? Private key compromise = total wallet loss. Credential compromise = exchange account takeover requiring VASP cooperation.
3
On-Chain Drain Tracing
Trace stolen funds immediately. Token approval drains move funds within seconds of approval. Check for malicious contract address — the contract deployer wallet is investigable.

Deepfake Fraud & AI-Enabled Social Engineering

Emerging 2025AI-Enabled

Definition
Crypto Nexus
Key Indicators
Investigation

Deepfake fraud uses AI-generated synthetic media — video, audio, or both — to impersonate real individuals for financial deception. Voice cloning enables real-time phone impersonation of executives. Video deepfakes are used in fake investment endorsements, fraudulent "live" video calls to bypass identity verification, and romance scam personas.

In 2025, deepfake technology became accessible enough that criminal groups routinely use it to bypass KYC/liveness checks at exchanges, create fraudulent influencer investment endorsements, and conduct real-time video fraud against corporate financial teams.

Exchange KYC Bypass
AI-generated "liveness" videos submitted during exchange KYC to open accounts using stolen identities. Enables anonymous VASP access with someone else's identity.
Fake Investment Scams
Deepfake videos of Elon Musk, Warren Buffett, or prominent crypto figures endorsing fraudulent investment platforms. Deployed via social media advertising at scale.
Corporate CFO Fraud
Real-time deepfake video call to corporate finance team impersonating CEO, directing emergency crypto transfer. Multiple cases of $25M+ losses reported in 2025.
Romance Scam Personas
AI-generated photos and video for fake romantic personas — eliminating the need for real human involvement in long-con pig butchering operations.
Video Red Flags
  • Blurry edges around face/hairline
  • Inconsistent lighting or shadows
  • Unnatural blinking or eye movement
  • Audio/lip sync mismatch
  • Background artifacts when person moves
Context Red Flags
  • Unusually urgent financial request via video call
  • Refusal to switch video platforms for verification
  • Investment guaranteed returns promised by celebrity
  • KYC submitted by known deceased person
1
Deepfake Verification Tools
Use Microsoft Video Authenticator, Sensity AI, or Deepware Scanner to analyze suspicious videos for AI-generation artifacts. Cross-reference with known authentic footage of the impersonated individual.
2
Identity Theft Investigation
Identify whose identity was used for KYC bypass. That person is a victim of identity theft. Coordinate with VASP to flag the account and obtain the deepfake submission as evidence.
3
Infrastructure Attribution
Trace the AI generation infrastructure — some deepfake services leave metadata artifacts. Investigate the hosting of fake celebrity investment ads for WHOIS/domain intelligence.

Investment Fraud — Pump & Dump / Rug Pulls / Ponzi Schemes
Market manipulation · Token fraud · Pyramid schemes
Market Manip.DeFi
›
Definition
Crypto Nexus
Key Indicators
Investigation

Pump & Dump: Coordinated artificial inflation of a token's price through coordinated buying, followed by mass selling at peak — leaving retail investors holding worthless tokens. Coordinated via private Telegram/Discord groups.

Rug Pull: Developers launch a token, build hype, attract investor liquidity, then drain the liquidity pool and abandon the project. Hard rug = immediate drain. Soft rug = gradual abandonment.

Ponzi / Pyramid: Returns paid to earlier investors from capital of newer investors — no legitimate underlying business. Crypto variants often promise automated trading bots or "yield farming" strategies with guaranteed returns.

Rug Pull Mechanics
  • Token deployed on DEX (Uniswap, PancakeSwap)
  • Liquidity provided — investors buy in
  • Liquidity removed by deployer ("rug")
  • Token price collapses to near zero instantly
Smart Contract Exploits Used
  • Mint function retained by deployer — infinite token creation
  • Honeypot contracts — buy enabled, sell disabled
  • Hidden back-doors in ownership functions
  • Unlock mechanisms on locked liquidity
2025 Memecoin Context
Political memecoins ($TRUMP, $MELANIA) created pump-and-dump template replicated thousands of times on Solana. Insider wallets identified on-chain buying before public launch.
Preferred Platforms
Solana (Pump.fun), BNB Chain (PancakeSwap), Ethereum (Uniswap) — low listing requirements enable rapid deployment and exit
Pre-Launch Red Flags
  • Anonymous team with no verifiable identities
  • No smart contract audit
  • Liquidity lock expiring imminently
  • Team wallet holds excessive % of supply
  • Copied/plagiarized whitepaper
On-Chain Red Flags
  • Deployer wallet has history of prior rugs
  • Large wallet cluster buying before public launch
  • Mint function not renounced
  • Concentrated holder distribution (top 10 own 70%+)
1
Deployer Wallet Analysis
Trace the token's deployer wallet backward — prior projects, funding sources, and connections. A deployer wallet linked to previous rug pulls is strong evidence of criminal intent.
2
Smart Contract Review
Inspect the token contract for retained mint functions, hidden ownership controls, and sell restrictions (honeypot). Tools: Token Sniffer, HoneyPot.is, Go+ Security.
3
Coordinated Wallet Cluster
Identify the cluster of wallets that bought before public launch (insider wallets) and sold at peak. These wallets are the suspects. Trace to VASP deposits for KYC process.

Money Laundering — Core Stages

Money Laundering — Three Core Stages (FATF / PCMLTFA Framework)
STAGE 01
Placement
Introducing illicit funds into the financial system. The riskiest stage — proceeds are most closely linked to the underlying crime and most detectable.
Crypto Methods:
Cash→Bitcoin ATM · Peer-to-peer purchase · Casino chips→crypto · Structuring
STAGE 02
Layering
Creating distance between funds and their criminal origin through complex layers of transactions designed to obscure the audit trail.
Crypto Methods:
Chain-hopping · Mixers · DEX swaps · NFT wash trading · Instant exchanges
STAGE 03
Integration
Re-entering funds into the legitimate economy so they appear to have a lawful origin. The final stage — funds are now "clean" and indistinguishable from legitimate wealth.
Crypto Methods:
OTC broker → fiat · Real estate · Luxury goods · Business investment
🔍 Investigator Focus
Blockchain forensics is most powerful at detecting Layering — every on-chain move leaves a traceable record. Placement and Integration require collaboration with financial institutions and law enforcement for AML reporting data (SARs) and asset seizure authority.

PCMLTFAAML

Definition
Crypto Methods

Investigation

Money laundering is the process of making illegally obtained proceeds appear legitimate. The FATF / PCMLTFA three-stage model (Placement → Layering → Integration) describes the lifecycle. Cryptocurrency has become integral to all three stages, particularly layering, where the blockchain's pseudonymity and cross-border accessibility provide significant advantages to launderers.

PCMLTF — Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing — is the Canadian statutory framework; equivalent to AML/CFT internationally. Investigators must understand both the financial crime and the technical blockchain mechanics to trace the full laundering chain.

Placement Methods
  • Bitcoin ATM cash-to-crypto (often structuring below reporting thresholds)
  • P2P platform purchases with cash or gift cards
  • Online casino chip-to-crypto cycles
  • NFT self-purchase to move value (wash trading)
  • Crypto-collateralized loans against illicit holdings
Layering Methods
  • Chain-hopping (BTC→ETH→XMR→TRON)
  • Mixer/tumbler use (Tornado Cash, Wasabi)
  • DEX swaps (no KYC, no operator to subpoena)
  • Peeling chains (sequential address hops)
  • Instant exchange services (ChangeNow, FixedFloat)
  • NFT wash trading between self-controlled wallets
Integration Methods
  • OTC broker fiat conversion (Khinkis case)
  • Real estate purchase with crypto-origin funds
  • Luxury goods (watches, vehicles, art) purchased for resale
  • Shell company investment from "clean" crypto
  • Crypto payroll to criminal network members
Anonymizing Tools
  • VPN / TOR for IP obfuscation
  • Non-custodial wallets (no KYC)
  • Privacy coins (XMR, ZEC) at conversion points
  • No-KYC instant exchanges
  • Self-hosted nodes to avoid centralized services

Money mules are individuals who receive and transfer illicit proceeds — knowingly or unwittingly — on behalf of criminal networks. In crypto, mule networks provide an additional identity layer between the criminal and the VASP's KYC records.

Crypto Mule Roles
  • Account mules: open exchange accounts under their real identity for criminals
  • Transfer mules: receive and forward crypto through their wallets
  • Cash-out mules: convert crypto to fiat via ATM or P2P, keep a commission
  • Identity mules: provide KYC documents for criminal-controlled exchange accounts
Recruitment Methods
  • Fake job postings ("crypto payment processor")
  • Romance scam victims recruited as unknowing mules
  • Social media ads for "easy income"
  • Exploitation of financially desperate individuals
Red Flags for Mule Accounts
  • Account opened recently with minimal history
  • Large deposits immediately withdrawn or transferred
  • Multiple transactions just below reporting thresholds (structuring)
  • IP address inconsistent with stated address
  • Multiple accounts opened with same device fingerprint
Investigation Note
The KYC holder on a mule account is often the mule, not the criminal. Always investigate beyond the named account holder. Mules may be victims themselves and require sensitive handling.
1
Trace the Full ML Chain
Document each stage: Placement origin → Layering hops → Integration endpoint. Map the complete graph before serving legal process to ensure you capture all leverage points simultaneously.
2
Quantify Exposure
Use TRM Labs or Chainalysis to calculate direct and indirect exposure percentages. High exposure (15%+) to ransomware or sanctioned entities is a key indicator used in legal process justification.
3
SAR Correlation
Request SAR (Suspicious Activity Report) data from FINTRAC/FinCEN if available. Mule account activity often triggers automated SAR filing at VASPs — these reports provide additional investigative leads.
4
OTC Broker Identification
OTC brokers are often the final cashout point. Identify them through VASP records, on-chain clustering, or commercial intelligence (TRM's VASP database). Some jurisdictions permit civil action against OTC brokers who knowingly facilitate laundering.
